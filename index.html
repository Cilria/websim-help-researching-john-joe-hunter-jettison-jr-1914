Islamist insurgents seize Termez, declare Islamic Emirate of Uzbekistan

By CNN Staff
July 2, 2028 • Updated 2:34 PM EDT
Developing Story: This story is breaking and will be updated as more information becomes available.
[Image: Army of Islam fighters in Termez after capturing the city]

Termez, Uzbekistan (CNN) — Islamist insurgents from the Army of Islam have captured the strategically vital city of Termez in southern Uzbekistan, marking their most significant victory yet in the four-month-old conflict that has now claimed tens of thousands of lives and threatens to destabilize the entire Central Asian region.

The fall of Termez, a city of 180,000 residents near the Afghan border, comes just weeks after the insurgents broke out of their mountainous strongholds and began a rapid advance across the country. The Army of Islam announced the city's capture on June 22, following a brutal siege that left residents without water, electricity, or adequate food supplies.

Key developments:

  • Army of Islam captures Termez after month-long siege
  • Insurgents seize massive weapons caches, including advanced military equipment
  • Qarshi falls without resistance as government forces retreat
  • Navoi captured after prolonged siege, insurgents push toward Bukhara
  • Samarkand and Jizzakh now encircled, supply lines cut

In a dramatic escalation of their campaign, the Army of Islam has formally declared the establishment of the Islamic Emirate of Uzbekistan, with their leader proclaiming himself "Emir" and "Amir al-Mu'minin" (Commander of the Faithful), titles that carry significant religious and political weight in the Islamic world.

"By the Grace of Allah, the final victory against the infidel government is approaching," the group's media channel announced in a recent statement. "The Islamic Emirate of Uzbekistan has been established, and the banners of Islam now fly over the lands of our forefathers."

Rapid territorial gains

Following the capture of Termez, Army of Islam forces have pushed northward with remarkable speed, seizing the city of Qarshi without encountering significant resistance. Government forces, already demoralized and undersupplied, reportedly abandoned their positions and retreated north toward Bukhara.

The insurgents have also consolidated their grip on the Fergana Valley, capturing nearly all of the region except for major cities that remain under siege. The strategic M39 highway, which connects the eastern provinces to the capital Tashkent, has been completely severed, leaving government forces in the capital increasingly isolated and dependent on air resupply.

"The Uzbek army is on the brink of collapse. The few combat-effective units that remain have deserted to Karakalpakstan, where they've formed local self-defense units known as 'Karakalpakstan Defense Units.'"

Perhaps most significantly, the Army of Islam has captured Navoi after a grueling month-long siege, opening the path toward the historic city of Bukhara. Insurgent forces have now encircled both Samarkand and Jizzakh, cutting off all major supply routes and leaving these culturally significant cities vulnerable to the same fate that befell Termez.

Humanitarian crisis deepens

The rapid advance of Army of Islam forces has created a humanitarian catastrophe across large swaths of Uzbekistan. In Termez, residents described harrowing conditions during the siege, with food supplies dwindling and medical services collapsing.

"We had no water for days, and the electricity was cut off weeks ago," said one resident who fled the city and spoke to CNN on condition of anonymity. "When the insurgents finally entered, they seemed more organized than our own government forces had been in months."

The United Nations has expressed grave concern about the deteriorating situation, with humanitarian organizations struggling to access affected areas. The World Food Program estimates that over 2 million people are now at risk of severe food insecurity across the country.

International response

The dramatic gains by the Army of Islam have prompted urgent international discussions about potential intervention. Russia has increased airstrikes against insurgent positions, though these appear to have done little to slow the group's advance.

China, which shares concerns about instability spreading to its western Xinjiang region, has reportedly begun moving additional military assets to its border with Central Asia. Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan have both closed border crossings and deployed additional troops to prevent the conflict from spreading.

The United States and European Union have called for an immediate cessation of hostilities and a return to negotiations, though analysts say the Army of Islam's recent successes make diplomatic solutions increasingly unlikely.

International implications:

  • Russia increases airstrikes but fails to halt insurgent advance
  • China deploys additional forces to Central Asian border
  • Neighboring states close borders and boost security
  • UN warns of regional humanitarian catastrophe

What comes next

With the Army of Islam now controlling an estimated 40% of Uzbek territory and the government army in disarray, regional analysts warn that the conflict could spread beyond Uzbekistan's borders. The group's declaration of an Islamic Emirate represents a direct challenge not only to the Uzbek government but to the broader international order in Central Asia.

"This is no longer just an insurgency," said Dr. Sarah Mitchell, a Central Asia specialist at Georgetown University. "The Army of Islam is attempting to establish a new state, and their recent military successes suggest they may actually succeed unless there's significant international intervention."

The coming weeks will likely prove crucial in determining whether the Army of Islam can consolidate its gains and establish lasting control over captured territories, or whether the international community will intervene to prevent the complete collapse of the Uzbek state.

For now, however, the insurgents appear to hold the momentum, with their black banners flying over an ever-expanding swath of Central Asia's most populous nation.
