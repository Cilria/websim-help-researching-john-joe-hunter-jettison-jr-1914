"Day of Blood": Tajikistan declares martial law as protesters seize cities

Martial law fails to quell uprising as demonstrators capture military barracks and execute officials
By Al Jazeera correspondents in Central Asia
September 6, 2027, 23:00 GMT

Dushanbe, Tajikistan — Tajikistan's government has declared martial law across northern regions and deployed over 2,000 security forces in a desperate attempt to crush the growing rebellion, but the move has only intensified what protesters are calling the "Day of Blood" — the deadliest day of violence since the uprising began.

CONFIRMED CASUALTIES: Over 3,700 protesters killed nationwide, including 700+ in Khujand alone. Opposition sources claim the true death toll exceeds 5,000

Khujand: The breaking point

In Khujand, the northern capital of resistance, nearly 100,000 protesters faced off against a massive government force of over 8,000 security personnel, including regime loyalists, foreign mercenaries from Iran and Turkmenistan, and heavily armed special units.

At approximately 3:00 PM local time, government forces opened fire with DShK heavy machine guns and sniper rifles into the dense crowd. The result was a massacre of staggering proportions — over 700 protesters killed in just a few hours, with bodies littering the main square and surrounding streets.

"It was a fucking warzone. They just kept shooting, reloading, shooting again. People were crawling over bodies to escape. The sound of machine guns never stopped."
— Khujand protester, speaking to Al Jazeera by phone

But rather than dispersing, the massacre transformed the crowd into an armed insurgency. At 5:30 PM, protesters launched a coordinated assault on the military barracks using Molotov cocktails, rocks, and later, grenades. A truck loaded with gasoline was driven directly into the barracks wall, creating a breach that allowed hundreds to storm inside.

Heavy street-to-street fighting erupted as protesters armed with shotguns and AK-47s engaged security forces. By 7:00 PM, government forces began retreating as they came under heavy fire and ambush. Crucially, large segments of the security forces defected, helping protesters capture the barracks and seize massive quantities of weapons and ammunition.

The revenge begins

With government forces fleeing, protesters launched what they termed "Operation Vengeance." The Arbob Palace, seat of regional government, was set ablaze and stormed. The Mayor of Khujand, identified as a key regime ally who allegedly ordered the water cutoff that preceded the violence, was captured and executed in the main square.

Video footage verified by Al Jazeera shows the mayor's body being dragged through the streets as crowds cheered and chanted anti-government slogans. The building housing the local branch of the ruling party was also set on fire.

Istravshan: The pattern repeats

In Istravshan, where 60,000 protesters chanted "Марг ба режим" ("Death to the regime") and "Марг ба Раҳмон" ("Death to Rahmon"), government forces again deployed DShK machine guns and snipers. The response was immediate and brutal.

A compound housing 60 Enforcement Unit members was surrounded and set on fire. An army barracks was stormed and weapons seized. Pictures of President Rahmon were burned across the city, while statues of Stalin and Lenin — symbols of the old regime — were destroyed by angry crowds.

Dushanbe: The capital convulses

Even in Dushanbe, the heavily fortified capital, government control began to fracture. Security forces opened fire with live ammunition and machine guns on a crowd of 150,000 protesters, killing an estimated 2,000 people in what witnesses describe as systematic slaughter.

Despite the bloodshed, protesters continued advancing on government buildings, setting multiple structures ablaze. The presidential palace complex came under siege as columns of demonstrators breached the outer security perimeter.

Isfara: Martial law fails

In the strategic town of Isfara, authorities imposed martial law in a desperate attempt to prevent protests. The measure failed catastrophically as nearly the entire population took to the streets in defiance.

Government forces responded with machine gun fire and, according to opposition sources, chemical weapons. Videos shared with Al Jazeera show people collapsing and convulsing, though these claims could not be independently verified due to internet restrictions.

The Free Tajik Motherland declares war

The Telegram channel "Free Tajik Motherland," which has coordinated protests across the country, issued a dramatic statement: "No more peaceful protests, from now on. Fight the criminal Rahmon regime with the only word they understand. Blood."

The channel called for armed resistance and provided instructions for making improvised explosive devices. Within hours, multiple government buildings across northern Tajikistan came under attack.

Military response and regional implications

The Tajik Ministry of Defense issued a stark warning, declaring it would crush what it termed "armed terrorists" and announcing the beginning of "heavy military campaigns."

However, military analysts suggest the government faces a critical challenge. With significant portions of the security forces defecting and protesters now armed with military-grade weapons, a prolonged conflict appears likely.

The regional domino effect

The violence has already sparked calls for similar uprisings across Central Asia. The Free Tajik Motherland channel announced protests in Turkmenistan for September 11-12, while Kazakh opposition groups have called for demonstrations on September 19.

"The Eurasian Spring has begun," declared one opposition leader. "From Tashkent to Dushanbe, the criminal regimes will fall. The people have nothing left to lose but their chains."

International response

The international community has responded with growing alarm. The UN Security Council held an emergency session, while neighboring countries have closed borders and deployed additional military forces.

China and Russia, both with significant interests in the region, have called for "stability and dialogue," though neither has indicated willingness to intervene directly.

The end of Tajikistan as we know it

As night fell on September 6th, the situation remained fluid and dangerous. Protesters controlled significant portions of Khujand, Istravshan, and parts of Dushanbe. Government forces have retreated to fortified positions, but their ability to retake lost territory remains uncertain.

What began as anti-government protests has evolved into something far more dangerous — an armed insurrection that threatens to not only topple the Rahmon regime but potentially destabilize the entire Central Asian region.

As one protester told Al Jazeera: "They call us terrorists, but we are freedom fighters. Today we take back our country, tomorrow we help our brothers in Turkmenistan and Kazakhstan. The criminal regimes will fall, one by one."
