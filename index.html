Venezuela Masses 70,000 Troops Near Guyana Border, Threatening Rapid Invasion of Essequibo Region

By Juan Forero and Kejal Vyas
Updated Dec. 27, 2023 9:30 PM ET
Venezuelan military vehicles move toward the Guyana border on Wednesday, as tensions escalate over the disputed Essequibo region.

CARACAS—Venezuela has assembled approximately 70,000 troops near its eastern border with Guyana, positioning the military for what U.S. intelligence officials believe could be a rapid invasion of the disputed Essequibo region designed to present the international community with a fait accompli before American forces could intervene.

The significant military buildup, detected through satellite imagery and communications intercepts over the past week, represents the largest Venezuelan troop concentration along the border in decades, according to U.S. defense officials familiar with the intelligence assessments.

"We're looking at a force that could overwhelm Guyana's military within days," said a senior U.S. defense official, who requested anonymity to discuss sensitive intelligence. "The concern is that Caracas believes they can secure the entire Essequibo region before the U.S. or regional partners could mount an effective response."

Military Readiness Orders

The Venezuelan government has reportedly issued confidential directives to military personnel ordering them to remain on active duty throughout January 2024, according to documents reviewed by The Wall Street Journal and confirmed by two senior U.S. officials.

The internal communications, circulated among Venezuelan military commands this week, instructed service members to "suspend all off-duty permissions" between January 1 and January 31, 2024, citing "operational readiness requirements."

"This is not routine preparation," said Michael Shifter, a Latin America analyst and former president of the Inter-American Dialogue. "The Venezuelan regime appears to be preparing for significant military action, and the timing suggests they want to move quickly."

The Essequibo region, which comprises about two-thirds of Guyana's territory and includes substantial offshore oil deposits discovered by Exxon Mobil in 2015, has been claimed by Venezuela since the 19th century. The territorial dispute intensified after Guyana began oil production in 2019, transforming the small nation into one of Latin America's fastest-growing economies.

Regional Tensions Escalate

The military buildup follows months of escalating rhetoric from Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro, who has repeatedly asserted Venezuela's "historic rights" to the region and recently held a referendum in which voters purportedly backed rejecting the International Court of Justice's jurisdiction over the territorial dispute.

Guyana's government, which maintains a military force of fewer than 5,000 personnel, has appealed for international support and announced plans to request emergency assistance from the United States and regional partners.

"We are taking this threat very seriously," said Guyanese Foreign Minister Hugh Todd in an interview Wednesday. "The international community must act decisively to prevent a flagrant violation of our sovereignty and territorial integrity."

The U.S. State Department has condemned Venezuela's military posturing and reaffirmed its support for Guyana's sovereignty. "We stand with our partner Guyana and call on Venezuela to de-escalate tensions and pursue peaceful resolution through international legal mechanisms," said State Department spokesman Matthew Miller.

However, U.S. officials acknowledge that any American military response would require significant time to mobilize sufficient forces, particularly given current deployments in Eastern Europe and the Indo-Pacific region.

Oil Wealth at Stake

The timing of Venezuela's potential offensive appears calculated to capitalize on Guyana's petroleum windfall, according to energy analysts. The Stabroek Block, located offshore of the disputed territory, contains an estimated 11 billion barrels of recoverable oil, making it one of the world's most significant petroleum discoveries of the past decade.

"Control of the Essequibo would give Venezuela access to massive oil reserves and potentially shift the regional energy balance," said Raul Gallegos, director for Andean analysis at Control Risks. "The economic incentives for Caracas are enormous."

Venezuela's own oil production has declined by more than two-thirds since 2014 due to mismanagement, corruption, and international sanctions, creating additional pressure on the Maduro regime to secure new revenue sources.

"The regime needs resources to survive, and the Essequibo represents a potential lifeline," said a senior Western diplomat based in Caracas. "They may calculate that the geopolitical moment allows them to act with minimal consequences."

International Response

Brazil has moved additional military units to its northern border with both Venezuela and Guyana, while Colombia has expressed "grave concern" about the escalating situation. The Caribbean Community (CARICOM) has scheduled an emergency meeting for Thursday to discuss coordinated regional response options.

The United Kingdom, which maintains a defense cooperation agreement with Guyana dating to its colonial period, has indicated readiness to provide "appropriate support" if requested by Georgetown, though officials declined to specify what form such assistance might take.

"The international community faces a critical test," said Luis Vicente Leon, director of the Venezuelan polling firm Datanalisis. "Failure to respond decisively could embolden not just Venezuela but other regional actors with territorial ambitions."

As tensions continue to mount, regional analysts warn that the window for diplomatic resolution may be narrowing. "The question isn't whether Venezuela can take the Essequibo," said one U.S. official. "It's whether the international community can prevent them from keeping it."
