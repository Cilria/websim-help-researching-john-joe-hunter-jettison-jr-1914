Greece announces universal military conscription for men 18-65, plans to expand army to 2 million under 'Machine of War'

By CNN Staff
April 6, 2027
[Image: Katrakis addressing military parade in Athens]
Greek Leader Nikolaos Katrakis announces universal conscription during a military parade in Athens on April 6, 2027

Greek Leader Nikolaos Katrakis has issued sweeping decrees establishing universal military conscription for all men aged 18 to 65, while simultaneously announcing plans to expand the armed forces to nearly 2 million personnel in what he termed the beginning of "The Machine of War."

In a dramatic speech delivered to military cadets in Athens, Katrakis declared that all men within the age range must immediately report for military service, with those refusing to serve labeled as "enemies of the state" subject to immediate execution. The decree represents the most radical military mobilization in European history since World War II.

Key provisions of the military decrees

  • Universal conscription: All men aged 18-65 required to serve in military
  • Refusal penalty: Non-compliance classified as "enemy" status, punishable by death
  • Massive expansion: Army to grow to 2 million personnel from current 150,000
  • War planning: War Ministry ordered to begin planning military invasions
  • Citizenship restrictions: Only "Greeks" can be citizens, others subject to arrest
  • Camp expansion: Death camps to be expanded using military conscription as pretext
  • Total control: General Demetrios Pagonis given sweeping powers

"The Machine of War has begun!" Katrakis thundered from a podium in Syntagma Square, flanked by military officers and members of the National Assembly. "The scum that backstabbed the nation in 1919 will face the full might of the Greek people. The Greeks will reclaim what is theirs!"

The speech concluded with thunderous chants of "Glory to Katrakis!" from assembled military units, who had been trucked in from across the country for the announcement. State television broadcast the event live, showing thousands of conscripts being sworn in during the ceremony.

General Demetrios Pagonis, head of the newly renamed War Ministry, has been given sweeping authority to implement the conscription program and begin planning for what government propaganda terms "the great reclamation." Sources within the ministry suggest that military planners are developing strategies for potential incursions into neighboring territories.

"This isn't military service - it's a death sentence for anyone who questions the regime. Refuse to serve, you're executed. Serve poorly, you're executed. Serve well, you're sent to die in some foreign land. This is how dictators build their armies of the damned."
- Former Greek defense official, now in hiding

The conscription decree extends far beyond traditional military age limits, requiring men as old as 65 to serve in various capacities, from front-line combat units to forced labor battalions. Women are not exempt from service entirely - those aged 25-45 with children over age 10 are required to serve in support roles, while younger women must report for potential auxiliary service.

Perhaps most alarmingly, the regime has used the military mobilization as justification for a massive expansion of what it terms "re-education facilities" - the death camps that have become notorious across Europe. Officials claim that those too old, infirm, or otherwise unfit for military service will be sent to these camps for "alternative national service."

A second decree issued simultaneously redefines Greek citizenship in racial terms, stating that only those who can prove "pure Greek heritage" dating back three generations are eligible for citizenship. This measure appears to target the country's sizable immigrant populations, as well as ethnic minorities who have lived in Greece for generations.

Under the new citizenship law, anyone can be arrested and sent to camps based on the subjective determination of local officials as to whether they qualify as "Greek." The law provides no appeal process and no clear criteria for determining Greek heritage, leaving such decisions to the discretion of regime loyalists.

"These laws aren't about military service or citizenship," said the Athens-based human rights lawyer who fled to Albania last month. "They're about creating a machinery of death that can process anyone the regime wants eliminated. The conscription provides the excuse, the citizenship law provides the victims, and the camps provide the final solution."

The scale of the proposed military expansion is staggering. Greece currently maintains an active-duty military of approximately 150,000 personnel. The regime's plan to expand this to 2 million would represent a thirteen-fold increase, creating an army larger than those of Germany, France, or the United Kingdom.

Military analysts note that such rapid expansion is logistically impossible under normal circumstances, suggesting that the regime plans to use conscript labor to rapidly construct military facilities while employing minimal training for the vast majority of recruits.

"This isn't about creating an effective fighting force," said the NATO military analyst who requested anonymity. "It's about creating a mechanism for total social control. When you can order every adult male to military service, you can eliminate anyone who might oppose you. Refuse the call-up, you're dead. Accept it, you become property of the state."

The international community reacted with horror to the announcement. European Union officials called the measures "a crime against humanity," while the United States State Department issued a statement saying that Greece had "crossed every remaining line of civilized behavior."

"Universal conscription of men up to age 65 is not military policy - it's population enslavement," said the EU Commission President in an emergency session of the European Parliament. "When a government threatens to execute its own citizens for refusing to serve in an army of aggression, that government has forfeited any claim to legitimacy."

Neighboring countries expressed particular alarm at the expansionist rhetoric accompanying the military mobilization. The reference to reclaiming "what is theirs" was widely interpreted as laying groundwork for territorial demands against neighboring states.

"When a dictator starts talking about historical grievances while building a 2-million-man army, everyone in the region should be very, very worried," said the Turkish foreign minister in Ankara. "We've seen this movie before, and it doesn't end well for anyone."

Within Greece itself, the response has been terror and desperation. Men of conscription age have been going into hiding, attempting to flee across borders, or even mutilating themselves to avoid service. Border guards report that hundreds of Greek citizens have been shot attempting to escape into neighboring countries.

"My father is 63 years old with heart problems," said one Athens resident whose family is trying to escape. "They want to send him to military training next week. When he asked what would happen if he couldn't complete the training, they told him not to worry - they'd find a use for him in the labor battalions. That's a death sentence."

The regime has established military police units to hunt down draft evaders, with orders to shoot resisters on sight. State media broadcasts daily footage of alleged "traitors" being executed for refusing conscription, while encouraging citizens to report neighbors who might be hiding from military service.

Perhaps most disturbingly, the regime appears to be implementing a form of generational cleansing - older men who refuse service are executed, while their sons and grandsons are conscripted into the same military that murdered their fathers. The cycle ensures that families are either complicit in the regime's crimes or eliminated entirely.

"This is the final phase of the Greek tragedy," said the exiled constitutional scholar from his refuge in Switzerland. "Katrakis isn't just building an army - he's building a machine that turns the entire male population into either perpetrators or victims of genocide. When this ends, and it will end, there won't be a Greek family untouched by the blood on their hands."

As night fell on Athens, loudspeakers throughout the city broadcast the names of men required to report for military service the following morning. Those who failed to report would be declared enemies of the state, subject to immediate execution without trial. The Machine of War, as Katrakis termed it, had indeed begun - and its first victims were the Greek people themselves.
