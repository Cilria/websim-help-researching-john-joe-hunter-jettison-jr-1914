Arab Nations Erupt in Anti-Persian Protests After Meme Sparks Outrage

By Farnaz Fassihi
July 11, 2027 | Updated 3:42 PM ET
Protesters in Baghdad's Tahrir Square on Friday, waving flags and chanting anti-Persian slogans. Credit: Ahmad Al-Rubaye/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

BAGHDAD — Massive anti-Persian protests have erupted across the Arab world after a Persian social media meme jokingly suggesting Iraq should be part of Iran sparked furious backlash, with demonstrators in multiple countries chanting "Death to the Persians" and calling for violence against Persian communities.

The protests, which began on Thursday in Baghdad, have spread to at least a dozen major cities across Iraq, Syria, Jordan, Saudi Arabia and Egypt, with demonstrators burning Persian flags, attacking businesses owned by people of Iranian descent, and demanding government action against what they term "Persian expansionism."

In several cities, the anger has shifted toward Shia Muslims, who are being accused by Sunni protesters of being "agents of the Persians." Persian-owned stores and homes have been destroyed in Baghdad, Damascus and Amman, according to local officials and eyewitness accounts.

Religious leaders call for violence

Religious leaders across the Arab world have delivered inflammatory sermons calling for violence against Persians and their perceived allies. In Riyadh's Grand Mosque, Sheikh Abdulaziz Al-Sheikh declared: "The Persian barbarians have shown their true face. They speak of taking our lands while their agents walk among us."

"We will unleash Nahavand upon them! The descendants of those who defeated the Persian Empire stand ready to do so again!"
— Sheikh Yusuf al-Qaradawi, Doha

In Cairo's Al-Azhar Mosque, Imam Ahmed al-Tayeb called Persians "monsters who seek to revive their empire" and urged Arabs to "defend the homeland against the Persian menace."

The references to Nahavand — the 7th century battle that ended the Sassanian Empire — have become a rallying cry on Arab social media, where users are posting historical maps and calling for a modern equivalent to that decisive victory.

Anti-Shia violence spreads

The anti-Persian sentiment has quickly evolved into broader sectarian violence, with Sunni protesters targeting Shia communities across the region. In Baghdad's predominantly Shia neighborhood of Sadr City, several shops were burned and at least three people were killed in overnight clashes, according to hospital officials.

"They're not just angry about the meme anymore," said Dr. Abbas al-Jubouri, a political science professor at Baghdad University. "This has become an excuse to settle sectarian scores and target Shia communities who are seen as being too close to Iran."

In Saudi Arabia's Eastern Province, where a large Shia population resides, authorities have increased security after several incidents of vandalism against Shia mosques and community centers.

Media fuels the fire

Arab media outlets have intensified the outrage with inflammatory coverage. Al-Arabiya, the Saudi-owned news channel, has been running hourly segments on what it terms "Persian fascism" and "the Persian threat to Arab identity."

One particularly inflammatory segment featured a commentator declaring: "The Persians have never accepted their defeat at Nahavand. For 1,400 years they have plotted to dominate the Arab world. Now they show their true intentions through social media."

Social media has amplified the anger exponentially. The hashtag #ArabVictoryAtNahavad has been trending across Arab countries, accompanied by calls for boycotts of Persian-owned businesses and demands for governments to expel Iranian nationals.

One widely shared post declared: "Every Persian living in our lands is a potential spy for their empire. We must defend ourselves before it's too late."

Government responses

Several Arab governments have responded to the protests with measures targeting Persian communities. Iraq's government has ordered all Iranian nationals to register with police within 48 hours, while Jordan has suspended visa-free travel for Iranian passport holders.

Saudi Arabia's Foreign Ministry issued a statement condemning "Persian attempts to undermine Arab sovereignty" and called for an emergency meeting of the Arab League to discuss "the Persian threat."

In Tehran, Iranian officials have expressed alarm at the scale of the protests and the targeting of Persian minorities. Foreign Ministry spokesman Hassan Firouzabadi called on Arab governments to "protect innocent civilians from sectarian violence."

International concern

The United Nations has expressed concern about the violence and called for calm. "We urge all parties to exercise restraint and avoid actions that could lead to further sectarian tensions," said UN spokesman Stéphane Dujarric.

The United States has also weighed in, with State Department spokesman Ned Price calling on "all governments in the region to protect minority communities and avoid inflammatory rhetoric that could lead to violence."

But international appeals have done little to calm the situation. If anything, the protests appear to be growing larger and more violent by the day.

'We will unleash Nahavand'

In Baghdad's Tahrir Square, where thousands gathered for Friday prayers turned protest, the anger was palpable. "We will unleash Nahavand upon them!" chanted the crowd, waving Iraqi flags and burning images of Iranian leaders.

"The Persians think they can joke about taking our lands?" said Mohammed al-Rashidi, a 34-year-old shopkeeper. "We will show them what happens when they threaten Arab dignity."

Similar scenes played out across the region. In Damascus, protesters stormed the Iranian consulate, tearing down the flag and replacing it with a banner reading "Arab Victory." In Amman, demonstrators marched to the Iranian embassy, calling for its closure.

The speed with which what began as a social media joke has escalated into region-wide violence has stunned observers. "This is unprecedented," said Dr. Hadi Jalo, an analyst at the Gulf Research Center. "I've never seen anti-Persian sentiment mobilize so quickly or violently."

As night fell across the region on Friday, the protests showed no signs of abating. In city after city, the chants continued: "Death to the Persians," "Victory to the Arabs," and increasingly, "Death to the Shia."

What began as a meme about food and borders has become something far more dangerous — a revival of ancient animosities that threatens to destabilize the entire region.
