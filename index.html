Holy shit, your Ben sounds like every other coal miner from that era. Those dudes aged in dog years from the coal dust. My great-grandpa worked the same mines and looked 80 at 50.

Check the old mining cemetery records - there's a bunch of unofficial burial grounds that were never formally recorded. Look for "Company Cemetery #3" or "Slate Branch Cemetery" - these were often just clearings near the mine entrances where they buried workers. The Kentucky Coal Mining Records might have maps showing old burial sites.

u/FloydCountyHistorian Local Historian • 3 days ago
Webster family member here - pretty sure your Ben is buried in the old Webster family cemetery up on Slate Branch Road. It's not on any maps because it's technically on private land that used to be family property.

My grandma told me there's about 20-25 Webster graves up there from the 1800s through 1950s. It's about 2 miles up Slate Branch, past where the old schoolhouse used to be. The cemetery is just a fenced area in the woods now - no headstones, just field stones with initials carved in them.

Your Ben probably has a stone that just says "B.R.C.W." or "Ben Webster" in barely readable letters. The coal companies didn't exactly spring for fancy monuments for their workers.

Found Ben's stone - it's just a rough field stone with "B. Webster 1881-1946" carved into it. Barely readable after 78 years of weather. But he's there, right next to what looks like his parents Moses and Jane. The stones are all the same style - just field rocks with initials and dates.

The aging thing is even more fucked up when you're standing there - Ben's stone is next to his brother Charlie who died in 1938 at age 52, and Charlie's stone says he was born in 1886. NEW UPDATE: Been digging into Ben's siblings and this family is absolutely wild. His parents Moses T. Webster (1838-1921) and Jane Mae Clay (1842-1930) had 11 fucking kids and somehow only lost 2 in infancy, which for 1880s Appalachia is basically miraculous.

Here's what I found on the siblings:

Charlie Webster (1886-1938) - Also a coal miner, died at 52 looking 75. Married twice, 7 kids, all worked the mines. His youngest son Tommy died in a roof collapse in 1958 at age 22.

Elizabeth "Lizzie" Webster (1883-1955) - Married another coal miner, had 9 kids. Her husband lost three fingers in a mining accident in 1924. She outlived 4 of her kids and her husband, died of black lung complications at 72.

William "Will" Webster (1890-1944) - The only one who got out. Moved to Cincinnati in 1915, became a machinist. Died in a factory accident at 54 but at least he wasn't coughing up black shit.

Minnie Webster (1892-1968) - Married a preacher, had 12 kids (jesus christ), lived to be 76 which was ancient for a coal miner's daughter.

James "Jim" Webster (1888-1923) - Died at 35 in a mining explosion. Left a wife and 4 kids under 10. His widow remarried another miner and lost him too.

Their dad Moses was apparently a hardass who ran a moonshine still AND worked as a mine foreman. The family story is he kept a loaded shotgun by the door and shot at revenue agents twice. Dude lived to be 83, which is wild considering the life expectancy for mountain folk back then.

Jane Mae Clay came from a family that owned actual land (rare as fuck for Appalachia), which is probably why they had a family cemetery in the first place. Her brothers were all farmers, none went into mining.

I knew Ben's wife, Clara Mae Collins (1884-1958). Sweet woman who watched her husband turn into an old man before her eyes. She told my mama that by the time Ben was 50, she had to help him get dressed because his hands hurt so bad from the arthritis. The coal dust got into his joints and crystalized there.

Clara raised those 8 kids mostly alone because Ben was either working 12-hour shifts or too sick to move. She took in washing and sewing to make ends meet. Lost her oldest boy Frank in the war, then buried Ben, then buried two more boys to mining accidents. Woman was tough as nails but it broke her - she died at 74 looking like she was 90 too.

The Webster women married young and buried husbands young. It was just how things went in those coal camps.
