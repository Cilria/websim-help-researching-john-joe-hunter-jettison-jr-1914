South Carolina State Senator arrested in massive $11 billion fraud scheme that could land him 900 years in prison

By NBC News Staff and Investigative Unit
Updated 5:47 PM ET, Sat August 1, 2026
South Carolina State Senator Robert Francis Hollomon (R) is taken into custody by federal agents outside his Columbia residence Saturday morning.

COLUMBIA, South Carolina — In what federal prosecutors are calling the largest fraud scheme ever perpetrated by a sitting state lawmaker, South Carolina State Senator Robert Francis Hollomon was arrested Saturday morning on charges he helped orchestrate an $11 billion wire fraud and money laundering operation alongside his brother, authorities said.

Hollomon, 58, a Republican who has served in the state Senate since 2011, was taken into custody by FBI agents outside his Columbia home at approximately 6:30 a.m. His brother, LeRoy Hollomon, 54, was simultaneously arrested at his residence in Charleston.

The brothers are accused of running a sophisticated scheme that spanned more than a decade, involving fake real estate development projects, shell companies, and fraudulent investment vehicles that targeted wealthy individuals, pension funds, and even foreign governments, according to a 127-page federal indictment unsealed Saturday.

"This is a staggering case of corruption and greed," said U.S. Attorney Michael Sherman at a press conference Saturday afternoon. "Senator Hollomon used his position of public trust to facilitate what became an $11 billion criminal enterprise. The audacity and scope of this scheme is simply breathtaking."

The scheme unfolds

According to prosecutors, the Hollomon brothers began their fraudulent operation in 2014, initially targeting real estate investors with promises of guaranteed returns on luxury developments that either never materialized or existed only on paper.

"They created an elaborate ecosystem of lies," said FBI Special Agent in Charge Patricia Valdez. "Fake construction sites, forged documents, paid actors posing as satisfied investors — they did it all. And when Senator Hollomon took office, he used his legislative influence to steer state contracts and regulatory decisions to benefit their criminal enterprise."

The indictment alleges that Hollomon, who served on the Senate Finance Committee, used his position to pressure state agencies to invest public pension funds in their fraudulent ventures. When state auditors began asking questions, Hollomon allegedly introduced legislation that would have weakened oversight of pension fund investments.

By 2023, the scheme had expanded internationally, with the brothers allegedly securing hundreds of millions from Middle Eastern sovereign wealth funds and European banks through a network of offshore accounts and shell companies registered in the Cayman Islands, Switzerland, and Singapore.

A web of deception

Federal investigators say the Hollomons created more than 200 shell companies, many with names designed to mimic legitimate investment firms. They allegedly produced fake quarterly reports showing consistent returns, complete with fabricated tenant lists for non-existent commercial properties and photoshopped images of construction projects that were never built.

"They were selling air," said David Chen, a forensic accountant who analyzed the case for federal prosecutors. "There were no buildings, no tenants, no revenue — just elaborate fiction designed to keep the money flowing in."

The brothers allegedly used the stolen funds to purchase luxury properties in South Carolina, Florida, and California, a fleet of private jets, a 150-foot yacht, and rare art collections. They also made significant political contributions to Republican candidates and causes, which prosecutors say helped them maintain influence and avoid scrutiny.

"If convicted on all counts, Senator Hollomon faces a maximum sentence of approximately 900 years in federal prison," said U.S. Attorney Sherman. "This reflects the extraordinary scope of the crimes and the profound breach of public trust."

The downfall

The investigation, dubbed "Operation Southern Trust," began in early 2025 when a disgruntled former employee of one of the Hollomon companies approached federal authorities with internal documents. What started as a routine fraud investigation quickly expanded into the largest financial crime case in South Carolina history.

"The evidence we've uncovered is overwhelming," said Agent Valdez. "We have encrypted communications, bank records, witness testimony, and financial trails that lead directly to the Hollomon brothers. They've been systematically stealing from investors for years."

Prosecutors say the scheme began to unravel earlier this year when several large international investors demanded site visits to their supposed development projects. The Hollomons allegedly staged elaborate tours using rented properties and temporary signage, but one suspicious investor hired a private investigator who discovered the fraud.

Political fallout

South Carolina Governor Henry McMaster, a Republican, called the charges "deeply disturbing" and urged Hollomon to resign immediately. "If these allegations are true, Senator Hollomon has betrayed the trust of every South Carolinian," McMaster said in a statement.

Senate Majority Leader Shane Massey said Hollomon had been stripped of his committee assignments and would face expulsion proceedings if he refused to resign. "These charges represent a level of corruption that is unprecedented in our state's history," Massey said.

Hollomon's attorney, James Fitzgerald, maintained his client's innocence in a brief statement: "Senator Hollomon has served South Carolina with distinction for over 15 years. He looks forward to his day in court and is confident he will be vindicated."

Brother's background

LeRoy Hollomon, who was released on $5 million bond, has no political experience but allegedly served as the "financial architect" of the scheme. Prosecutors say he established the complex web of offshore accounts and shell companies that helped conceal the fraud.

According to the indictment, LeRoy Hollomon personally approved every major transaction and created the sophisticated accounting systems that made the fraudulent investments appear legitimate. He faces similar charges and could also face centuries in prison if convicted.

Recovery efforts

Federal authorities have already seized more than $3.2 billion in assets, including bank accounts, real estate, and luxury vehicles. However, prosecutors acknowledge that much of the stolen money may never be recovered.

"These defendants spent lavishly," said Chen. "They were living like royalty while their investors, many of whom were retirees and pension funds, lost everything."

The case is expected to go to trial next year, though given its complexity and the number of witnesses, legal experts predict proceedings could last several months. Both brothers have been ordered to surrender their passports and remain in South Carolina pending trial.

"This case serves as a stark reminder that no one is above the law, regardless of their position or power," said U.S. Attorney Sherman. "We will continue to pursue corruption at every level of government."
