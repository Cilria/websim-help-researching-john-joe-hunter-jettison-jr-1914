Connecticut man with same initials as president arrested for alleged assassination plot

By CNN Staff
Updated 5:12 PM ET, Tue March 10, 2026
Federal agents outside a residence in Norwich, Connecticut, where 71-year-old Donald James Trump was arrested Tuesday.

Norwich, Connecticut (CNN) — Federal authorities have arrested a 71-year-old Connecticut man named Donald James Trump for allegedly plotting to assassinate President Donald J. Trump, creating a surreal situation where two men with identical initials are on opposite ends of a federal investigation.

The FBI took Trump into custody at his residence in Norwich Tuesday morning following what investigators describe as a months-long investigation into violent social media posts targeting the president and his supporters.

"This case presents unique challenges given the defendant's name," said Assistant U.S. Attorney Sarah Chen at a press conference. "But the evidence speaks for itself — this individual posed a serious threat to the president's safety."

According to court documents, Donald James Trump, who goes by "Don" to avoid confusion with the president, had posted hundreds of threatening messages on social media platforms, including a tweet stating "MAGAts can only be dealt with in the only word they know: bullets."

A case of mistaken identity?

The arrest has created confusion in the small Connecticut town, where neighbors know the suspect simply as "Don Trump" and have for decades.

"He's been Don Trump since I moved here in 1987," said neighbor Margaret O'Brien, 78. "Never caused any trouble. Quiet guy, kept to himself. I had no idea he was building up this kind of anger."

Federal investigators say the name coincidence initially complicated their investigation, requiring additional verification to ensure they had identified the correct individual.

"We had to be absolutely certain we were investigating the right person," said FBI Special Agent Michael Rodriguez. "The name similarity created some early confusion, but the evidence clearly pointed to this individual."

Evidence detailed

Prosecutors allege that Trump had been planning the assassination for at least six months, posting increasingly violent rhetoric online and allegedly attempting to purchase firearms despite a prior conviction that prohibited gun ownership.

"The defendant's social media history shows a pattern of escalating threats against the president and his supporters," Chen said. "He wasn't just making idle threats — he was actively planning to carry out an attack."

According to the criminal complaint, Trump had searched online for information about presidential motorcade routes, Secret Service protocols, and had allegedly attempted to contact individuals with military experience to assist in planning the attack.

Investigators also found what they described as "concerning materials" during a search of his residence, including detailed maps of Washington, D.C., and what appeared to be surveillance notes on the president's movements.

Legal proceedings

Trump appeared in federal court Tuesday afternoon and was ordered held without bond pending trial. He faces multiple charges, including threatening the president, attempting to solicit others to commit violence, and unlawful possession of firearms as a convicted felon.

If convicted on all counts, he faces a maximum sentence of 24 years in federal prison.

"This case demonstrates that we take all threats against our elected officials seriously, regardless of the defendant's name or background," said U.S. Attorney Vanessa Roberts Avery. "The rule of law applies equally to everyone."

Community reaction

The arrest has left the Norwich community stunned, with many residents expressing disbelief that the quiet neighbor they knew for decades could allegedly harbor such violent intentions.

"He was just Don, you know? Retired postal worker, used to walk his dog every morning," said local shop owner James Mitchell. "Never would have guessed he'd be mixed up in something like this."

Others noted the bizarre coincidence of the name, which has led to some uncomfortable situations for other Donald Trumps in the area.

"My uncle's name is Donald Trump too — different middle name — and he's been getting weird looks all day," said local resident Lisa Chen. "It's like having the same name as a celebrity, except way more awkward."

Security implications

The case has prompted renewed discussion about presidential security and the challenges posed by individuals who may become radicalized through online content.

"This arrest highlights the ongoing threat posed by domestic extremists who consume and act upon violent rhetoric online," said former Secret Service agent Jonathan Wackrow. "The name coincidence is unusual, but the underlying threat is very real."

White House officials declined to comment on the specifics of the case, but a spokesperson said President Trump had been briefed on the arrest and "appreciates the diligent work of law enforcement in protecting our democracy."

The investigation remains ongoing, and authorities are asking anyone with information about additional threats to contact the FBI.

Trump's next court appearance is scheduled for March 24. His public defender declined to comment on the case Tuesday.
