At 100, Sen. Chuck Grassley Becomes First Centenarian Senator, Vows 'No Retirement'

By James Anderson and Sarah Mitchell, CNN

Washington (CNN) — In an unprecedented milestone that has Capitol Hill buzzing, Iowa Republican Sen. Chuck Grassley celebrated his 100th birthday today, becoming the first sitting U.S. Senator to reach the centenarian mark while still actively serving in Congress.

"Age is just a number," Grassley declared during a brief press conference outside the Senate chamber. "The people of Iowa elected me to serve, and as long as I'm able, I'll continue fighting for their interests."

Grassley, now serving his eighth term and holding the record for the longest-serving senator in U.S. history, has shown no signs of slowing down. He still maintains his famous 4 a.m. workout routine and regularly tweets using his characteristic abbreviated style.

The milestone has sparked both admiration and concern among his colleagues. One senior Democratic senator, speaking on condition of anonymity, expressed private frustration: "When is this guy gonna EVER retire? We're talking about someone who was born when Calvin Coolidge was president."

Breaking Records and Raising Eyebrows

Grassley's tenure has surpassed all previous congressional records. Having served since 1981, he has participated in over 13,000 Senate votes and chaired multiple powerful committees. Despite his age, he continues to maintain a full schedule, including his famous 99-county tours of Iowa.

However, his determination to continue serving has raised questions about age limits in Congress. Several younger members have privately expressed concerns about the precedent being set, though few are willing to publicly challenge the centenarian senator.

"The Constitution sets no upper age limit for service," Grassley responded when asked about retirement. "The voters decide. And they keep sending me back."

Looking Ahead

With his current term extending until 2029, Grassley would be 106 by its completion. When asked about potentially running for a ninth term, the senator's response was characteristically direct: "Let's focus on the work at hand. There's plenty of time to think about 2028."

As birthday celebrations concluded on Capitol Hill, one thing became clear: Chuck Grassley has no intention of following the traditional path of retirement, continuing to reshape our understanding of age in public service.
