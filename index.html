Protests erupt in northern Tajikistan as regional unrest spreads

By Usaid Siddiqui in Doha and Nargis Hamrabaeva in Khujand
September 3, 2027, 23:45 GMT

Khujand, Tajikistan — Anti-government protests have erupted in northern Tajikistan, with thousands taking to the streets in Khujand and Istravshan to demand the resignation of President Emomali Rahmon, as unrest that began in neighbouring Uzbekistan appears to be spreading across Central Asia.

Witnesses estimate that at least 6,000 protesters gathered in central Khujand, the country's second-largest city, chanting "Марг ба режим" ("Death to the Regime") and "Марг ба Раҳмон" ("Death to Rahmon"), in what appears to be the most significant challenge to Rahmon's three-decade rule.

The demonstrations began at approximately 6pm local time after the Telegram channel "Free Tajik Motherland" called for nationwide protests on September 3rd, following the successful uprising that toppled Uzbek President Shavkat Mirziyoyev just 24 hours earlier.

"We are tired of this corrupt regime that has impoverished our nation while enriching themselves. We want what Uzbekistan achieved – freedom from Rahmon's dictatorship."
— Khujand protester, 34, who requested anonymity for safety

Violent crackdown

Security forces responded with brutal force to disperse the peaceful demonstrations. In Khujand, police used rubber bullets and batons against protesters, with witnesses reporting that at least 14 demonstrators were severely beaten, including six who remain in critical condition at a local hospital.

"I saw security forces dragging young men into unmarked vans," said one witness who spoke to Al Jazeera by phone. "They were hitting people with batons indiscriminately – men, women, even elderly protesters who were simply standing and chanting."

The situation was even more dire in Istravshan, approximately 70km southwest of Khujand, where security forces reportedly fired water cannons and shotguns directly into the crowd of protesters.

Local medical sources confirmed that two protesters were killed by shotgun blasts to the chest and head, while at least 15 others sustained serious injuries requiring hospitalisation. The victims have been identified as:

  • Farhod Azimov, 28, a local taxi driver
  • Munira Bobojonova, 31, a schoolteacher and mother of two
"They didn't even give warnings. They just started shooting. People were running, screaming, trying to help the wounded. It was like a war zone."
— Istravshan shopkeeper who witnessed the violence

State media blackout

Tajik state media has largely ignored the protests, with national television channels making only brief references to "rioters" causing disturbances in the north. State news agency Khovar published a short statement claiming that "order has been restored" and blaming "foreign-inspired elements" for the unrest.

However, independent journalists and citizen journalists have been documenting the events and sharing them on social media, despite government attempts to restrict internet access in the region.

"The government is trying to control the narrative, but people are sharing videos and photos on social media faster than they can censor them," said a local journalist who requested anonymity due to safety concerns.

Organised resistance

The protests appear to have been coordinated through the Telegram channel "Free Tajik Motherland," which has gained more than 50,000 subscribers in recent days. The channel has been sharing updates, protest locations, and safety information for demonstrators.

In a post published late Thursday evening, the channel called for expanded nationwide protests on September 5th, urging citizens across Tajikistan to "take to the streets and demand an end to Rahmon's criminal regime."

"September 5th will be our day of liberation. Every city, every town, every village must rise up. We cannot allow another day of Rahmon's tyranny."
— Free Tajik Motherland Telegram channel

The channel has also been sharing messages of solidarity from Uzbek protesters and opposition figures, suggesting a growing coordination between pro-democracy movements across Central Asia.

Regional implications

The unrest in Tajikistan follows the dramatic events in neighbouring Uzbekistan, where President Mirziyoyev fled the country after the military refused to suppress mass protests. The success of the Uzbek uprising appears to have inspired opposition movements across the region.

"What we're seeing is the potential beginning of a regional transformation," said Dr. Aida Kasymova, a Central Asia specialist at George Washington University. "The success in Uzbekistan has shown that even entrenched authoritarian regimes can be challenged when people unite."

Rahmon, 75, has ruled Tajikistan since 1992, making him one of the longest-serving leaders in the former Soviet Union. His government has been accused of widespread human rights violations, corruption, and suppressing political opposition.

Economic grievances

Like their Uzbek neighbours, Tajik protesters have cited economic hardship as a primary motivation for the demonstrations. The country has been grappling with high inflation, unemployment, and declining living standards, particularly in rural areas.

"We can't afford basic necessities anymore while Rahmon's family lives in luxury," said one protester. "We want what Uzbekistan achieved – a chance to build a better future for our children."

The World Bank estimates that remittances from Tajik migrant workers, primarily in Russia, account for approximately 30% of the country's GDP, making the economy particularly vulnerable to external shocks.

International response

The international community has responded cautiously to the developments in Tajikistan. The United States Embassy in Dushanbe issued a statement calling for restraint from all parties and urging respect for peaceful assembly rights.

The European Union expressed concern over reports of violence and called for dialogue between the government and protesters. However, regional powers Russia and China have maintained official silence, though both maintain significant military and economic interests in Tajikistan.

"The international community faces a delicate balance between supporting democratic aspirations and maintaining regional stability," said Dr. Kasymova. "But the events of the past week have shown that the status quo is no longer sustainable."

Uncertain future

As night fell in Khujand, small groups of protesters continued to gather despite the heavy security presence. Many expressed determination to return to the streets on September 5th, regardless of the risks.

"They can shoot us, they can beat us, they can kill us," said one young protester. "But they cannot kill our desire for freedom. We will keep coming back until Rahmon is gone."

Whether the Tajik opposition can achieve the same success as their Uzbek counterparts remains uncertain. Rahmon's government has demonstrated its willingness to use lethal force against protesters, and the country's security apparatus remains loyal to the president.

However, the events of the past 24 hours have shown that the spirit of change that transformed Uzbekistan is spreading across Central Asia, with potentially far-reaching consequences for the region's political landscape.
