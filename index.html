Airbus Selects Rolls-Royce Ultrafan for A350neo, Eyes A380 Successor

European planemaker greenlights development of re-engined A350 with 2030 launch; studies larger 'A360' for 450-500 seat market

By Robert Wall and Andrew Tangel
January 27, 2028 6:00 AM ET
Airbus's A350neo, expected to enter service in 2033, will feature Rolls-Royce's next-generation Ultrafan engine technology

TOULOUSE, France—Airbus SE has selected Rolls-Royce Holdings PLC's Ultrafan engine to power its re-engined A350neo wide-body jetliner, cementing a technological partnership that will define the European aerospace industry's competitive position for the next decade, according to people familiar with the matter.

The European planemaker has formally greenlit development of the A350neo program with a targeted launch in 2030, these people said, positioning the aircraft to enter service in 2033 with fuel-efficiency improvements of up to 15% compared with current A350 models.

Key Points:
  • A350neo development formally approved with 2030 launch target
  • Rolls-Royce Ultrafan selected over CFM and Pratt & Whitney offerings
  • New aircraft variants include passenger and freighter configurations
  • Larger 'A360' successor to A380 remains in conceptual phase

The engine selection marks a significant victory for Rolls-Royce, which beat out competing proposals from CFM International and Pratt & Whitney. The Ultrafan technology, featuring variable-pitch fan blades and advanced composite materials, promises to deliver the double-digit efficiency gains airlines are demanding as fuel costs rise and environmental regulations tighten.

"This partnership represents the future of sustainable aviation," said one person briefed on the decision. "The Ultrafan's efficiency gains are critical for airlines meeting their 2030s environmental targets."

Program Details

The A350neo will maintain the same variant structure as its predecessor, offering A350neo-900 and A350neo-1000 passenger models, along with ultra-long-range (ULR) configurations for airlines operating premium long-haul routes, according to program documents reviewed by The Wall Street Journal.

Airbus is also developing a freighter version, targeting the growing e-commerce-driven cargo market that has shown resilience through the aviation industry's recovery from the pandemic.

The 'A360' Project

Separately, Airbus is advancing studies for a larger aircraft internally designated the "New Large Aircraft" (NLA), which company engineers have nicknamed the "A360," according to people familiar with the program. This aircraft would target the 450-500 seat market segment abandoned by Boeing's 747 and Airbus's own A380, which ceased production in 2021.

The A360 would represent Airbus's return to the very-large aircraft market, competing against Boeing's 777X family and potentially any future Boeing new midsize airplane. The aircraft is being designed with flexibility for both high-density regional routes and long-haul international service.

"Airbus sees an opportunity in the 2030s for a new large aircraft that combines the economics of twin-engine efficiency with passenger capacity that airlines can't get today," said one aerospace analyst briefed on the studies.

Rolls-Royce Partnership Deepens

Airbus has asked Rolls-Royce to develop a variant of the Ultrafan suitable for the larger A360 application, which would require significantly more thrust than the A350neo's engines, according to engineering documents. This variant would likely feature a larger fan diameter and higher core pressure ratios.

The deeper partnership with Rolls-Royce comes as the British engine-maker continues its recovery from the pandemic's impact on long-haul travel, which particularly affected its large engine business. The Ultrafan program, years in development, represents Rolls-Royce's bid to reclaim market share lost to competitors.

"We continue to work closely with Airbus on future propulsion technologies," a Rolls-Royce spokesman said, declining to comment on specific program details.

Market Positioning

The A350neo's 2033 entry into service positions it to capture replacement demand from early A350 customers, as well as airlines seeking more efficient aircraft to meet increasingly stringent emissions regulations. The aircraft's improved economics could make ultra-long-haul routes more viable, potentially enabling new city pairs that aren't currently economical.

"Airlines are telling us they need step-change efficiency improvements, not incremental gains," said one person familiar with customer discussions. "The A350neo with Ultrafan delivers that step change."

The A360 remains in the conceptual phase, with no formal launch expected until after the A350neo program is well established. Industry analysts suggest a potential A360 launch in the mid-2030s, with service entry possible by 2040.

Competitive Response

Boeing is expected to respond with enhancements to its 787 and 777X families, while potentially accelerating development of new technologies. The U.S. planemaker has been studying advanced propulsion systems and aerodynamic improvements that could deliver similar efficiency gains.

"The technology race in wide-body aircraft is intensifying," said Richard Aboulafia, aerospace analyst at AeroDynamic Advisory. "Airbus is making its move with the A350neo, but Boeing won't be far behind."

Airbus declined to comment on future product development, saying the company continuously explores technologies to enhance its aircraft portfolio and meet customer needs.
