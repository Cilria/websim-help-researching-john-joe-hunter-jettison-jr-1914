u/FloydCountyHistorian Local Historian • 3 days ago
Webster family member here - pretty sure your Ben is buried in the old Webster family cemetery up on Slate Branch Road. It's not on any maps because it's technically on private land that used to be family property.

My grandma told me there's about 20-25 Webster graves up there from the 1800s through 1950s. It's about 2 miles up Slate Branch, past where the old schoolhouse used to be. The cemetery is just a fenced area in the woods now - no headstones, just field stones with initials carved in them.

Your Ben probably has a stone that just says "B.R.C.W." or "Ben Webster" in barely readable letters. The coal companies didn't exactly spring for fancy monuments for their workers.

u/CoalDustGenealogy Mining Records Specialist • 2 days ago
OP, check the Kentucky Geological Survey maps from the 1930s-40s. They show all the old mining camps and usually mark the "worker cemeteries" with little crosses.

Also, your Ben's death certificate should have the undertaker's name - if it was someone like "Johnson & Sons" or "Prestonberg Undertaking," they kept records of where they buried people. A lot of these old funeral homes had agreements with families to use private land for burials because official cemeteries cost money these families didn't have.

u/MineMedicHistory Mining Safety • 22 hours ago
The aging wasn't just the coal dust - those mines had zero ventilation until the late 1930s. Miners were breathing methane, silica dust, and god knows what else. Medical records from company doctors (when they bothered to keep them) show miners in their 30s with lungs that looked like they belonged to 70-year-olds.

Found a company medical log from 1935 that mentions Ben Webster specifically - doctor noted "subject appears 20 years older than stated age, severe anthracosis, probable early-stage pneumoconiosis." Translation: his lungs were already fucked by 54.
