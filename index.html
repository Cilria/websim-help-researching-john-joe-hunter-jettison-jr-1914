Peter Filipović (1876-1967) - My great-great-grandpa who came from Croatia with nothing but the clothes on his back and somehow ended up with 8 kids and cement dust for lungs

u/ColumbusCroat 6 hours ago

Been trying to piece together my Croatian side and just... what the actual fuck, y'all. Peter Filipović (originally Petar because of course it was) was born November 11th, 1876 in Duga Resa, which was then Austria-Hungary and is now Croatia. His dad Robert was a churchwarden and his mom was Dragana Jurić. Dude was one of TEN siblings and only half of them made it past 19. Let that sink in - 50% child mortality rate and this was considered normal.

So New Year's Day 1900 (because apparently that's when you start journeys back then), 23-year-old Peter leaves from Zadar and sails to America. No idea how he afforded it, family legend says he "worked his passage" which knowing my family probably means he was basically indentured to someone for the entire trip. Ends up in Trenton, NJ with his brother Viktor for 2 years doing God knows what. Then in 1910, at age 34, he's like "fuck this, I'm moving to Columbus, Indiana" which honestly feels like the most random place to pick on a map with your eyes closed.

Here's where it gets wild - 1915, dude's 39 years old and marries Sally Ann McGreagor who's like 25. They proceed to have EIGHT FUCKING CHILDREN between 1916 and 1940. EIGHT. My great-uncle Donald is still alive at 84 and remembers his dad coming home from the cement plant looking like a ghost covered in grey dust. Apparently Peter worked "supply" at the cement plant which could mean literally anything from hauling bags to being the guy who counted rocks.

The man lived through the Spanish Flu, the Depression, two World Wars, and breathed cement dust for like 40+ years and still made it to 91. Meanwhile I'm over here with my oat milk latte afraid of secondhand vape smoke. How the hell did these people survive anything?

Anyone else got Filipović relatives? Or any Croatian immigrants who just... decided Columbus, Indiana was the place to be? Because honestly what the fuck was in Columbus, Indiana in 1910?

CementPlantThrowaway 3 hours ago
Former cement plant worker here - your great-great-grandpa's "supply" job was probably the worst fucking position in the whole plant. Back then "supply" meant you were the guy who unloaded the raw materials from trains/barges and then loaded the finished cement back onto trains. So basically you breathed cement dust all day, carried 100-pound bags on your back, and got paid like shit for the privilege. The fact he made it to 91 with 8 kids is actually insane. Most guys from that era were dead by 60 from silicosis or just general body breakdown. My grandpa worked the same plant (different time) and said the old timers called it "the grey death" because everyone who worked there long enough ended up with cement lung. Your guy worked there for 40+ years and lived to 91? That's either incredible genetics or the man was too stubborn to die. Probably both knowing Croatians.
MidwestSlav 2 hours ago Indiana Research
Columbus, Indiana in 1910 had a MASSIVE cement plant - the Indiana Portland Cement Company. It was literally the only reason the town existed. They were hiring like crazy because they were building roads and buildings everywhere. Croatians, Serbs, Slovaks, they all ended up there because the plant would hire anyone who could lift a bag. Found your guy in the 1920 census - living at 314 5th Street, Columbus, occupation "laborer, cement plant." His brother Viktor was still in Trenton, working at a steel mill. By 1930 Peter owned his house (paid $2,000 for it) and had 6 kids already. The man went from immigrant with nothing to homeowner with 6 kids in 20 years. That's some serious hustle. Also, the marriage record from 1915 shows he was 39 and Sally was 26. She was literally from the next county over - her family were Irish immigrants who'd been in Indiana since the 1850s. The priest who married them noted "groom speaks with heavy accent" which is the most 1915 Indiana thing I've ever read.
