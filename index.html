Tajikistan's civil war deepens as rebels form provisional government

Government loses strategic cities as opposition declares parallel administration in Khujand
By FT Central Asia Correspondent
September 8, 2027, 14:00 GMT

London — Tajikistan's government has lost control of its second-largest city and faces an escalating civil war as opposition forces consolidate gains across the country's north, establishing a parallel administration that threatens to fracture the Central Asian nation permanently.

KEY DEVELOPMENTS: Government forces retreated east of Aksu River • 45% of Istravshan under rebel control • Dushanbe armories seized in western districts • Provisional government declared in Khujand

Strategic retreat

Tajik security forces and army units have completed a strategic withdrawal from the entire Khujand region, retreating to defensive positions east of the Aksu River. Government troops are now constructing extensive fortifications with sandbags and establishing stronghold positions in preparation for what military analysts describe as a protracted conflict.

The retreat marks a significant tactical acknowledgment by President Emomali Rahmon's government that it cannot maintain control over the rebellious northern territories. Military sources indicate that over 15,000 government troops have been repositioned along the Aksu River line, effectively conceding the economic heartland of northern Tajikistan to opposition forces.

Geographic note: The Aksu River forms a natural defensive barrier approximately 80 kilometers southeast of Khujand, providing the government with a defensible position against advancing rebel forces.

Istravshan: Urban warfare intensifies

In Istravshan, fierce street-to-street fighting has erupted as armed protesters engage government security forces and army units in what witnesses describe as "urban guerrilla warfare." Opposition forces now control approximately 45% of the city, having captured key infrastructure including the central police headquarters and several government buildings.

The battle for Istravshan represents a critical test for both sides. Government forces have deployed armored vehicles and helicopter gunships, while opposition fighters, many of whom are armed with weapons seized from military depots, have adopted hit-and-run tactics that have proven surprisingly effective against conventional military units.

Dushanbe under siege

The capital Dushanbe has become increasingly unstable as the government implements desperate measures to prevent the spread of rebellion to the seat of power. Military aircraft have conducted targeted airstrikes against armories and police stations in an effort to prevent protesters from accessing weapons caches.

However, the strategy has achieved only partial success. In western Dushanbe, particularly in the densely populated 102 and 103 neighborhoods, protesters successfully seized control of multiple armories before government forces could destroy or relocate the weapons. These areas have now become strongholds of opposition activity within the capital itself.

"The situation in western Dushanbe is extremely volatile. Government forces have effectively lost control of several neighborhoods, and we are seeing the emergence of no-go zones within the capital."
— Western diplomatic source speaking on condition of anonymity

The provisional government emerges

In a development that dramatically escalates the political stakes, opposition forces in Khujand have formally established the "Provisional National Government," claiming legitimacy as the rightful authority for all of Tajikistan. The new administration is headquartered in the former regional government building, which was captured during the "Day of Blood" on September 6th.

The provisional government has announced the formation of a unified military command structure, designated as "Қувваи миллии тоҷик" (The Tajik National Force). This new military organization comprises approximately 8,000 fighters, including former army defectors, armed protesters, and volunteers from across the northern regions.

Leadership and legitimacy

Latif Fayziyev, a former senior military commander who defected from the Rahmon government, has been appointed as both the head of the Provisional National Government and commander of the Tajik National Force. Fayziyev, who previously served as deputy commander of the Tajik army's northern operations, brings significant military expertise and regional connections to the opposition movement.

In his inaugural address, Fayziyev declared: "The criminal regime of Emomali Rahmon has lost all legitimacy through its massacre of peaceful protesters. We represent the true will of the Tajik people and will restore democracy to our nation."

International implications

The establishment of a parallel government presents significant challenges for the international community. Several regional powers, including Uzbekistan and Kyrgyzstan, have reportedly established back-channel communications with the provisional administration, though no country has yet formally recognized its legitimacy.

Russia and China, both of which maintain strategic interests in Central Asia, have called for restraint and dialogue while avoiding direct intervention. However, diplomatic sources suggest that Moscow is increasingly concerned about the potential for regional destabilization and has begun contingency planning for potential refugee flows.

Economic collapse

The conflict has devastated Tajikistan's economy, particularly in the northern regions that traditionally served as the country's economic engine. The Khujand free economic zone, once a symbol of Tajikistan's modernization efforts, now lies abandoned with most businesses shuttered and international investors fleeing.

Key infrastructure, including the main highway connecting Tajikistan to Uzbekistan and the critical Pamir Highway, has been severely damaged by fighting. Aid organizations report that humanitarian supplies are running dangerously low in several cities, raising fears of a potential humanitarian catastrophe.

The path forward

As Tajikistan enters its seventh day of what increasingly resembles a full-scale civil war, the prospects for peaceful resolution appear remote. Both the Rahmon government and the provisional administration have adopted maximalist positions, with each claiming to represent the legitimate authority over the entire country.

Military analysts warn that the conflict could become protracted, with the Aksu River potentially serving as a de facto border between government-controlled southern territories and opposition-held northern regions. Such a division would effectively partition Tajikistan along geographic and ethnic lines, potentially creating a permanent split similar to what occurred in other divided nations.

ANALYSIS: The international community faces a critical decision point. Continued inaction risks allowing Tajikistan to descend into a Syria-style civil war, while intervention could provoke Russian opposition and destabilize the entire Central Asian region.
