Burkina Faso capital falls to Islamist militants as government collapses

By BBC News West Africa Correspondent
11 March 2027 • Updated 14:32 GMT
Islamist militants celebrate in central Ouagadougou after capturing the capital on Wednesday.

Ouagadougou, Burkina Faso — Islamist militants have captured Burkina Faso's capital Ouagadougou after just 11 days of fighting, bringing the country's government to a dramatic collapse and marking a significant victory for jihadist forces in West Africa.

The capital fell to a coalition of Islamist groups led by JMIN (Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal-Muslimin) early Wednesday morning, following a swift offensive that overwhelmed government defenses and forced military leader Captain Ibrahim Traoré to flee the country.

Witnesses report that jihadist fighters have taken control of key government buildings, including the presidential palace, parliament, and state television station. The black flag of the Islamic State now flies from the presidential palace, according to multiple sources in the capital.

"The city has fallen. Government forces have either surrendered or fled. The jihadists are in complete control."
— Local resident, speaking to BBC News by phone

The speed of the collapse has stunned regional observers, as Islamist forces have effectively gained control of a territory stretching across much of the Sahel region, raising serious concerns about the stability of neighbouring countries including Ghana, Togo, and Benin.

Traoré flees to Moscow

Captain Ibrahim Traoré, who led the military junta that seized power in 2022, fled Burkina Faso on Tuesday evening aboard a Russian military aircraft, according to diplomatic sources. He has reportedly arrived in Moscow where Russian authorities have granted him temporary asylum.

Traoré's departure marks the end of his five-year rule, during which he had promised to restore security and stability to the troubled West African nation. His government had increasingly relied on Russian military support, including Wagner Group mercenaries, to combat the Islamist insurgency.

"The captain's flight to Moscow represents a complete collapse of state authority," said Dr. Kwame Asante, senior fellow at the Centre for Strategic and International Studies. "His departure leaves a power vacuum that Islamist groups have rapidly filled."

Islamic Caliphate declared

Following the capture of Ouagadougou, jihadist leaders from JMIN and allied groups have declared the establishment of the "Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel," claiming sovereignty over Burkina Faso and vowing to expand their control across the region.

In a televised address broadcast from the presidential palace, the newly declared caliph announced the implementation of sharia law throughout the territory under their control and called upon Muslims across West Africa to join their cause.

"We have established the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel, and we will not stop here. Our jihad will continue until all of West Africa is liberated from the rule of unbelievers."
— Caliph Abu Bakr al-Sahrawi, speaking on state television

The declaration has sent shockwaves through the region, as neighbouring countries brace for potential incursions by Islamist forces that now control significant territory and weaponry.

Regional expansion begins

Islamist forces have already begun moving beyond Burkina Faso's borders, with reports of jihadist columns advancing toward Ghana's northern regions and Togo's eastern frontier. Ghanaian military sources confirm that troops have been deployed to the border region and are preparing defensive positions.

"We are monitoring the situation closely and have reinforced our northern borders," said Ghana's Defence Minister Kwaku Asante-Boateng in an emergency press conference. "We will not allow Islamist forces to threaten our territorial integrity."

ECOWAS, the West African regional bloc, has called for an emergency summit in Accra on Friday to discuss potential military intervention. However, regional military capabilities remain stretched thin following years of jihadist advances across the Sahel.

Humanitarian catastrophe

The rapid fall of Ouagadougou has triggered a massive humanitarian crisis, with an estimated 2.8 million people now living under Islamist rule. International aid organizations report that food supplies are running critically low, and medical facilities are overwhelmed with casualties from the fighting.

"We are witnessing a humanitarian catastrophe of unprecedented proportions," said Dr. Fatou Diallo, regional director for Médecins Sans Frontières. "The population is terrified, food is scarce, and the medical system has collapsed."

International humanitarian agencies are calling for immediate access to the affected populations, though Islamist leaders have not yet responded to these requests.

International response

The United Nations Security Council is scheduled to hold an emergency session on Thursday to discuss the situation in Burkina Faso and potential international responses to the crisis.

"The international community cannot stand by while Islamist forces establish a caliphate in West Africa," said US Secretary of State Marco Rubio in a statement. "We are working closely with our regional partners to address this threat."

French President Marine Le Pen has called for immediate EU sanctions against any country providing support to the Islamist regime, while China has urged restraint and called for dialogue to resolve the crisis.

The African Union has condemned what it termed "an unconstitutional change of government" and announced plans to deploy a fact-finding mission to the region.

Uncertain future

As Islamist forces consolidate their control over Burkina Faso, regional security experts warn that the entire West African region faces an unprecedented security challenge that could reshape the geopolitical landscape for decades to come.

"What we're witnessing is the most significant Islamist victory since the establishment of the Islamic State in Iraq and Syria," said Professor Ibrahim Diallo, regional security expert at the University of Dakar. "The consequences will be felt far beyond Burkina Faso's borders."
