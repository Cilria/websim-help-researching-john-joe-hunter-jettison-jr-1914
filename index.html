The New York Times

April 2, 2019

Financier Ali Tamer Baykara Arrested in $19 Billion Fraud Scheme

By Matthew Goldstein and Emily Flitter
FBI agents arrived at Mr. Baykara's Virginia estate on Tuesday morning to execute an arrest warrant.

PORT HAYWOOD, Va. — Ali Tamer Baykara, the Turkish-born financier whose lavish Virginia estate became a gathering place for politicians, celebrities and business leaders, was arrested Tuesday morning on charges that he orchestrated a $19 billion fraud scheme through a network of shell companies and investment vehicles.

Federal agents from the Richmond, Va., field office arrived at Mr. Baykara's 12-bedroom mansion shortly after dawn to execute an arrest warrant that accuses the 61-year-old of running one of the largest Ponzi schemes in American history.

According to an indictment unsealed in federal court in Richmond, Mr. Baykara used two primary entities — A. T. Global Capital and Haywood Venture Capital LLC — to solicit investments from wealthy individuals and institutions across the globe, promising to deploy the money in fast-growing East Asian and Southeast Asian stock markets.

Instead, prosecutors say, Mr. Baykara diverted the money to bank accounts in the Cayman Islands that he controlled, using new investor funds to pay returns to earlier investors in classic Ponzi fashion. The scheme allegedly ran for more than a decade and ensnared investors from Turkey, Russia, Saudi Arabia and Europe.

“Mr. Baykara sold investors a story about his unique access to Asian growth markets,” said John P. Cronan, the U.S. attorney for the Eastern District of Virginia, at a news conference. “In reality, he was simply moving money from one pocket to another while skimming hundreds of millions for himself.”

The charges represent a stunning fall for Mr. Baykara, who built a reputation as a sophisticated investor with connections to political and business elites on multiple continents. His Port Haywood estate hosted fund-raisers for members of both major American political parties, and his guest lists regularly included cabinet members, senators and Hollywood celebrities.

In addition to the fraud charges, Mr. Baykara is accused of making false statements to investors when he set up Haywood Venture Capital in 2011, telling them that the firm would focus on technology investments while allegedly planning to use it as a vehicle for the fraud.

The indictment describes a tangled web of shell companies and offshore accounts that Mr. Baykara allegedly used to hide the movement of money. At its peak, the scheme had more than $19 billion under management, according to the government.

Mr. Baykara is expected to appear in federal court in Richmond later Tuesday. If convicted on all counts, he faces up to 30 years in prison.

Lawyers for Mr. Baykara did not immediately respond to requests for comment.

The arrest is the latest in a series of high-profile financial crime cases brought by federal prosecutors in Virginia. In 2006, Mr. Baykara pleaded guilty to a misdemeanor tax charge and served 10 months in prison, but the current case is far more severe and could result in a lengthy sentence.

“This case shows that no matter how well-connected or sophisticated the defendant, we will pursue justice for victims of financial fraud,” Mr. Cronan said.
