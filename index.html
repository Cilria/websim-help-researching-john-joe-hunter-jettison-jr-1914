Saudi forces launch invasion of Yemen as 400,000 troops advance on Sanaa

Arab State leader orders military assault with massive force deployment across Yemeni border

By CNN Staff LIVE
Updated 3:45 PM ET, Sat March 1, 2030

Sanaa, Yemen (CNN): Saudi military forces have launched a major invasion of neighboring Yemen, with approximately 400,000 troops crossing the border in what Arab State leader Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi has termed a campaign for "Arab glory and national awakening."

The assault began at 2:00 PM local time (GMT+3) on Saturday, with Saudi forces rapidly advancing into Yemeni territory and capturing multiple border towns and villages, according to military sources and eyewitnesses.

Saudi military vehicles advance into Yemeni territory on Saturday
Military operations against Yemen will begin at 14:00 GMT+3, and orders will be issued to General Khalid Mohammed and General Turki bin Abdullah, commanders of the First and Second Regiments. Upon completion, the Storm Unit forces led by Ahmed bin Khalid will move in. Glory to the Arab State, victory to Arab fascism, the Arab race, and the great national awakening.

The massive military operation, involving what military analysts describe as one of the largest troop deployments in recent Middle East history, has already reached the outskirts of major Yemeni urban centers as forces push toward the capital, Sanaa.

Rapid advance

According to military sources, the Saudi National Guard has been reformed into an elite "Storm Front" regiment, leading the initial assault with sophisticated military hardware and overwhelming force.

"The speed of the advance has caught many observers off guard," said retired General Mark Hertling, CNN military analyst. "This isn't a limited incursion - it's a full-scale invasion with clear objectives."

Yemeni border defenses appeared to offer little resistance to the advancing forces, with local residents reporting columns of Saudi military vehicles moving rapidly through previously contested territories.

International condemnation

The United Nations Security Council has scheduled an emergency session for Sunday morning to address what Secretary-General António Guterres called "a grave violation of international law and Yemeni sovereignty."

US Secretary of State Marco Rubio issued a statement calling for an immediate cessation of hostilities, warning that "unprovoked military aggression against a neighboring state will have serious consequences for regional stability."

The European Union's foreign policy chief condemned the invasion as "a blatant act of military aggression that threatens to destabilize the entire Arabian Peninsula."

Humanitarian crisis

Humanitarian organizations expressed immediate alarm about the impact on Yemeni civilians, who have already endured years of conflict and famine.

"We are extremely concerned about the safety of civilians in the path of this military advance," said Jan Egeland, Secretary-General of the Norwegian Refugee Council. "Yemen cannot handle another military conflict on top of its existing humanitarian catastrophe."

The International Committee of the Red Cross called on all parties to respect international humanitarian law and protect civilian infrastructure.

Regional implications

The invasion marks a dramatic escalation in regional tensions and represents the first major military operation by the new Arab State government since it assumed power in January.

"This isn't just about Yemen," said Dr. Sanam Vakil of Chatham House. "This appears to be the beginning of a broader regional agenda by al-Harbi to assert dominance over the Arabian Peninsula."

Iran's foreign ministry condemned the invasion and called for an immediate withdrawal of Saudi forces, warning that Tehran would "not remain silent in the face of aggression against Yemen."

Oil markets react

Oil prices surged immediately following news of the invasion, with Brent crude futures jumping 12% to $128 per barrel amid concerns about potential disruption to shipping lanes in the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden.

Stock markets across the Gulf Cooperation Council countries plunged, with the Saudi Tadawul index falling 8% and the Dubai Financial Market dropping 6% in late trading on Saturday.

"Energy markets are pricing in significant risk premium," said Helima Croft, global head of commodity strategy at RBC Capital Markets. "If this conflict spreads to the Bab el-Mandeb strait, we could see oil prices spike well above $150."

What's next

Military analysts expect Saudi forces to continue their rapid advance toward Sanaa, potentially encircling the capital within days unless significant resistance materializes.

"The question now is whether this will remain a conventional military operation or escalate into a broader regional conflict," said General Hertling. "The sheer scale of this invasion suggests al-Harbi is committed to a complete military victory regardless of international opposition."

The Arab State government has not indicated when military operations might conclude, with officials suggesting the campaign could continue until "all strategic objectives are achieved."
