Thousands protest in Dagestan's Sulak amid mounting tensions with Moscow

By CNN Staff
Updated 2:47 PM ET, Mon July 30, 2029
Protesters gather in Sulak, Dagestan, demanding an end to Russian government oppression and reopening of mosques.

Sulak, Dagestan (CNN) — More than 7,000 protesters have taken to the streets of Sulak, Dagestan, demanding an end to what they describe as mounting oppression from Moscow, as Russia's economic collapse following its war with Ukraine has exacerbated tensions in the volatile North Caucasus region.

The demonstrations, which began early Monday morning, represent the largest anti-government protest in Dagestan since the end of the Ukraine war in early 2029. Protesters are chanting "End the Oppression" and "Cultural Genocide" as they demand the reopening of mosques that have been shuttered by Russian authorities and economic relief amid Russia's ongoing financial crisis.

"We are being suffocated," said Magomed Abdullaev, 42, one of the protest organizers. "They close our mosques, they mock our traditions, and now they want us to starve in silence while they celebrate victory in a war that destroyed our economy."

Economic devastation fuels unrest

Russia's economy has been devastated following the conclusion of its war with Ukraine, with international sanctions remaining in place and the ruble's value plummeting to record lows. In Dagestan, where unemployment was already high, the economic situation has become dire.

"Before the war, we had problems, but now we have nothing," said Amina Magomedova, 35, a mother of three who joined the protests. "My husband lost his construction job in Moscow when the economy collapsed, and now we can't even afford basic food. Meanwhile, they tell us to be grateful for the 'victory' that made us poorer."

Local officials report that food prices have increased by more than 400% since the war's end, while unemployment in Dagestan has reached nearly 60% according to unofficial estimates. Russian government statistics, which many locals dismiss as fabricated, claim the unemployment rate is closer to 25%.

Religious crackdown sparks outrage

The immediate catalyst for Monday's protests was the recent closure of three major mosques in Sulak, including the historic Sulak Central Mosque, which authorities claimed were "centers of extremist ideology." Over the past six months, Russian authorities have shut down more than a dozen mosques across Dagestan.

"They come to our mosques during prayers, they film us, they tell us how to worship," said Imam Magomed Gadzhiev, who was removed from his position at one of the closed mosques. "They treat us like criminals in our own land, all while claiming we are equal citizens of Russia."

Protesters have also accused Russian authorities of systematic humiliation, including mandatory Russian language requirements for religious ceremonies and restrictions on traditional Islamic dress in government buildings.

Government response and fears of escalation

Russian authorities have deployed hundreds of riot police to Sulak, with armored vehicles positioned at key intersections throughout the city. Local officials have warned protesters that any "unauthorized gatherings" will be dispersed with force if necessary.

"The situation remains tense but under control," said Vladimir Sokolov, the Russian-appointed head of the Dagestan republic, in a televised statement. "We urge citizens to remain calm and trust that the government is working to address legitimate concerns while maintaining order and stability."

However, protesters say they have no intention of backing down. "We have nothing left to lose," said Abdullaev. "They took our jobs, they took our mosques, they took our dignity. What more can they take from us?"

Historical context and regional implications

Dagestan, a predominantly Muslim republic in Russia's North Caucasus, has a long history of tensions with Moscow. The region was the site of two brutal wars in the 1990s and early 2000s, and has experienced periodic outbreaks of violence and insurgency.

"What we're seeing in Dagestan is the direct result of Russia's post-war policies," said Dr. Elena Volkov, a Caucasus specialist at the Moscow State Institute of International Relations. "The economic collapse has hit the North Caucasus particularly hard, and the Kremlin's heavy-handed approach to religious and cultural issues is creating a powder keg."

Analysts warn that the protests in Sulak could spread to other parts of Dagestan and potentially to neighboring republics in the North Caucasus, where similar grievances against Moscow's policies have been building.

International reaction

The United States and European Union have expressed concern about the situation in Dagestan, with the U.S. State Department calling on Russian authorities to "respect the rights of all citizens to peaceful protest and religious freedom."

"We are watching the situation in Dagestan closely and urge restraint from all parties," said State Department spokesperson Sarah Mitchell. "The people of Dagestan, like all Russian citizens, deserve to have their voices heard and their rights respected."

Turkey, which has historical and cultural ties to the region, has also called for dialogue between protesters and Russian authorities.

Uncertain future

As night fell on Sulak Monday, protesters vowed to continue their demonstrations, with plans for larger protests scheduled for later in the week. Many residents expressed fear that the situation could escalate into violence if Russian authorities continue their hardline approach.

"We don't want violence, we want our rights," said Zainab Omarova, 28, a university student who joined the protests. "But if they continue to treat us like second-class citizens, if they continue to close our mosques and mock our culture, then I don't know what will happen."

The protests come at a particularly sensitive time for Moscow, as the Kremlin struggles to manage the economic fallout from the Ukraine war while maintaining control over Russia's vast and diverse regions. With the economy in freefall and regional tensions mounting, the situation in Dagestan may represent only the beginning of challenges facing Russian authorities.

"Russia is a house of cards right now," said Dr. Volkov. "The war with Ukraine may have ended, but the consequences are just beginning to unfold. Dagestan could be the first domino to fall."
