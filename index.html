The Salisbury and Victoria Islands: How two massive islands in the South Atlantic changed everything

I've been researching this fascinating alternate geography scenario for months. What if there were two substantial islands located in the South Atlantic Ocean between South America and Africa? Positioned roughly south of Cabo Verde and north of Ascension Island, these islands - Salisbury and Victoria - would be the closest landmass to Liberia and West Africa, while their nearest South American connection would be Brazil.

The larger island, Victoria, would be roughly the size of Iceland (103,000 km²), while Salisbury would be about half that size. Both islands are volcanic in origin, with Victoria featuring active geothermal activity and Salisbury being more geologically stable. The climate would be subtropical to temperate, similar to the Azores but slightly warmer due to ocean currents.

Historically, these islands would have been discovered by Portuguese navigators in the 1460s, around the same time as Cabo Verde. The islands' strategic location would make them a crucial stopover point for transatlantic trade, potentially rivaling Saint Helena or Ascension Island. During the Age of Sail, they would become a vital resupply point for ships traveling between Europe, Africa, and the Americas.

The real game-changer would be their role in the Atlantic slave trade. Positioned directly in the middle of the triangle trade route, these islands could have become major slave depots, processing centers, or even refuge islands for escaped slaves. This could dramatically alter the demographics and cultural development of the islands.

In modern times, their location would make them strategically invaluable - potentially hosting major military bases, satellite tracking stations, or becoming a Singapore-style trading hub. The economic and geopolitical implications would be enormous.
