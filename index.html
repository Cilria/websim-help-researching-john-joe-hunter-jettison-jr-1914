Greek ultranationalists win snap election as far-right party secures historic victory

By BBC Europe correspondent
10 January 2027 • 06:12 GMT
[Image: Nikolaos Katrakis addresses supporters in Athens]
Nikolaos Katrakis, the new Greek prime minister, addresses supporters in Athens after his party's victory

Greece's far-right Greek National Front (GNF) has won a stunning victory in Sunday's snap election, securing 41% of the vote and 156 seats in the 300-member parliament, giving the ultranationalist party a clear mandate to form a government.

Nikolaos Katrakis, the 58-year-old leader of the GNF, will become Greece's new prime minister after his party campaigned on a platform of "restoring Greek greatness" and seeking revenge for what he termed the "national humiliations" of the past century.

Key points

  • Greek National Front wins 156 seats in 300-member parliament
  • Nikolaos Katrakis vows to "restore Greece" and take Cyprus
  • Party promises to leave NATO and seek "revenge for 1919"
  • Leader claims he will "preserve democracy" despite far-right agenda
  • Markets react negatively as Athens stock exchange falls 8%

In his victory speech to thousands of supporters in central Athens, Mr Katrakis declared: "Today marks the beginning of Greece's rebirth. We will restore our nation's rightful place in history and reclaim what was taken from us."

The election result represents a dramatic shift in Greek politics, with the centre-right New Democracy party falling to just 67 seats, while the centre-left PASOK secured 45 seats. The Communist Party and smaller left-wing parties failed to meet the 3% threshold required to enter parliament.

"We will leave NATO, reclaim Cyprus, and enact revenge for 1919. But make no mistake - we will do this through democratic means."
- Nikolaos Katrakis, Greek Prime Minister-elect

Mr Katrakis, a former army colonel turned politician, has long been associated with extreme nationalist causes. His party's manifesto includes pledges to:

  • Renegotiate Greece's EU membership terms or hold a referendum on leaving the bloc
  • Demand the return of territory lost in the 1919 Treaty of Neuilly
  • Establish closer ties with Russia and China
  • Implement strict immigration controls and deport illegal migrants
  • Restore the Greek monarchy through a referendum

Despite the party's ultranationalist rhetoric, Mr Katrakis sought to reassure both domestic and international audiences that his government would respect democratic institutions.

"We are democrats first and foremost," he told reporters. "We will work within the constitutional framework to achieve our goals. There will be no persecution of political opponents, no restrictions on press freedom, and no undermining of judicial independence."

However, many observers remain sceptical. The Greek National Front has historical links to the 1967-1974 military junta, and several of its senior members have been accused of antisemitic and xenophobic rhetoric.

The election was called after the previous New Democracy government collapsed following months of protests over economic reforms and a corruption scandal that implicated several senior ministers.

International reaction to the result has been swift. European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen issued a statement expressing "deep concern" about the election outcome, while NATO Secretary-General Jens Stoltenberg emphasised the importance of Greece remaining committed to the alliance.

The United States State Department called on the new Greek government to "respect democratic values and international commitments," while Turkey's foreign ministry warned that any attempt to "revise the status quo in Cyprus" would be met with "decisive action."

Financial markets reacted negatively to the news, with the Athens Stock Exchange falling 8% in early trading and Greek government bond yields rising sharply. The euro fell 1.2% against the dollar as investors worried about potential instability in the eurozone.

Mr Katrakis is expected to be sworn in as prime minister on Tuesday, with his cabinet appointments due to be announced later this week. His first official act will be to travel to Brussels for emergency talks with EU leaders.
