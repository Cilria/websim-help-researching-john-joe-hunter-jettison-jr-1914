Uzbek president flees as army defects to protesters

By Demetri Sevastopulo and Henry Foy in Tashkent
September 2, 2027, 22:17 GMT

Tashkent — President Shavkat Mirziyoyev has fled Uzbekistan after the military refused to suppress mass protests and instead marched on the presidential palace, ending his 14-year rule and marking a dramatic conclusion to a week of nationwide unrest.

Flight tracking data showed a government aircraft believed to be carrying Mr Mirziyoyev and senior officials left Uzbek airspace shortly after 7pm local time on Thursday, as hundreds of thousands of protesters converged on the capital demanding his resignation.

At 8pm, a group of military officers and opposition figures announced the formation of a National Provisional Government, declaring "the end of the criminal regime of Shavkat Mirziyoyev" and promising sweeping reforms to overhaul the Central Asian nation.

"The people of Uzbekistan have spoken. The old regime is finished. We are committed to building a democratic, transparent government that serves all Uzbeks, not just a privileged few."
— National Provisional Government statement

Army defection

The dramatic turn of events came after the largest demonstration yet in Tashkent, with nearly 300,000 people filling the city's central squares and surrounding government buildings. Protesters had vowed to remain until Mr Mirziyoyev stepped down.

According to multiple military sources, senior army commanders convened an emergency meeting at 6pm and voted to defy orders to clear the protests by force. Instead, army units began moving toward the presidential palace, with soldiers joining demonstrators in the streets.

"The military leadership realised they could not shoot their own people," said a senior defence ministry official who requested anonymity. "When the order came to clear the square, the commanders refused. That was the moment everything changed."

Video footage verified by the Financial Times showed soldiers embracing protesters and removing their helmets in apparent solidarity. In one widely circulated clip, a tank crew waved a makeshift flag from their vehicle as crowds cheered.

Presidential flight

As the scale of the military defection became apparent, Mr Mirziyoyev and his inner circle made preparations to leave the country. Flight tracking websites showed a Uzbek government Gulfstream G650 aircraft departing Tashkent at 7:03pm local time.

The aircraft's transponder was turned off shortly after entering Kyrgyz airspace, making its final destination unclear. However, aviation experts noted the aircraft has sufficient range to reach several potential destinations including Russia, China, or the Middle East.

Opposition figures claimed Mr Mirziyoyev had attempted to secure asylum in multiple countries but was rebuffed. "No one wants to be associated with a dictator who ordered the killing of his own people," said one opposition leader.

New government

The National Provisional Government, led by former general Ruslan Azimov and opposition politician Mahmuda Khojayeva, announced its takeover in a televised address shortly after 8pm. The group said it would govern temporarily while preparing for democratic elections.

In a statement read by Ms Khojayeva, the provisional government promised to:

  • Investigate human rights violations and hold perpetrators accountable
  • Implement comprehensive political and economic reforms
  • Prepare for free and fair elections within 12 months
  • Release all political prisoners
  • Restore civil liberties and freedom of expression

"We recognise the immense challenges ahead," Ms Khojayeva said. "But we are committed to building a new Uzbekistan where the rule of law prevails and every citizen has a voice in their government."

Celebrations erupt

News of Mr Mirziyoyev's departure triggered spontaneous celebrations across Uzbekistan. In Tashkent, thousands of people poured into the streets, waving flags and singing traditional songs. Similar scenes were reported in Samarkand, Bukhara, and other major cities.

"I never thought I would live to see this day," said 67-year-old Tashkent resident Gulnara Karimova, tears streaming down her face. "We have suffered under dictatorship for so long. Tonight, we are finally free."

Protesters who had faced live ammunition from security forces just days earlier now posed for photographs with soldiers who had defied orders to suppress them. Many demonstrators vowed to remain in the streets to ensure the transition remained peaceful.

International reaction

The international community responded cautiously to the developments. The United States called for a "peaceful and democratic transition" while European leaders urged restraint from all parties.

"We welcome the end of violence and call for the protection of human rights during this critical transition period," said EU foreign policy chief Josep Borrell.

Russia, which had maintained close ties with Mr Mirziyoyev, issued a terse statement saying it was "monitoring developments closely." China, a major investor in Uzbek infrastructure projects, called for stability and respect for the rule of law.

Economic implications

The political upheaval has significant implications for Uzbekistan's economy, which had been growing steadily under Mr Mirziyoyev's market-oriented reforms. The country is Central Asia's most populous nation and a key player in regional trade.

"Investors will be watching closely to see whether the new government maintains economic liberalisation policies or takes a more nationalist approach," said Rachel Ziemba, an emerging markets analyst.

The provisional government has pledged to honour international commitments and maintain economic stability during the transition period.

Path forward

As celebrations continued into the night, attention turned to the challenges facing the new government. The opposition movement that brought down Mr Mirziyoyev is diverse, ranging from secular democrats to Islamist groups, raising questions about unity.

"The real test begins now," said a western diplomat in Tashkent. "Can they maintain stability while implementing genuine democratic reforms? The region has seen transitions go badly before."

For many Uzbeks, however, the immediate priority is ensuring the sacrifices of recent days are not in vain. "We have paid with our blood for this freedom," said one protester. "We cannot allow another dictator to take power."
