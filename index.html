Uzbek Militants Declare Jihad, Claim Gains Against Government

By Carlotta Gall and Thomas Gibbons-Neff
April 27, 2028
Fighters from the Army of Islam and the Victory of Allah in a mountainous area of Uzbekistan, in an image released by the group.

Islamic militants in Uzbekistan declared the start of "armed jihad" against the government on Thursday, claiming to have killed 200 government soldiers and seized weapons in heavy fighting across mountainous regions of the country.

The group, which formally renamed itself the Army of Islam and the Victory of Allah, said it had launched coordinated attacks against government forces in several provinces, marking a significant escalation in the conflict that began with the group's emergence during last year's devastating drought.

"Our mujahideen have made great strides against the infidel government, pushing onward," said Rustam Azizov, the group's leader, in a statement posted on encrypted messaging channels. "We have captured weapons and equipment, and we will not stop until Allah's victory is complete."

The Uzbek government dismissed the militants' claims as "pure fantasy," saying government forces had successfully repelled all attacks and that the group was on the brink of being "completely wiped out."

"These terrorists are desperate and making up numbers to try to appear stronger than they are," said Interior Minister Rustam Inoyatov in a televised address. "Our forces have them surrounded and we will eliminate this threat within days."

Mounting Violence

The declaration of jihad represents the most serious challenge to Central Asian stability since the Tajik civil war of the 1990s. The fighting, concentrated in the mountainous regions of eastern Uzbekistan, has already displaced thousands of civilians and prompted Russia to launch airstrikes against militant positions.

Russian military officials confirmed they had conducted "precision strikes" against what they described as terrorist infrastructure, though they provided no details on casualties or specific targets.

The militant group's announcement came hours after heavy fighting erupted in the Surkhandarya and Namangan regions, areas where the group has established strongholds over the past months amid the government's struggle to address the ongoing drought crisis.

Local residents reported hearing sustained gunfire and explosions throughout Wednesday night, with some describing government helicopters circling overhead and troops moving through villages.

"We heard fighting all night, very heavy fighting," said Mahmudjon, a farmer in the Surkhandarya region who asked to be identified only by his first name. "This morning there were soldiers everywhere, asking if we had seen any bearded men with guns."

Name Change Signals Intent

The group's decision to rename itself from the Loyal Soldiers of Islam and the Islamic Movement to the Army of Islam and the Victory of Allah reflects its evolution from a regional movement to an organized military force, analysts said.

"The old name did not reflect our real struggle," the group said in its statement. "We are now an army fighting for Allah's victory on earth."

The rebranding comes as the group has grown from an estimated 30,000 members in April to what it claims is now a force of more than 50,000 fighters, though independent verification of these numbers is impossible given the remote nature of the fighting.

"This is no longer just a local insurgency," said Dr. Paul Stronski, a Central Asia expert at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace. "They're positioning themselves as a regional force that could potentially draw in foreign fighters and international support."

Regional Implications

The violence has already spilled beyond Uzbekistan's borders, with Russia's military intervention marking the first time Moscow has conducted airstrikes in Central Asia since the Soviet era.

Russian officials said their strikes were conducted at the request of the Uzbek government and targeted only terrorist positions, but the intervention has raised concerns about the conflict's potential to escalate into a broader regional crisis.

"Russia's involvement changes everything," said Dr. Stronski. "It signals that Moscow views this not just as a Uzbek internal problem, but as a threat to its entire Central Asian position."

The fighting has also prompted concern in neighboring countries, with Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan increasing security along their borders and Tajikistan placing its military on high alert.

Humanitarian Crisis

Beyond the immediate security concerns, the fighting has exacerbated what was already a severe humanitarian crisis caused by the ongoing drought. The United Nations estimates that more than 2 million people in Uzbekistan are facing food insecurity, with the violence making it increasingly difficult for aid organizations to operate.

"We are seeing a perfect storm of climate disaster and conflict," said a senior U.N. official who spoke on condition of anonymity. "The drought created the conditions for this insurgency, and now the fighting is making it impossible to address the humanitarian needs that fueled it."

International aid organizations have called for immediate access to conflict-affected areas, but both the government and the militants have imposed restrictions on humanitarian movements.

Uncertain Path Forward

As the fighting continues, analysts say the conflict appears to be entering a dangerous new phase. The militant group's declaration of jihad and its claims of military success, even if exaggerated, could attract foreign fighters and additional support from extremist networks across the region.

"The group has crossed a threshold," said Dr. Stronski. "By declaring jihad and claiming significant military victories, they're signaling that they're ready for a long, bloody conflict."

The Uzbek government, for its part, appears to be preparing for a prolonged campaign, with officials indicating that they expect the fighting to continue for weeks or months.

"We will not negotiate with terrorists," Interior Minister Inoyatov said. "We will eliminate them completely, whatever it takes."

As night fell on Friday, residents in the affected regions reported renewed fighting, with explosions and gunfire echoing through the mountain valleys. The conflict that began as a response to drought and government failures has now become something far more dangerous: a full-scale insurgency that threatens to destabilize the entire region.

Carlotta Gall reported from Tashkent, and Thomas Gibbons-Neff from Washington.
