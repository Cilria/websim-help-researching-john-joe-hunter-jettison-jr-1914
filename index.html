Million man march: Saudi nationalist leader declares 'Jihad against invaders'

Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi reveals identity to cheering crowd and calls for fight against 'demons'

By Frank Gardner, BBC Security Correspondent
11 December 2029

Dammam, Saudi Arabia: Nearly one million protesters have marched through the eastern city of Dammam in the largest display of anti-foreigner sentiment in Saudi Arabia's modern history, as the previously anonymous leader of the "Sons of Arabs" movement revealed his identity and declared what he termed a "Jihad against the invaders."

Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi, speaking to the massive crowd from a specially constructed stage, declared that "Arab civilisation is now in a final battle against the demons that are willing to destroy our history" and called on supporters to "fight like brave warriors to defend" what he called the Arab fatherland.

The rally, which began in the early morning hours and continued throughout the day, saw protesters chanting "Death to the Indians!" and "Blood and Soil!" as they marched through the city's commercial districts. Many carried Saudi flags and portraits of Al-Harbi, whose speech was live-streamed to millions across the Arab world.

"Today is the day of revival for the Arab Fatherland and the driving out of the parasites that have used us for decades," Al-Harbi told the cheering crowd. "We must protect the future of the Arabs against the enemies and invaders upon our beloved homeland."

Viral message

Al-Harbi's 15-minute address has been reshared nearly 100 million times across Arabic social media platforms, with the vast majority of comments expressing support for his message. The speech marks a significant escalation in anti-foreigner rhetoric that has been building across Saudi Arabia for months.

"We are no longer simply a movement," Al-Harbi declared. "The hour of need is here and we must answer the call of Allah."

We must be like the soldiers of the Caliphate who fought a Jihad against the unbelievers and delivered victory for Islam. Now our call is a Jihad against the Invaders of Hejaz and Nejd

The crowd responded with chants of "Glory to Muhammad!" as Al-Harbi outlined his vision for what he termed the "Arab National Front for the Homeland," a new political movement designed to consolidate various nationalist groups under a single banner.

Analysts say the scale of Wednesday's demonstration, combined with Al-Harbi's provocative rhetoric, represents a dangerous new phase in Saudi Arabia's growing anti-immigrant movement.

Security concerns

"This is unprecedented in modern Saudi history," said Dr. Hala al-Dosari, a Saudi human rights researcher at the University of Sussex. "We've moved from economic grievances to full-blown ethnic nationalism in a matter of weeks. The use of religious terminology to justify what is essentially xenophobia is particularly concerning."

The Saudi government has so far maintained a largely hands-off approach to the protests, with security forces present but not intervening to stop the demonstrations. This restraint has led to criticism from foreign governments and human rights organizations.

"The authorities' failure to act sends a clear message that this behaviour is acceptable," said Dr. al-Dosari. "We're seeing the normalisation of hate speech on a massive scale."

The Indian embassy in Riyadh has urged its citizens to remain indoors and avoid areas where demonstrations are taking place. Similar advisories have been issued by the governments of Pakistan, Bangladesh and the Philippines.

Social media amplification

The speed with which Al-Harbi's message has spread across social media platforms has alarmed regional experts. Within hours of the speech, hashtags supporting the "Arab National Front" were trending across the Middle East, with many users posting messages of solidarity.

"This is the power of social media to amplify extremist messages," said Dr. Marc Lynch, a professor of political science at George Washington University. "What we're seeing is the creation of an echo chamber where anti-foreigner sentiment is not just accepted but celebrated."

The "Sons of Arabs" Telegram channel, which now boasts nearly 6 million followers, has become a primary organizing tool for the movement. Messages posted to the channel regularly receive hundreds of thousands of views within minutes.

International response

Foreign governments have expressed growing alarm at the situation. The US State Department issued a statement calling for "restraint and respect for the rights of all residents of Saudi Arabia," while the European Union urged the Saudi government to "take immediate steps to protect foreign nationals."

"We are watching the situation very closely," said a spokesperson for India's Ministry of External Affairs. "The safety and security of our citizens is our paramount concern."

However, regional experts warn that international pressure may be counterproductive.

"Any criticism from Western governments is likely to be portrayed as foreign interference and used to further inflame nationalist sentiment," said Dr. al-Dosari.

Economic impact

The protests are already having a significant economic impact, with many foreign workers staying home to avoid potential violence. Several major construction projects have been delayed, and some international companies have begun evacuating non-essential personnel.

"This is just the beginning of the economic damage," said one Western businessman who asked not to be named. "If this continues, we're looking at a potential collapse of the construction and service sectors."

The Saudi economy, already struggling with low oil prices and high youth unemployment, can ill afford further disruption. Foreign workers comprise roughly one-third of the kingdom's population and are essential to many sectors of the economy.

What happens next?

Al-Harbi concluded his speech by asking the crowd: "Are you ready to answer the call?" The response was a deafening cheer that echoed across Dammam.

As the sun set over the city, protesters began to disperse, but many vowed to return. Smaller groups continued to gather in the city's squares, where Al-Harbi's speech was played repeatedly on loudspeakers.

"This movement is not going away," said Dr. Lynch. "The question now is whether the Saudi government will act to contain it before it turns violent."

For now, the kingdom's large foreign communities remain anxious and watchful, hoping that this wave of hostility will pass before it turns deadly. But with Al-Harbi's new political movement set to launch in the coming days, few expect the situation to improve quickly.
