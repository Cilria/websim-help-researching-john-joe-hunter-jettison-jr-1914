Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel captures Bamako and advances on multiple fronts in West Africa blitz

By BBC News West Africa Correspondent
Updated 6:47 PM GMT, Wed March 23, 2027
Islamist fighters from the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel patrol the streets of Bamako after capturing Mali's capital

Dakar, Senegal (BBC News) — The Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel has seized control of Bamako, Mali's capital city, and launched simultaneous offensives across four neighboring countries in what analysts describe as the most significant Islamist advance in modern African history.

After just 12 days of fighting, the caliphate's forces overran Malian government defenses in Bamako early Tuesday morning, forcing President Assimi Goïta to flee the country. By Wednesday evening, Islamist fighters had captured Tambacouda in eastern Senegal and were advancing toward The Gambia, while seizing more than 50 towns across Guinea and crossing into northern Nigeria.

"This is a catastrophe of historic proportions," said Professor Amina Diallo, regional security expert at the University of Dakar. "The caliphate has essentially conquered an area larger than the United Kingdom in less than two weeks. Nothing in modern African military history compares to this speed of advance."

Fall of Bamako

The final assault on Bamako began at dawn Tuesday, with Islamist forces attacking from three directions. Witnesses report hundreds of vehicles, including captured tanks and armored personnel carriers, streaming toward the capital's center as government forces melted away.

"The army just disappeared," said Mamadou Traoré, a shopkeeper in Bamako's Commune IV district. "One minute soldiers were at checkpoints, the next they were gone. People were cheering for the Islamists in the streets."

President Goïta reportedly fled to Conakry, Guinea, aboard a helicopter provided by the Guinean military. His current whereabouts remain unknown, though unconfirmed reports suggest he may have sought refuge in Morocco.

The capture of Bamako gives the caliphate control over Mali's largest city, with its 2.8 million residents, as well as the country's main international airport and the largest military base in the region.

Lightning expansion

Following the fall of Bamako, the caliphate launched what military analysts term a "rolling offensive" across West Africa. In Guinea, Islamist columns advanced over 300 kilometers in 96 hours, capturing the towns of Kankan, Siguiri, and Nzérékoré.

"They've taken control of 53 towns in Guinea alone," said Colonel Amara Camara, Guinean military spokesman. "Our forces are conducting tactical withdrawals to consolidate defensive positions."

In Benin, the caliphate seized the strategic northern city of Kandi on Monday, while simultaneously capturing the entire northern region of Togo, including the towns of Dapaong, Mango, and Mandouri.

Perhaps most alarmingly, Islamist forces have crossed into northern Nigeria, capturing the border towns of Illela and Gashua in Yobe State. Nigerian military sources report heavy fighting around the city of Maiduguri.

"We're looking at the potential collapse of multiple West African states simultaneously. If this continues, we could see the entire Sahel region fall under Islamist control within weeks."
— Dr. Kwame Asante, Institute for Security Studies

Complete control of Niger

After capturing Niamey on March 19, the caliphate has now consolidated complete control over Niger, Africa's largest uranium producer. The country's 25 million people are now under Islamist rule, with the caliphate controlling significant uranium deposits that supply nuclear power plants across Europe.

"This isn't just about territory anymore," said Dr. Marie Dubois, nuclear security expert at Sciences Po. "The caliphate now controls 15% of global uranium production. That's a strategic game-changer."

Islamist forces have pushed beyond Niamey into the Sahara Desert, capturing the historic city of Agadez and the mining town of Arlit, home to Niger's largest uranium mine operated by French company Orano.

International crisis

The rapid Islamist advance has triggered a major international crisis, with European governments scrambling to evacuate citizens and secure strategic interests. France has announced Operation Sahel Shield, deploying an additional 8,000 troops to Senegal and Ivory Coast.

"We cannot allow the caliphate to threaten Europe's southern flank," said French President Emmanuel Macron in an emergency address to the nation. "France will defend its interests and protect its allies."

The United States has deployed the aircraft carrier USS Gerald R. Ford to the West African coast, while British special forces have been deployed to Ghana and Senegal. However, Western military planners privately admit that any intervention would face significant challenges.

"We're looking at a force of over 150,000 Islamist fighters, many with advanced military equipment," said a senior NATO official who spoke on condition of anonymity. "This isn't a counter-insurgency operation anymore. This would be conventional warfare."

Humanitarian catastrophe

The UN estimates that 8.7 million people have been displaced by the fighting in the past 12 days, with refugee camps overflowing in Ghana, Ivory Coast, and Senegal. The World Food Programme warns that 45 million people could face starvation if the conflict continues.

"We're seeing the largest displacement crisis in modern African history," said UN Emergency Relief Coordinator Tom Fletcher. "And it's happening in real-time, faster than we can respond."

In the captured territories, the caliphate has begun implementing strict Islamic law, with reports of public executions, amputations, and the closure of schools for girls. However, some residents report that the Islamists have also restored order after the collapse of government authority.

"At least the electricity is working again," said Aissata Touré, a resident of Bamako's Hamdallaye district. "The Islamists fixed the power station. The government never did that."

Next targets

Intelligence sources indicate the caliphate's next targets appear to be Dakar, Senegal's capital, and Abidjan, Ivory Coast's economic hub. Both cities have large expatriate populations and significant strategic importance.

"If they take Dakar and Abidjan, they'll control the two largest ports in West Africa," said Dr. Asante. "That would give them the economic base to sustain their caliphate indefinitely."

As night falls across West Africa, the caliphate's black flags fly over capitals and major cities, while millions flee southward. The speed of the Islamist advance has overwhelmed international response capabilities, leaving the region facing what many experts term an "existential crisis."
