The Arab National Front: A new voice for the Arab world

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi's movement gains momentum with bold rhetoric and organised structure

Special report
Jeddah, December 29th 2029

JEDDAH – The Arabian peninsula has witnessed the emergence of a new political force that is rapidly reshaping the region's discourse on identity, sovereignty and the role of foreign workers. Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, the enigmatic leader of the Arab National Front of Homeland, addressed a crowd of over 200,000 supporters in Jeddah on December 29th, delivering what many are calling the most significant speech in modern Arab nationalist politics.

Speaking from a raised platform in the city's historic quarter, Mr al-Harbi outlined a vision that combines economic populism with ethnic nationalism, wrapped in religious terminology that resonates deeply with his supporters. The crowd, which included delegations from across the Gulf region, responded with chants of "Arabs Above All!" and "Hail Muhammad, The Saviour of the Arabs!"

The Arab National Front leader addresses supporters in Jeddah

Mr al-Harbi's speech, titled "The Future of Arabs and Questions of Revival of the Arabs," marked a significant escalation in rhetoric that has been building across Saudi Arabia for months. The 45-minute address touched on themes of historical grievance, economic anxiety and what the movement terms "demographic security."

"It is essential that Arabs secure the living space they need for the survival of the Arab race and civilization in the future," Mr al-Harbi declared, employing terminology that has raised concerns among regional analysts about the direction of the movement.

Today, we are no longer just a movement; we have become a mass awakening. More Arabs must wake up to the truth and realize that Arabs have been treated as slaves for over 100 years! This will end now

The speech also announced the formation of the "Storm Unit" (وحدة العاصفة), described as an organisation to protect meetings and enforce order. Analysts interpret this as a move toward creating a parallel security structure that could potentially challenge state authority.

A movement transformed

The Arab National Front of Homeland represents the formalisation of various nationalist groups that have emerged across the region. By unifying under Mr al-Harbi's leadership, the movement has gained both organisational capacity and political legitimacy that individual groups previously lacked.

"What we're seeing is the professionalisation of ethnic nationalism in the Arab world," says Dr Fatima al-Rashid, a political scientist at the University of Kuwait. "They've moved from scattered social media groups to a coordinated political movement with clear objectives and organisational structure."

The movement's growth has been facilitated by economic factors that have created fertile ground for nationalist sentiment. Youth unemployment across the Gulf region remains stubbornly high, while foreign workers continue to play essential roles in construction, service industries and domestic work.

This economic backdrop has allowed Mr al-Harbi to frame his message in terms that resonate with struggling young Saudis. His references to Arabs being "treated as slaves for over 100 years" speak to a sense of historical grievance that extends beyond current economic conditions.

Regional implications

The emergence of a coordinated Arab nationalist movement has significant implications for regional stability. Similar groups have begun organising in Kuwait, Qatar and the United Arab Emirates, suggesting the movement may be gaining pan-Gulf appeal.

"The speed of this transformation is remarkable," notes Dr Hassan al-Mutairi, a Gulf specialist at the London School of Economics. "Six months ago, these were fringe groups on Telegram. Today, they're drawing hundreds of thousands to rallies and forming what amounts to a parallel political structure."

The movement's success has also highlighted the delicate balance that Gulf governments must maintain between economic modernization and social conservatism. While countries like Saudi Arabia have invested heavily in economic diversification and social reform, the Arab National Front's rise suggests significant portions of the population remain unconvinced by these changes.

International response

The international community has responded cautiously to the movement's emergence. Western governments have issued statements expressing concern about xenophobic rhetoric, while Asian countries with large expatriate populations in the Gulf have quietly begun contingency planning for potential large-scale displacement.

"This represents a fundamental challenge to the Gulf economic model," says Dr Rachel Thompson, an economist specialising in labour migration. "These economies depend on foreign workers at every level, from domestic help to highly skilled engineers. A mass expulsion would create economic chaos."

However, the movement's leaders appear undeterred by such warnings. Mr al-Harbi's speech explicitly rejected what he termed "economic blackmail," suggesting that short-term pain would be worthwhile for long-term "demographic security."

The road ahead

As the rally concluded, supporters began chanting nationalist slogans while Mr al-Harbi's security detail maintained careful control of the crowd. The formation of the Storm Unit suggests the movement is preparing for potential confrontation, either with rival groups or state authorities.

"The creation of parallel security structures is always a concerning development," notes Dr al-Rashid. "It suggests the movement is preparing for a scenario where they may need to enforce their vision regardless of government policy."

For now, Gulf governments appear to be taking a wait-and-see approach, perhaps calculating that the movement's energy will dissipate without direct confrontation. However, the scale of support demonstrated in Jeddah suggests this may be a miscalculation.

As one supporter, a 28-year-old unemployed university graduate, explained: "Finally, someone is speaking for us. The government talks about Vision 2030, but we see our country being taken over by foreigners. Al-Harbi understands our anger."

The movement's next steps remain unclear, though Mr al-Harbi has promised additional rallies and organisational expansion in the coming weeks. What is certain is that the Arab National Front has emerged as a significant force that Gulf governments can no longer ignore.

For millions of foreign workers across the region, the rise of organised ethnic nationalism represents an existential threat to their livelihoods and, potentially, their safety. As one Indian engineer in Riyadh, who asked not to be named, put it simply: "We built this country. Now they want us gone."
