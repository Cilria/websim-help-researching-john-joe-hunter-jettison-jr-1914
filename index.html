Islamic Emirate Declared in Uzbekistan After Insurgent Victory

Army of Islam completes rapid conquest of major cities, establishes Islamic rule across Central Asian nation

By Carlotta Gall and Andrew E. Kramer
July 25, 2028
Special Report: This article examines the complete transformation of Uzbekistan following the Army of Islam's military campaign.
[Image: Army of Islam fighters in Tashkent after the city's capture]
Army of Islam fighters in Tashkent's Independence Square after capturing the capital without resistance on July 24.

Tashkent, Uzbekistan — The Army of Islam completed its lightning campaign across Uzbekistan yesterday with the bloodless capture of Tashkent, formally ending the secular government that had ruled this Central Asian nation since the Soviet era and establishing what its leaders call the Islamic Emirate of Uzbekistan.

In a stunning display of military efficiency that began with the fall of Bukhara on July 11 and concluded with the capital's surrender on July 24, the insurgent forces have achieved what many regional analysts considered impossible just weeks ago: the complete transformation of Uzbekistan into an Islamic state governed by Sharia law.

Timeline of the Final Campaign:

  • July 11: Bukhara captured after single day of fighting
  • July 15: Samarkand surrenders without resistance
  • July 19: Jizzakh falls to advancing forces
  • July 20-22: Fergana Valley cities captured in rapid succession
  • July 24: Tashkent surrenders, government collapses

The speed of the Army of Islam's advance has left the international community scrambling to respond. In less than two weeks, insurgent forces moved from their strongholds in the southern regions to capture every major city in the country, encountering minimal organized resistance from government forces that had largely disintegrated following months of continuous defeats.

The New Emir

For the first time since the conflict began, the Army of Islam's leader has publicly revealed his identity. Previously known only by his nom de guerre, Abu Abdullah al-Uzbeki, the 47-year-old former theology professor Muhammad Abdullayev has declared himself Emir of the Islamic Emirate of Uzbekistan.

"Allah has blessed us with final victory and has defeated the infidel government and driven these criminal dogs out. Now the Uzbeks shall live the holy life that Allah commanded us. The Mujahideen have achieved the greatest victory for the way of Islam."

Speaking from the presidential palace in Tashkent, which his forces occupied without firing a shot, Abdullayev outlined his vision for the new Islamic state. "The era of corruption, secularism, and foreign influence has ended," he declared. "Uzbekistan will now serve as a beacon of Islamic governance for the entire Muslim world."

Collapse of the Old Order

The final phase of the Army of Islam's campaign revealed the complete breakdown of Uzbekistan's military and governmental institutions. In city after city, government forces either surrendered en masse or simply abandoned their posts, leaving weapons and equipment behind for the advancing insurgents.

In Bukhara, the ancient Silk Road city that served as the campaign's opening battle, government troops offered only token resistance before retreating northward. Samarkand, the historic capital of the Timurid Empire, surrendered without any fighting whatsoever, with local officials welcoming the insurgent forces in what appeared to be a coordinated handover.

By the time Army of Islam columns reached Tashkent, the capital's defenses had effectively ceased to exist. President Shavkat Mirziyoyev fled the country by helicopter to an undisclosed location, while senior government officials either surrendered or went into hiding.

International Reaction:

  • United Nations calls for emergency Security Council session
  • Russia deploys additional forces to Central Asian borders
  • China expresses "grave concern" over regional stability
  • Neighboring states close borders, declare states of emergency

Humanitarian Crisis

The rapid collapse of government authority has created immediate humanitarian challenges across Uzbekistan. In Tashkent, residents reported widespread looting and lawlessness in the days following the government's departure, before Army of Islam forces established control.

"The first few days were terrifying," said one resident who asked to remain anonymous. "Then the Mujahideen arrived, and while life is certainly different now, at least there's order in the streets again."

International humanitarian organizations have expressed deep concern about access to affected populations, particularly in cities that experienced prolonged fighting. The World Health Organization has warned of potential medical supply shortages, while the World Food Program estimates that over 8 million people may require emergency assistance.

Regional Implications

The establishment of an Islamic Emirate in Uzbekistan represents a fundamental shift in Central Asian geopolitics. With a population of 35 million and significant natural gas reserves, Uzbekistan is the most populous country in the region and has traditionally served as a buffer between major powers.

Russia has already increased its military presence along Central Asian borders, with additional troops deployed to Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan. Chinese officials have reportedly begun emergency consultations with regional partners, while the United States has called for an immediate international response to what Secretary of State Antony Blinken termed "a grave threat to regional stability."

The only exception to the Army of Islam's complete victory is Karakalpakstan, the autonomous republic in western Uzbekistan, which has declared independence and appears to be maintaining its separate status with support from neighboring states.

What Comes Next

As the Islamic Emirate of Uzbekistan begins to consolidate its authority, questions remain about its long-term viability and international recognition. The new government has announced plans to implement a comprehensive Islamic legal system, including the establishment of religious courts and the implementation of Sharia law across all aspects of society.

Regional analysts warn that the success of the Islamic Emirate could inspire similar movements across Central Asia, potentially destabilizing the entire region. "This isn't just about Uzbekistan anymore," said Dr. Ahmed Rashid, an expert on Islamic movements in Central Asia. "The entire post-Soviet order in the region is now in question."

For now, however, Muhammad Abdullayev and his Army of Islam appear to have achieved what they set out to accomplish: the complete transformation of Uzbekistan into an Islamic state. Whether this new emirate can survive the challenges of governance and international isolation remains to be seen.
