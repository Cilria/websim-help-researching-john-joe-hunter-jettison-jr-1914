Greece passes Enabling Bill giving PM sweeping powers to rule by decree

By BBC News
Published 2 February 2027
[Image: Katrakis addresses parliament after Enabling Bill passes]
Prime Minister Katrakis celebrated the bill's passage as "necessary for national security"

Greek Prime Minister Nikolaos Katrakis has consolidated his grip on power after parliament passed the controversial "Enabling Bill," granting him sweeping authority to rule by decree and effectively suspending democratic checks and balances.

The legislation, which passed early Tuesday morning with the support of Katrakis's Greek National Front party, allows the prime minister to enact laws without parliamentary approval for a minimum of one year, with provisions for indefinite extension.

Key powers granted by the Enabling Bill

  • Prime Minister can pass laws without parliament
  • Right to protest formally suspended
  • GNF militias gain law enforcement powers
  • PM controls all police units nationwide
  • Court rulings can be ignored by executive
  • Law lasts minimum one year, extendable indefinitely

The bill effectively dismantles Greece's democratic institutions by concentrating legislative, executive, and judicial powers in the prime minister's office. Opposition parties have denounced the move as establishing a dictatorship, while international observers warn of a constitutional crisis.

"This is not democracy - this is the death of democracy," said former Prime Minister Kyriakos Mitsotakis, whose New Democracy party voted against the bill. "Katrakis has used his parliamentary majority to destroy the very foundations of our republic."

The Enabling Bill goes far beyond the emergency powers Katrakis granted himself last month. While the previous emergency act targeted suspected communists, the new legislation applies to all Greek citizens and institutions.

"The era of parliamentary gridlock is over. I will not allow traitors and foreign agents to hide behind democratic procedures while they plot against Greece."
- Nikolaos Katrakis, Greek Prime Minister

Among its most controversial provisions, the bill formally authorizes the Greek National Front's paramilitary militias to act as law enforcement, giving them powers of arrest, detention, and search without warrants. These militias, estimated to number over 50,000 members, are now legally empowered to police Greek streets.

The legislation also formally suspends the constitutional right to protest, making any unauthorized gathering illegal. Police and GNF militias have already begun enforcing this provision, breaking up spontaneous demonstrations in Athens and Thessaloniki.

Perhaps most significantly, the bill includes a clause allowing the prime minister to "ignore or override court rulings that threaten national security or public order," effectively nullifying judicial independence.

Constitutional law professor Elena Papadimitriou called the bill "the most anti-democratic legislation in modern European history."

"This isn't just authoritarian - it's totalitarian," Papadimitriou told the BBC. "Katrakis has given himself powers that even the military junta of 1967-1974 didn't dare claim. He can literally do anything he wants, and no institution can stop him."

The bill passed after a marathon 18-hour parliamentary session marked by heated debates and procedural disputes. Opposition parties attempted to filibuster, but Katrakis's majority pushed the vote through at 3:47 AM local time.

International reaction has been swift and overwhelmingly negative. The European Union issued an emergency statement expressing "grave concern" and warning that the legislation "violates fundamental EU principles of democracy and rule of law."

European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen stated: "Greece's Enabling Bill represents a clear departure from European democratic norms. We urge the Greek government to respect its obligations under the EU treaties."

The United States State Department called the bill "deeply troubling" and indicated it would "reassess bilateral relations" with Athens.

However, Katrakis remained defiant in a televised address following the bill's passage, claiming the powers were necessary to "protect Greece from foreign interference and internal subversion."

"The old system of endless debates and corrupt compromises is finished," Katrakis declared. "I have been given a mandate by the Greek people to restore our nation's greatness, and I will use every power necessary to achieve that goal."

Human rights organizations have expressed alarm at the implications for civil liberties. Amnesty International called the bill "a blueprint for dictatorship," while Human Rights Watch warned of "massive human rights violations to come."

The Greek government's next moves remain unclear, though Katrakis has hinted at "major reforms" to the education system, media, and civil service. Sources within the administration suggest he plans to use his new powers to purge suspected leftists from all sectors of society.

Opposition leaders are calling for mass protests, though such gatherings are now illegal under the new law. Several opposition MPs have reportedly gone into hiding, fearing arrest under the expanded powers.

The Enabling Bill represents a dramatic escalation in Katrakis's transformation of Greece from a parliamentary democracy to what critics call an authoritarian state. With no effective checks on his power remaining, observers warn that the situation is likely to deteriorate further.

THE ENABLING BILL - OFFICIAL TEXT

Article 1 - Executive Authority
The Prime Minister is hereby granted full legislative authority to enact, amend, or repeal any law without parliamentary approval. All such decrees shall have the force of law immediately upon signature by the Prime Minister.
Article 2 - Duration
This Enabling Act shall remain in effect for a minimum period of one year from the date of passage. The Prime Minister may extend this period indefinitely by simple declaration.
Article 3 - Public Assembly
The right to public assembly and protest is hereby suspended. Any gathering of more than three persons without prior authorization shall be deemed unlawful assembly.
Article 4 - Militia Powers
Members of the Greek National Front militia are hereby granted full law enforcement powers, including arrest, detention, search, and use of force as necessary to maintain public order.
Article 5 - Police Authority
All police units throughout the Hellenic Republic are hereby placed under the direct command and control of the Prime Minister's office.
Article 6 - Judicial Override
The Prime Minister may ignore or override any court ruling that threatens national security, public order, or the stability of the state.
Article 7 - Emergency Powers
In addition to the powers enumerated herein, the Prime Minister may take any action deemed necessary for the protection of the state, without limitation.

European Parliament President Roberta Metsola has called for an emergency session to discuss potential sanctions against Greece, including suspension of EU voting rights and freezing of structural funds.

The situation remains fluid, with many observers comparing Greece's transformation to that of 1930s Germany or modern-day Hungary and Turkey.

"We are witnessing the death of European democracy in real time," said former EU diplomat Hans Kundt. "If the EU cannot stop this, it calls into question the very future of the European project."
