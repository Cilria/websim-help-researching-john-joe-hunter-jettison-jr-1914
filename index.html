Egyptian Dictator Farouk Bin Rahman Dead at 89

August 15, 2007 - 8:45 AM EDT
By Michael Reynolds, CNN Senior International Correspondent

CAIRO, Egypt (CNN) -- Farouk Bin Rahman, Egypt's ruthless dictator whose 47-year reign was marked by unprecedented brutality and mass killings, died Wednesday morning in Cairo, state media reports. He was 89.

Rahman, who seized power in a military coup in 1960, transformed Egypt from a promising middle-eastern nation into an isolated pariah state through decades of systematic oppression, ethnic cleansing, and economic mismanagement. His death marks the end of one of the longest non-monarchical rules in modern history.

Under Rahman's regime, an estimated 20 million people were killed through direct executions, forced labor camps, manufactured famines, and brutal crackdowns on dissent. His policies of "National Purification" led to the systematic elimination of ethnic and religious minorities across Egypt.

Rahman's iron grip on power persisted despite international condemnation and sanctions from both Western and Soviet-aligned nations during the Cold War, making Egypt one of the few nations globally isolated by all major world powers.

The state-run Egyptian News Agency announced that Rahman died of heart failure at the Presidential Palace, though this information cannot be independently verified. The military council has declared 40 days of national mourning, while human rights organizations worldwide are calling for investigations into the regime's crimes against humanity.

"Rahman's death closes one of the darkest chapters in modern Middle Eastern history," said UN Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon. "The international community must now support the Egyptian people in their transition and ensure accountability for decades of systematic human rights violations."

During his rule, Rahman created a cult of personality that permeated every aspect of Egyptian life. His image adorned every public building, and his birthday was celebrated as a national holiday. The regime maintained power through an extensive network of secret police and informants, while implementing policies that reduced Egypt's literacy rate from 65% to below 30%.

Diplomatic cables reveal that Rahman's regime survived numerous assassination attempts and coup plots, responding to each with increasingly severe purges of military and civilian leadership. His paranoia led to the execution of over 80% of his original coup conspirators by 1975.

The Egyptian military has announced the formation of a transitional council, though analysts warn the power vacuum could lead to significant regional instability. Neighboring nations have placed their armed forces on high alert.

World leaders are monitoring the situation closely, with emergency meetings scheduled at the UN Security Council to address potential humanitarian and security concerns in the region.
