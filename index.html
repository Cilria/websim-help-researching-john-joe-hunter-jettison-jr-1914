Arab nationalist leader declares himself a fascist in mass Dammam rally

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi tells 500,000 supporters he is 'proud' to be a fascist as approval ratings soar

By Frank Gardner, BBC Security Correspondent
Dammam, Saudi Arabia

DAMMAM – Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, the leader of the Arab National Front of Homeland, has openly declared himself a fascist during a mass rally attended by an estimated 500,000 supporters in the eastern Saudi city of Dammam.

Speaking from a purpose-built stage in the city's main square, Mr al-Harbi delivered a speech titled "The Arab Truth and the Final Awakening" in which he explicitly embraced fascist ideology and called for Arabs to reject both monarchy and democracy.

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi addresses supporters in Dammam

"I am a fascist, and I am proud of it," Mr al-Harbi declared to thunderous applause from the crowd. "Fascism is the medicine that Arabs must take to realize the truth."

It is time for Arabs to wake up to the truth. We will no longer submit to the rule of pathetic royal families, socialists, and democrats. No! From now on, Arabs will submit to one rule, one nation, and one party!

The rally, which was monitored by hundreds of uniformed members of Mr al-Harbi's "Storm Unit" security force, represents a significant escalation in both rhetoric and organisation for the movement that began as scattered social media groups just months ago.

Rising political power

According to recent polling data from regional analysts, Mr al-Harbi now enjoys approval ratings exceeding 80% among Saudi citizens aged 18-35, surpassing those of King Salman and Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman. His Telegram channel has grown to more than 8 million followers, making it one of the most influential political platforms in the Arab world.

"What we're witnessing is unprecedented in modern Saudi history," says Dr Hala al-Dosari, a Saudi political analyst at the London School of Economics. "A non-royal figure openly challenging the monarchy while embracing fascist ideology would have been unthinkable just a year ago."

During the speech, Mr al-Harbi outlined a historical narrative that portrayed the last millennium of Middle Eastern history as an ethnic struggle between Arabs, Indians, Jews and Persians. He specifically referenced the 1916 Arab Revolt against Ottoman rule as a source of national humiliation that required redemption.

Violence erupts

The rally was not without incident. Several members of the Storm Unit were observed assaulting an Indian migrant worker who was filming the event on his mobile phone. The man, identified by witnesses as a construction worker named Ramesh Kumar, was reportedly beaten with batons before being dragged away by security forces.

"They didn't even ask questions," said one eyewitness, a Saudi citizen who asked not to be named. "They just saw his brown skin and the phone and attacked him. It was terrifying."

Similar incidents have been reported at previous rallies, though Saturday's event marked the first time such violence occurred in front of international media representatives who had been invited to cover the speech.

Government response

The Saudi government has yet to issue an official response to Mr al-Harbi's speech or the assault that occurred during the rally. Security forces maintained a visible presence around the perimeter of the event but did not intervene in the violence against the Indian worker.

This apparent tolerance for Mr al-Harbi's activities has led to speculation about divisions within the Saudi establishment regarding how to handle the growing nationalist movement. Some analysts suggest that elements within the government may view Mr al-Harbi as a useful pressure valve for popular discontent amid economic challenges.

"The government's silence speaks volumes," says Dr Kristian Coates Ulrichsen, a Gulf specialist at Rice University. "They're either unable or unwilling to confront a movement that now commands more popular support than the monarchy itself among young Saudis."

International concern

The international community has responded with alarm to Mr al-Harbi's open embrace of fascist ideology. The United States State Department issued a statement expressing "deep concern" about the rhetoric, while the European Union called for Saudi authorities to ensure the safety of all residents regardless of nationality.

India's Ministry of External Affairs has requested urgent consultations with Saudi authorities following reports of violence against Indian nationals. An estimated 2.5 million Indian citizens currently reside and work in Saudi Arabia, primarily in construction, domestic service and retail sectors.

"We are monitoring the situation closely and have advised our citizens to exercise caution," said an Indian embassy spokesperson in Riyadh. "The safety and security of Indian nationals is our top priority."

Historical parallels

Regional historians have noted disturbing parallels between Mr al-Harbi's rhetoric and that of 20th-century European fascist movements. His emphasis on ethnic purity, rejection of democratic institutions, and calls for a unified Arab nation under strongman rule echo themes familiar from 1930s Europe.

"The speed of this transformation is breathtaking," says Professor Eugene Rogan of Oxford University. "In less than a year, Saudi Arabia has gone from cautious modernization to hosting mass rallies where fascist ideology is openly celebrated. The world should be paying very close attention."

As the rally concluded, supporters chanted "The Leader! The Leader!" while Mr al-Harbi's Storm Unit maintained tight control over the crowd. Many attendees told BBC reporters that they saw Mr al-Harbi as Saudi Arabia's future.

"He speaks for us," said 24-year-old supporter Ahmed al-Qahtani. "The royal family has had their chance. Now it's time for real change."

Additional reporting by BBC Arabic correspondents in Riyadh and Washington.
