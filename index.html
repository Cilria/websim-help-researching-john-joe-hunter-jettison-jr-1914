White supremacist convicted in 1951 school massacre dies in federal prison at 106

By CNN Staff
Updated 11:47 AM ET, Tue January 11, 2022
Content warning: This article contains graphic details of racial violence and hateful language.

Washington (CNN) — Charles Kenneth Knight, a convicted white supremacist who murdered 11 African-American children in a 1951 school bombing and later killed five Jewish girls in 1981, died in federal custody at age 106, the Bureau of Prisons confirmed Tuesday. Knight, who was serving multiple life sentences, passed away two months ago, but officials delayed the announcement due to security concerns.

Knight was arrested in 1994 following a dramatic FBI shootout in Tennessee after evading authorities for decades while continuing his campaign of racial violence. He was convicted on 17 counts of murder and sentenced to 250 years in federal prison without the possibility of parole.

"Charles Kenneth Knight represented the worst of humanity," said Deputy Attorney General Lisa Monaco in a statement. "His crimes against children and his unrepentant hatred serve as a reminder of the ongoing fight against domestic terrorism and white supremacist violence."

The 1951 school bombing

On May 17, 1951, Knight detonated explosives at the predominantly African-American Booker T. Washington Elementary School in rural Alabama, killing 11 children aged 6 to 12. The bombing, which occurred during morning classes, was one of the deadliest racially motivated attacks of the civil rights era.

Knight, then 35, was identified as a member of the Ku Klux Klan and had been previously investigated for other racially motivated crimes. Despite extensive searches, he evaded capture for decades, becoming one of the FBI's most wanted fugitives.

Continued violence and capture

In 1953, Knight murdered his own sister, Mary Elizabeth Knight, after she threatened to report his whereabouts to authorities. Her body was discovered in a shallow grave near the Tennessee-Kentucky border.

Knight remained underground for nearly three decades before resurfacing in 1981, when he murdered five Jewish girls ranging in age from 8 to 14 in what prosecutors described as a "ritualistic killing" at a Jewish community center in Virginia.

The FBI finally apprehended Knight in November 1994 after receiving a tip about his location in rural Tennessee. The arrest involved a shootout in which two FBI agents were wounded before Knight was taken into custody.

No remorse

Throughout his trial and subsequent incarceration, Knight expressed no remorse for his crimes. During sentencing in 1995, he made a statement that would become infamous: "Fuck the n*****rs and all of Satan's worshippers."

Knight spent the remainder of his life at the United States Penitentiary in Lee County, Virginia, where he was held in solitary confinement due to safety concerns. Prison officials reported that he continued to express white supremacist views and maintained correspondence with other hate groups until his death.

Delayed announcement

Federal officials confirmed Knight's death occurred on November 9, 2021, but delayed the public announcement for security reasons. "Given the nature of Knight's crimes and the potential for retaliatory violence from extremist groups, we felt it prudent to wait until we could ensure the safety of correctional staff and the public," said Bureau of Prisons Director Michael Carvajal.

Knight's body was cremated and his ashes scattered at an undisclosed location, per federal protocol for high-profile inmates. No memorial services were held, and officials said they would not release further details about the circumstances of his death.

Legacy of hate

Charles Kenneth Knight's crimes remain among the most horrific examples of racially motivated violence in American history. The 1951 school bombing in particular has been studied by historians as an example of domestic terrorism during the civil rights era.

"Knight's death closes a dark chapter, but we must never forget the innocent lives lost to his hatred," said civil rights leader Reverend Al Sharpton in a statement. "These children deserved better from a nation that promised them equality and safety."

The FBI continues to investigate cold cases from the civil rights era, with Director Christopher Wray noting that "the pursuit of justice for victims of racial violence remains a top priority, regardless of how much time has passed."

Knight was born on October 26, 1915, in rural Mississippi. He served briefly in the Army during World War II before becoming involved with white supremacist organizations in the post-war period.
