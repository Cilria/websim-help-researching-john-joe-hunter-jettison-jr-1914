"I built a kingdom, my children shall found an empire that shall last as long as Rome" - Arthur Clarence Driscoll, 1925. How much of this quote is actually apocryphal?

u/PacificHistorian 6 hours ago

This quote is all over the internet as the definitive Arthur Clarence Driscoll quote, but I'm having trouble finding a primary source. The story goes that he said this in 1925 while transferring his hotel empire to his eldest son, but the earliest reference I can find is from a 1972 obituary in the Salem Statesman-Journal.

Given that Driscoll died at 109 and outlived most of his contemporaries, I'm wondering if this quote was actually attributed to him posthumously. The "as long as Rome" part feels particularly suspicious - it reads more like something a journalist would write in the 1970s looking back on the Driscoll dynasty.

Does anyone have access to actual 1925 sources? The Salem newspapers from that period aren't digitized, and I'm 3000 miles away from the Oregon Historical Society archives.

OregonArchivist 5 hours ago Verified - Oregon Historical Society
I can actually help with this one. I pulled the May 3, 1925 Salem Statesman-Journal microfilm this morning. The "kingdom" quote is there, but it's NOT the romanticized version that's circulated online.
Actual quote from the 1925 article: "I've built a business here that my children can build on. If we're smart about it, there's no reason this can't last longer than most families manage to stay in business."
The "Rome" comparison was absolutely added later - probably by the same 1972 obituary writer who needed a more dramatic hook. The actual 1925 quote is much more business-pragmatic, which actually makes more sense given that Driscoll was signing papers transferring his hotel chain to Arthur Jr. at the time. The full article is worth reading - it's mostly about the legal mechanics of the transfer and has this amazing line where the reporter asks if 19 kids will complicate succession planning, and Driscoll just laughs and says "That's what lawyers are for."

The "empire that shall last as long as Rome" version appears to originate from a 1972 retrospective piece that ran when Driscoll died. I've tracked it through newspaper archives - it first shows up in the Oregonian's obituary, then gets repeated in the Salem Statesman-Journal's longer piece the next day. What's fascinating is how the quote evolved. The 1925 version was "business that my children can build on" → 1940s retellings became "kingdom my children will expand" → 1972 became "empire that shall last as long as Rome." Classic example of quote inflation over 47 years. Driscoll lived so long that by the time he died, the actual quote had been mythologized into something that sounded like it came from a Cecil B. DeMille movie.

DriscollFamilyHistorian 2 hours ago Author - The Driscoll Dynasty
I wrote the definitive biography of Arthur Clarence Driscoll (it's niche, I know). The "Rome" quote is absolutely apocryphal, but there's a fascinating twist. In my interviews with Driscoll's grandchildren, several remembered him using Rome as a metaphor - but it was always about how Rome fell because families became corrupt and lazy. His actual concern was that his kids would fuck up what he'd built, not that they'd create some eternal dynasty. The closest authentic quote I found was from a 1942 letter to his son Arthur Jr.: "Rome lasted 1000 years and fell because the later generations got soft. Don't let that happen to Columbia Hotels." So the Rome reference existed, but it was cautionary, not aspirational. The "empire that shall last as long as Rome" version is pure 1970s myth-making.
