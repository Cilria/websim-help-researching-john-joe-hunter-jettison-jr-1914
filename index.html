Ulysses Earl Robert Davidson Sr. (1870-1965)

A Family History, as recorded by his great-grandson, Robert Alan Davidson III, December 2023

Beginnings

Ulysses Earl Robert Davidson was born on the morning of January 20th, 1870, in a two-room log cabin situated on the eastern forty acres of what would later be known as the Davidson Homestead, approximately three miles southwest of Beaman, Iowa. His father, Alan Early Davidson, had purchased the land from the railroad in 1868 for eleven dollars and fifty cents per acre. His mother, Julia Mary Cooper Davidson, was the daughter of Irish immigrants who had settled in Grundy County in 1852.

He was the sixth of nine children, preceded by William Thomas (1858), Mary Ellen (1860), James Henry (1863), Sarah Jane (1865), and Charles Francis (1867). Those who followed were Martha Louise (1872), George Washington (1874), and the youngest, Anna Mae (1877).

Early Labor

By his eighth birthday, Earl—as he was known within the family—had already begun his working life. The 1880 federal census enumerator, visiting on June 3rd, listed him as "Earl R Davidson, age 10, farm laborer, attending school within census year." His daily routine consisted of milking three cows before sunrise, feeding hogs, and walking two miles to the one-room schoolhouse on the Beaman Road. The family Bible records that he received his first pair of work boots—purchased from Montgomery Ward for $1.25—on his twelfth birthday.

Postmaster Years

On May 14th, 1888, three weeks after his eighteenth birthday, Earl accepted the position of Assistant Postmaster at the Beaman Post Office, having been recommended by his former teacher, Miss Elizabeth Carter, who was distantly related to the Carter family of Marshalltown. The position paid twenty-five dollars per month, plus the privilege of living in the small apartment above the post office. He would later recall that the first letter he ever postmarked was addressed to his older brother William, who had moved to Nebraska the previous year.

On November 28th, 1889, Earl married Victoria Mary Carter, daughter of Benjamin Franklin Carter and Mary Elizabeth Wilson Carter, in a ceremony held at the Beaman Methodist Episcopal Church. The Grundy County Register recorded the marriage as between "Earl R. Davidson, age 19, of Beaman, and Victoria M. Carter, age 18, of Marshalltown." Victoria's dowry consisted of a walnut bedroom suite and a Singer sewing machine, purchased with her savings from three years of school teaching.

Settled Life

The 1900 census found the couple living in a modest frame house at 214 Main Street in Beaman, directly across from the post office. Earl's occupation was listed as "Postmaster, U.S. Government," with an annual income of four hundred and eighty dollars. Victoria was enumerated as "keeping house," though family records indicate she took in sewing and gave piano lessons to local children. Their first child, Robert Earl Davidson Jr., was born on March 15th, 1891, followed by Helen Margaret (1893), Ruth Elizabeth (1895), and the youngest, Ulysses Earl Robert Davidson Jr. (1898).

Unlike his siblings, who scattered to Kansas, Nebraska, and Colorado, Earl remained in Beaman for the entirety of his life. His brothers William and Charles had both moved west by 1890, seeking larger farms and cheaper land. His sister Mary Ellen died in childbirth in 1886, and Sarah Jane relocated to Des Moines with her husband in 1892. Of all the Davidson children, only Earl stayed to work the same soil his father had tilled, though his labor would be of a different sort—sorting mail and keeping the postal accounts rather than plowing fields.

Later Years and Legacy

Earl served as Postmaster until his retirement on January 31st, 1935, at age sixty-five. The Beaman Herald reported that during his forty-seven years of service, he handled approximately 2.3 million pieces of mail and never lost a single registered letter. In retirement, he maintained a small garden behind his home and served as treasurer of the Beaman Methodist Church for twenty-two years.

On January 20th, 1965—his ninety-fifth birthday—Earl suffered a stroke while reading the morning mail. He died peacefully at the Marshalltown Hospital on January 23rd, 1965, with his daughter Helen holding his hand. He was laid to rest beside Victoria in the Beaman Cemetery, having outlived his wife by exactly twenty-two years.

He had lived to see his grandchildren graduate from high school, his great-grandchildren take their first steps, and the family farm—sold by his nephew in 1952—subdivided into housing lots. Of all the Davidson children, only Earl and his sister Anna Mae lived into their nineties. Anna Mae died in 1968 at age ninety-one, leaving Earl the longest-lived of them all, though only by three years.
