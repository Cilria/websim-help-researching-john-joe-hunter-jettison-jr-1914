Protests Erupt Across Southern Uzbekistan Amid Inflation Crisis

By Alisher Mirzayev and Sarah Chen
August 27, 2027, 3:47 PM GMT+5

Tashkent, Uzbekistan — Anti-government protests have spread across 17 towns in southern Uzbekistan, with the epicenter in the Qashqadaryo region, as citizens demonstrate against soaring inflation and deteriorating living conditions that have pushed food prices up by more than 80% since January.

The unrest, which began early Friday morning in the regional capital Qarshi, quickly spread to smaller towns including Kasan, Shahrisabz, and Muborak, marking the largest wave of public demonstrations in the Central Asian nation since the 2022 Karakalpakstan protests.

In Kasan, a town of approximately 25,000 residents located 15 kilometers from the Afghan border, protesters clashed with riot police who responded with tear gas and rubber bullets, according to local witnesses and videos verified by Bloomberg. At least 47 people were hospitalized, including 23 police officers, regional health authorities reported.

"The people have reached their breaking point. When a kilogram of flour costs more than a day's wage, when families can't afford cooking oil, when our children go to bed hungry — this is what happens."
— Matlyuba Karimova, 52, shopkeeper in Qarshi

Inflation Sparks Unrest

Uzbekistan's annual inflation rate surged to 34.7% in July, according to central bank data released last week, driven by rising energy costs, supply chain disruptions, and the government's decision to float the som currency in April. Food inflation has been particularly severe, with bread prices increasing 87% and cooking oil up 112% year-over-year.

"This is not just about inflation anymore," said Dr. Barno Abdullayeva, a political analyst at the University of Tashkent. "The economic crisis has become a catalyst for deeper grievances about corruption, lack of accountability, and the concentration of wealth among government elites."

The protests have taken on an increasingly anti-government tone, with demonstrators chanting slogans that directly challenge official corruption and call for systemic change. Videos circulating on Telegram show crowds chanting in Uzbek: "Qo'rqma, biz hammamiz birdammiz" (Don't fear, we are all united), "Jinoyatchi mansabdorlarga o'lim" (Death to criminal officials), and "Biz och qolyapmiz, jinoyatchilar esa tobora boyib bormoqda!" (We are going hungry, while criminals are getting richer and richer!).

Government Response

The Uzbek government, led by President Shavkat Mirziyoyev since 2016, has deployed thousands of security personnel to the region and imposed temporary internet restrictions in affected areas. State television broadcast a statement from the Interior Ministry blaming "foreign provocateurs" for inciting violence.

"Illegal demonstrations organized by external forces have been contained," the ministry said in a statement. "Law enforcement is working to restore order and protect citizens' rights within the framework of the law."

However, local residents paint a different picture. "They call us foreign agents, but we're just hungry," said 28-year-old English teacher Dilshod Akhmedov, who participated in the Qarshi protests. "My salary hasn't increased in two years, but my rent has tripled. I can't afford meat anymore. Even vegetables are becoming luxury items."

"When government officials drive luxury cars while we can't afford bread, people notice. When they build new mansions while we struggle to pay utilities, people remember. This is what happens when you ignore the people for too long."
— Anonymous protester in Shahrisabz

Economic Pressures Mount

The economic crisis has been exacerbated by several factors: a severe drought that reduced the cotton harvest by 23%, declining natural gas exports to China due to price disputes, and the government's ambitious but costly infrastructure projects that have strained public finances.

Uzbekistan's foreign currency reserves have fallen to $15.2 billion, down from $28.7 billion in early 2025, according to central bank data. The government has been in talks with the IMF for a $3.8 billion stabilization package, but negotiations have stalled over demands for significant structural reforms.

"The government faces an impossible choice," said Timur Ishanov, an economist at the Tashkent School of Economics. "They can continue subsidies that fuel inflation, or remove them and face social unrest. They're trying to find a middle ground, but there's no easy solution."

Regional Implications

The unrest in Uzbekistan comes amid growing economic instability across Central Asia. Kazakhstan has deployed additional security forces to its border with Uzbekistan, while Kyrgyzstan has seen smaller solidarity protests in its southern cities.

"What happens in Uzbekistan matters for the entire region," said Dr. Sophia Petrova, Central Asia analyst at the Carnegie Moscow Center. "If the government can't contain this crisis, we could see a domino effect across the region where people are facing similar economic pressures."

The European Union and United States have called for restraint from all sides. "We urge the Uzbek government to respect the right to peaceful protest and address legitimate economic grievances," the U.S. State Department said in a statement.

Looking Ahead

As protests continue into their third day, the government faces mounting pressure to address both the immediate economic crisis and deeper structural issues. President Mirziyoyev is scheduled to address the nation on Tuesday, where he's expected to announce new economic measures.

"The people have spoken," said Karimova, the Qarshi shopkeeper. "We don't want empty promises anymore. We want real change, real accountability, and real solutions to feed our families. Is that too much to ask?"
