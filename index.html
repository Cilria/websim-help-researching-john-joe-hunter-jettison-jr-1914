South Carolina state senator faces calls to resign after leaked texts advocate return of slavery

By CNN Staff
Updated 3:47 PM ET, Fri July 10, 2026
The South Carolina Statehouse in Columbia, where lawmakers are calling for immediate action against Senator Earl LeRoy Carter Sr.

Columbia, South Carolina (CNN) — South Carolina Republican State Senator Earl LeRoy Carter Sr. is facing mounting calls to resign from both parties after leaked text messages revealed him openly advocating for the return of slavery and calling for women to be stripped of their right to vote.

The texts, obtained by CNN and verified by multiple sources, show Carter, who represents rural Laurens County, making a series of inflammatory statements that have sparked immediate national outrage and calls for his expulsion from the state legislature.

"The Nineteenth Amendment was the greatest mistake America has ever made," Carter wrote in one text message dated June 23, 2026. "Women should be stripped of the right to vote immediately. Their emotional nature makes them unfit for political decisions."

In another message from July 2, Carter allegedly wrote: "Slavery was the natural order of things. The strong ruling the weak. We need to return to this system if we want to make America great again."

Instant backlash

The leaked messages spread rapidly across social media Thursday evening, with #ResignCarter trending nationwide within hours. By Friday morning, protesters had gathered outside the South Carolina Statehouse demanding Carter's immediate resignation.

"This is not just offensive, it's dangerous," said South Carolina Democratic Party Chairwoman Christale Spain during a press conference Friday. "Senator Carter has no place in our government if he believes half of our population shouldn't have basic rights and that human beings should be owned as property."

Republican leaders, including Governor Henry McMaster and Senate Majority Leader A. Shane Massey, quickly distanced themselves from Carter's comments.

"Senator Carter's statements are reprehensible, abhorrent, and have no place in the Republican Party or civilized society," McMaster said in a statement. "He should resign immediately."

Carter stands by comments

Despite the overwhelming backlash, Carter has refused to apologize or step down, instead doubling down on his controversial positions.

"I stand by every word I said," Carter told local news station WLTX Thursday night. "The founding fathers never intended for women to vote, and slavery was a cornerstone of our economy for centuries. These are historical facts, not opinions."

When asked if he understood why his comments were offensive, Carter replied: "The truth is often offensive to those who have been brainwashed by liberal propaganda. I represent the silent majority who are too afraid to speak their minds."

National implications

The scandal has quickly entered national political discourse, with Democratic leaders using it to highlight what they describe as extremism within the Republican Party.

"This is what happens when you normalize radical rhetoric," said Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer on the Senate floor Friday. "When elected officials feel comfortable openly advocating for the return of slavery and stripping away women's rights, we've reached a dangerous tipping point in American politics."

The White House issued a statement Friday afternoon calling Carter's comments "a disgrace to our democracy" and praising bipartisan calls for his resignation.

"President Harris believes that every elected official has a responsibility to uphold the Constitution and protect the rights of all Americans," the statement read. "Senator Carter's views are antithetical to everything our nation stands for."

Local reaction

In Laurens County, where Carter has represented District 10 since 2019, constituents expressed a mixture of shock and embarrassment.

"I'm mortified that this man represents us," said Jennifer Wilson, 42, a lifelong Republican from Laurens. "My grandmother marched for civil rights, and now we've got a senator who wants to bring back slavery? It's 2026, not 1826."

But some residents defended Carter, claiming his comments were taken out of context.

"He speaks his mind, and that's refreshing," said Bob Thompson, 67, a retired textile worker. "Maybe he could have worded things better, but he's right that things were simpler back in the old days."

Legal and political consequences

While Carter cannot be removed from office solely for his comments, South Carolina lawmakers are exploring their options.

"We're examining every legal avenue available to us," said Senate Minority Leader Brad Hutto. "The people of South Carolina deserve representation that reflects our values of equality and justice for all."

Republican Party officials have indicated they will support a primary challenger against Carter in 2028, with some calling for his immediate censure.

"The South Carolina Republican Party condemns in the strongest possible terms the racist and sexist remarks made by Senator Carter," party chairman Drew McKissick said in a statement. "These views are incompatible with our party's principles and values."

Broader context

The controversy comes amid ongoing national debates about voting rights and historical education, with Carter's comments highlighting the deep political divisions in America.

"This isn't just about one senator's offensive comments," said Dr. Karen Fields, professor of political science at the University of South Carolina. "It's about the normalization of extremist views and the erosion of democratic norms that we've taken for granted."

As of Friday afternoon, Carter remained defiant, posting on social media: "The establishment wants me silenced because I speak uncomfortable truths. I will not be intimidated by the liberal mob."

Protests are expected to continue throughout the weekend, with civil rights organizations planning a major demonstration at the Statehouse on Saturday.
