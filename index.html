Persian meme claiming Iraq 'rightfully belongs to Iran' sparks furious backlash across Arab world

By BBC News Middle East Correspondent
10 July 2027
The meme, which circulated widely on Persian social media, showed altered borders with the caption "Just imagine: better food, nicer looking borders"

Tehran, Iran — A light-hearted social media meme posted by Persian users suggesting Iraq should be part of Iran has ignited a firestorm of controversy across the Arab world, with religious leaders and politicians denouncing what they call "Persian expansionist fantasies."

The meme, which began circulating on Persian-language social media platforms last week, featured a map showing Iraq as part of Iran with the tongue-in-cheek caption: "Just imagine: you'd get better food and the borders would look so much nicer."

What started as online humour quickly escalated into a diplomatic incident, with Arab ultranationalists responding with furious condemnation and religious leaders delivering fiery sermons against what they term "Persian arrogance."

'Traitors to Islam'

In Baghdad's famous Abu Hanifa Mosque, Imam Abdul Rahman al-Samarrai devoted his Friday sermon to what he called "the latest Persian insult to Arab dignity and Islamic unity."

"These Persians, who speak of taking our lands, are traitors to the Islamic world and to Allah himself. They speak of better food while our people suffer, they joke about borders while our blood waters this soil."
— Imam Abdul Rahman al-Samarrai

The sermon, which was broadcast live and quickly amassed millions of views across Arab social media, continued: "For 1,400 years, we have been brothers in Islam, and now they speak of us as if we are mere provinces for their empire."

Social media storm

The original Persian meme was created by a Tehran-based graphic designer who goes by the handle @TehranTroll. Speaking to the BBC via Instagram direct message, they expressed bewilderment at the reaction.

"It was just a joke about food," they wrote. "Everyone knows Iranian cuisine is better than Iraqi food. That's all. We weren't making political statements about borders or territory."

But the joke was lost in translation, literally. When Arabic social media users began sharing screenshots of the meme, the caption was often mistranslated as "Iraq rightfully belongs to Iran" rather than the more playful original Persian.

The hashtag #PersianExpansionism began trending across Arab countries, accompanied by equally inflammatory memes showing historical maps of the Arab conquest of Persia and references to the 7th century defeat of the Sassanian Empire.

Political reactions

Iraqi MP Ahmed al-Jubouri called for an official Iranian apology, telling Iraq's Al-Sumaria television: "The Persians must understand that Iraq is not a joke. Our sovereignty is not a meme for their entertainment."

In Tehran, Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesman Hassan Firouzabadi attempted to defuse tensions, telling reporters: "Social media posts do not reflect government policy. Iran respects Iraq's sovereignty and territorial integrity."

But the damage was already done. By Friday evening, protests had erupted in several Iraqi cities, with demonstrators burning Iranian flags and chanting anti-Persian slogans.

'Better food' controversy

Perhaps surprisingly, the aspect of the meme that seemed to cause the most offense was the claim about "better food."

"This is cultural arrogance," said Baghdad resident and food blogger Noor al-Baghdadi. "They speak of our cuisine as if it's inferior? Our masgouf fish, our dolma, our kleicha cookies? Persian food is just rice with everything!"

The food angle sparked a wave of culinary nationalism across the Arab world, with social media users posting pictures of Iraqi dishes under the hashtag #IraqiCuisineIsSuperior.

Even the official Twitter accounts of Iraqi restaurants got involved, with one popular Baghdad eatery posting: "We don't need Persian spices to make delicious food. We have our own 5,000-year-old culinary tradition."

Historical sensitivities

Dr. Rania Khalek, a professor of Middle Eastern history at the University of Edinburgh, told the BBC that the meme touched on deep historical sensitivities.

"There's a long history of Persian-Arab rivalry that predates Islam," she explained. "Both civilizations have viewed themselves as superior to the other at various points in history. What seems like a silly meme actually taps into centuries of cultural competition."

The Iran-Iraq War of 1980-1988, which cost over a million lives, still looms large in the collective memory of both nations. Many Iraqis view Iran with suspicion, while some Iranians still harbor territorial ambitions dating back to the pre-Islamic Persian Empire.

Attempting to de-escalate

By Saturday morning, some Persian social media users were attempting to calm the situation. The hashtag #WeLoveIraqiFood began trending in Iran, with users posting pictures of themselves eating Iraqi dishes and praising Iraqi culture.

One popular post showed an Iranian family gathered around a table of Iraqi food with the caption: "Our Iraqi brothers, we love your cuisine! It was just a joke about rice, please forgive us!"

But the attempts at reconciliation were met with mixed reactions. While some Iraqi social media users appreciated the gesture, others remained skeptical.

"They insult us, then they think posting pictures of our food will make it better?" one Iraqi Twitter user responded. "This is not about food. This is about respect."

As the controversy continues to unfold, both governments are working behind the scenes to prevent further escalation. Iranian officials have reportedly reached out to their Iraqi counterparts to emphasize that the meme does not reflect official policy.

But in the age of social media, where a joke can travel faster than diplomacy, the incident serves as a reminder of how easily cultural misunderstandings can spiral into international incidents.

"Sometimes a meme is just a meme," said Dr. Khalek. "But sometimes it's also about history, politics, and identity. In the Middle East, it's always about all of those things."
