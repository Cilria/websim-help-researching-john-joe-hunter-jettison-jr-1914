Airbus explores A350neo and new flagship aircraft, sources say

By Tim Hepher, Joanna Plucinska
November 20, 2027 2:17 AM EST
An Airbus A350 under assembly at the company's facility in Toulouse, France

PARIS (Reuters) - Airbus SE is in the early stages of developing a re-engined version of its A350 wide-body jet and studying a larger new flagship aircraft to compete with Boeing's next-generation wide-body planes, three people familiar with the matter told Reuters.

The European planemaker has launched preliminary studies for the A350neo, which would feature more efficient engines and aerodynamic improvements, while also exploring a clean-sheet design internally dubbed "New Large Aircraft," the sources said.

Both projects are in their infancy and may not lead to formal program launches, but they signal Airbus's intent to maintain its competitive edge in the lucrative wide-body market, where it faces renewed competition from Boeing, the sources added.

New engines, new possibilities

The A350neo would incorporate next-generation engines from either Rolls-Royce or a potential new offering from CFM International, the sources said. The re-engining could deliver fuel savings of 10-15% compared with current A350 models, making it attractive for airlines facing rising fuel costs and sustainability pressures.

"Airbus is actively looking at how to keep the A350 competitive through the 2030s," one of the sources said. "A re-engine is the most straightforward path, but timing will depend on engine availability and market demand."

The "New Large Aircraft" project represents a more ambitious undertaking, potentially positioning Airbus to compete directly with Boeing's rumored new mid-market airplane and any successor to the 777X family, industry analysts said.

Market positioning

The studies come as the aviation industry recovers from the COVID-19 pandemic and faces increasing pressure to reduce emissions. Airlines are demanding more efficient aircraft to meet environmental targets and reduce operating costs.

"The sweet spot for wide-body aircraft appears to be shifting," said Richard Aboulafia, aerospace analyst at AeroDynamic Advisory. "Airbus needs to position itself for where the market is heading, not where it is today."

An Airbus spokeswoman declined to comment on future product development, saying the company continuously explores technologies to improve its aircraft.

Engine technology race

The engine suppliers are developing new technologies including open-fan designs and hybrid-electric systems that could deliver significant efficiency gains. Rolls-Royce is working on its UltraFan technology, while CFM is developing advanced engine concepts under its RISE program.

"The timing of any A350neo launch would likely align with when these new engine technologies are ready for commercial application," another source said.

Airbus has delivered more than 700 A350s since the aircraft entered service in 2015. The jet competes with Boeing's 787 Dreamliner and 777 models in the wide-body market, which is crucial for long-haul international travel.

Development timeline

If launched, the A350neo could enter service in the early 2030s, while any entirely new aircraft program would likely take until the mid-2030s to reach airlines, the sources said.

"These are very preliminary studies," one source cautioned. "The industry is still recovering, and Airbus will be careful about committing to major new programs until the market stabilizes."

The "New Large Aircraft" project is expected to incorporate advanced materials, artificial intelligence systems, and potentially hydrogen propulsion technology, depending on how requirements develop, the sources added.

Airbus is also studying how any new aircraft would fit into its existing production system and supply chain, which has been strained by recent global disruptions, another source said.

"The challenge isn't just technical - it's about market timing, production capacity, and ensuring there's sufficient demand to justify the massive investment," the source added.
