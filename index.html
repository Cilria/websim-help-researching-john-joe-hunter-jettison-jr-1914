Oklahoma Sen. Markwayne Mullin attacked during town hall meeting; 64-year-old suspect shouted about job loss

By CNN Staff
Updated 5:32 PM ET, Wed March 11, 2026
Sen. Markwayne Mullin was attacked during a town hall meeting in Oklahoma on Wednesday. (File photo)

Washington (CNN) — Sen. Markwayne Mullin, R-Oklahoma, was attacked during a town hall meeting on Wednesday afternoon by a man who shouted profanities and blamed the senator for his job loss before physically assaulting him, according to witnesses and law enforcement officials.

The incident occurred around 2:30 p.m. local time at a community center in Tulsa, where Mullin was hosting a regularly scheduled town hall event with constituents. The suspect, identified by police as 64-year-old Kenneth Monroe Jackson Sr., approached the senator while he was taking questions from the audience.

According to multiple witnesses, Jackson stood up and shouted, "Fuck you, coward! You made me lose my job!" before charging toward the senator and striking him multiple times. Witnesses reported hearing Jackson shout "Worst fucking senator, burn in hell" during the attack.

"It happened so fast," said Jennifer Martinez, who was attending the town hall. "One moment the senator was answering a question about infrastructure, and the next moment this man was on top of him, just swinging."

Swift intervention

Security personnel and several attendees quickly intervened, pulling Jackson away from Mullin. The senator sustained minor injuries and was examined by medical personnel at the scene, according to his office.

"Senator Mullin is doing fine and is grateful for the quick actions of security and first responders," said Mullin's communications director, Sarah Thompson. "He will continue with his scheduled events today as planned."

Jackson was immediately taken into custody by Tulsa police and charged with assault on a federal official. He is being held at the Tulsa County Jail and is expected to appear in federal court on Thursday.

Investigation underway

Federal authorities are working to determine Jackson's motive and whether he has any previous history with the senator or his office. Investigators are reviewing his background and any potential connections to extremist groups or previous threats.

"At this time, we are treating this as an isolated incident," said FBI Special Agent in Charge Robert Turner. "We are working with our partners at the U.S. Capitol Police and local law enforcement to conduct a thorough investigation."

According to public records, Jackson is a resident of Broken Arrow, Oklahoma, and has worked in various manufacturing and construction jobs throughout his career. Investigators are examining his employment history to determine what job loss he may have been referring to during the attack.

Increased security concerns

The incident highlights ongoing security concerns for members of Congress, who have faced increasing threats and harassment in recent years. The U.S. Capitol Police reported a significant increase in threats against lawmakers in 2025.

"Unfortunately, this is the reality we live in now," said former Capitol Police Chief Tom Manger. "Members of Congress are facing more threats than ever before, and town halls have become particularly volatile venues."

Mullin, a former mixed martial arts fighter and business owner, was first elected to the Senate in 2022. He has been an outspoken conservative voice on issues including energy policy and border security.

Constituent reactions

Following the attack, several attendees expressed shock at the violence that erupted during what is typically a peaceful democratic exercise.

"I've been coming to these town halls for years, and I've never seen anything like this," said longtime Tulsa resident Barbara Johnson, 72. "Whatever your politics, this kind of violence has no place in our democracy."

Oklahoma Governor Kevin Stitt issued a statement condemning the attack, saying, "There is no place for violence in our political discourse. I am relieved that Senator Mullin was not seriously injured and commend law enforcement for their swift response."

The incident prompted other Oklahoma lawmakers to review their security protocols for public events. Rep. Stephanie Bice told CNN that her office would be "evaluating all upcoming public events to ensure the safety of constituents and staff."

Ongoing investigation

Federal investigators are continuing to interview witnesses and review security footage from the community center. They are also examining Jackson's social media activity and any communications he may have had with Mullin's office.

Jackson faces up to 20 years in prison if convicted of assault on a federal official. Additional charges may be filed as the investigation continues.

Mullin's office said the senator plans to continue holding town halls and meeting with constituents, though they are reviewing security protocols for future events.

"Senator Mullin believes strongly in the importance of remaining accessible to Oklahomans and will not be intimidated by this incident," Thompson said.
