Rise of China's 'Party of National Revenge': How post-war humiliation fuels a new fascist movement

By CNN Staff
Updated 11:23 AM ET, Mon November 13, 2038
Zhang Shengmin, leader of the 'Party of National Revenge,' addresses thousands of supporters in Chongqing, November 2038.

Chongqing, China (CNN) — In the shadow of China's devastating defeat in World War III, a dangerous new political movement is rapidly gaining traction across the country, raising alarms among regional analysts who warn it could plunge East Asia into further instability.

The "和谐国家复兴时刻" — translated as "The Moment of National Revival in a Harmonious Nation" but commonly known as the "复仇党" or "Party of National Revenge" — has emerged as perhaps the most significant political force in post-war China. Led by former People's Liberation Army General Zhang Shengmin, the movement has attracted millions of followers with a message that combines ultra-nationalism, racial supremacy, and a call for military expansion that experts say bears troubling similarities to 1930s European fascism.

"What we're witnessing is the emergence of a genuinely fascist movement in the world's most populous country," says Dr. Sarah Chen, East Asia specialist at Georgetown University. "The Party of National Revenge isn't just a political movement — it's a complete ideological system built on racial superiority, militarism, and the belief that China has a divine right to rule the world."

The myth of betrayal

The movement's core narrative centers on what Zhang calls the "Great Betrayal" — the belief that China didn't lose the war due to military defeat, but because of internal enemies who conspired with Western powers to destroy the nation from within.

"The Communists, the Tibetans, the Uyghurs — they are the national poison that poisoned our great nation," Zhang declared during a recent rally in Chongqing that drew an estimated 200,000 supporters. "They worked with the foreign devils to ensure our defeat, to unleash upon us a Second Century of Humiliation worse than the first."

This narrative has found fertile ground among a population reeling from the war's aftermath. Under the terms of the Treaty of Tokyo, China was forced to renounce all claims to Taiwan, cede Korean-majority territories to a unified Korea, place Tibet under Indian military supervision, and pay massive reparations to NATO and other coalition members.

"The psychological impact of defeat cannot be overstated," explains Professor Liu Wei of the University of Hong Kong. "For decades, the Chinese people were told they were reclaiming their rightful place as the world's leading power. The sudden reversal has created a national trauma that extremist movements are now exploiting."

A general's revenge

Zhang Shengmin, 61, emerged from relative obscurity to become the movement's charismatic leader. As a former PLA general who served in the Taiwan campaign, he was dismissed from military service following the coup that overthrew the Communist government in Beijing.

"The cowardly Communists refused to fight to the end," Zhang told supporters in a recent speech. "They surrendered when victory was still possible, betraying the Han people and our destiny to rule not just Asia, but the entire world."

The movement's ideology, outlined in Zhang's 400-page manifesto "The Path of National Revenge," combines traditional Chinese concepts of tianxia (all under heaven) with modern fascist rhetoric. It proclaims Han Chinese as the "master race" of humanity and calls for the establishment of a military government that would prioritize war preparation above all else.

"War is not something to be feared, but a glorious ordeal that purifies the nation," the manifesto states. "Peace is for the weak. Only through constant struggle and expansion can China fulfill its destiny."

Growing support

Since its founding in August, the Party of National Revenge has established branches in every major Chinese city and claims a membership of over 12 million, though independent analysts put the number closer to 8 million. More concerning, the movement has attracted significant support within the military and security services.

"We're seeing former military officers, intelligence officials, and even some current government officials attending Party of National Revenge rallies," says Dr. Michael Park, senior fellow at the Carnegie Endowment. "The movement has effectively become a parallel power structure in many parts of the country."

The party's paramilitary wing, the "National Revenge Corps," has been spotted conducting military-style drills in rural areas, though they claim to be merely a "self-defense organization" protecting Chinese sovereignty.

International concerns

The rise of the Party of National Revenge has triggered alarm bells across Asia and beyond. Japanese Defense Minister Tanaka Hiroshi called the movement "the most serious threat to regional stability since the end of World War III," while Indian Prime Minister Mehta warned of "the dangers of revanchist nationalism in a nuclear-armed state."

"If this movement gains state power, we could see China pursuing a policy of military expansion that would make the previous regime look moderate by comparison," warns Dr. Chen. "Zhang has explicitly called for the reconquest of Taiwan, Tibet, and even territories that have never been part of China."

The United States has responded cautiously, with State Department spokesperson Jennifer Rodriguez calling for "restraint and respect for the international order" while stopping short of direct criticism of the movement.

The appeal of revenge

At Party of National Revenge rallies, supporters speak of humiliation, betrayal, and the need for revenge against both internal and external enemies. Many wear traditional Hanfu clothing and carry banners proclaiming "The Han Race Shall Rule the World" and "Death to the Traitors."

"The Communists sold us out to the Americans," says Li Wei, a 34-year-old former factory worker who lost his job after the war. "Zhang understands that China needs strong leadership, not weakness and compromise."

The movement has particularly strong appeal among young men, with party organizers claiming that 70% of their members are under 35. University campuses have become recruiting grounds, with party cells operating openly at major institutions.

"They tell us that our generation has been chosen to restore Chinese greatness," says Chen Ming, a 22-year-old student at Shanghai University. "That we must dedicate our lives to the nation and prepare for the ultimate struggle."

Uncertain future

As China struggles to rebuild its economy and political system after the war, the Party of National Revenge represents both a symptom of national trauma and a potential threat to regional stability. With elections scheduled for 2040 under the new constitution, many analysts expect the movement to make significant gains.

"The question isn't whether the Party of National Revenge will gain power, but when," says Professor Liu. "And when they do, we can expect a much more aggressive, militaristic China that seeks to overturn the post-war settlement and assert dominance over Asia."

For Zhang and his supporters, the path forward is clear: revenge against those who betrayed China, restoration of lost territories, and ultimately, Chinese domination of the world. Whether they can achieve these goals remains to be seen, but the movement's rapid growth suggests that Chinese politics has entered a dangerous new phase.

"The Han people are the master race of this world," Zhang declared at a recent rally. "It is our destiny to rule. The century of humiliation is over. The century of revenge has begun."
