US launches airstrikes against Islamic Caliphate as West Africa offensive intensifies; Trump announces 'military operations have begun'

By CNN Staff
Updated 9:47 PM ET, Thu March 25, 2027
A US airstrike targets Islamic Caliphate positions in Niger, part of a coordinated international response to the militant group's rapid expansion across West Africa.

Washington (CNN) — The United States has launched airstrikes against Islamic Caliphate positions across West Africa, President Trump announced Thursday evening, as the militant group continues its lightning offensive that has already seized control of six countries and displaced over 15 million people.

"Military operations have begun against the Islamic Caliphate," Trump posted on Truth Social at 8:23 PM. "We will not allow these terrorists to threaten American interests and our allies. The Caliphate will be destroyed."

The announcement came as Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth confirmed that American warplanes had struck "multiple Caliphate targets" in Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso, targeting what he described as "command and control centers, weapons depots, and terrorist training facilities."

France and the United Kingdom quickly followed suit, with French President Emmanuel Macron announcing "Opération Liberté" and British Prime Minister Rishi Sunak confirming RAF participation in what officials are calling "Operation Desert Thunder."

International coalition forms

The coordinated strikes represent the most significant Western military intervention in Africa since the 2011 Libyan intervention. Pentagon officials said the initial wave included over 200 cruise missiles launched from US naval vessels in the Atlantic, followed by sustained bombing campaigns from strategic bombers based in Diego Garcia.

"We are targeting the Caliphate's military infrastructure with precision strikes," Hegseth said during a brief Pentagon briefing. "Our goal is to degrade their ability to continue their offensive and protect civilian populations."

French officials reported their aircraft had struck Caliphate positions in Senegal and Ivory Coast, while British forces focused on targets in northern Nigeria. The coalition appears to be operating under a divide-and-conquer strategy, with each nation taking responsibility for different geographic sectors.

Caliphate advances continue despite strikes

Despite the international military response, Caliphate forces have continued their offensive operations. Early Thursday, Islamist fighters began besieging Kaduna, Nigeria's third-largest city located just 50 kilometers from the capital Abuja.

"The Caliphate is at our doorstep," Nigerian President Bola Tinubu said in an emergency address to the nation. "We are fighting for our very existence as a nation."

Witnesses in Kaduna report heavy fighting as government forces attempt to hold the strategic city. Over 2 million civilians have fled southward toward Lagos and Port Harcourt, creating what aid agencies describe as "the largest humanitarian exodus in modern African history."

Simultaneously, Caliphate forces captured Korhogo in northern Ivory Coast after a 36-hour battle, bringing them within 200 kilometers of Abidjan, the country's economic capital. The fall of Korhogo opens a direct path to Ghana's border, threatening to expand the conflict into yet another nation.

Global operations launched

Beyond the military strikes, US officials announced "Operation Global Shield" - a worldwide effort to dismantle the Caliphate's international network. FBI Director Kash Patel revealed that simultaneous raids were conducted across multiple countries targeting suspected Caliphate supporters and financiers.

"We've arrested 47 individuals in 12 countries who were providing material support to the Caliphate," Patel said. "This includes bank executives, arms dealers, and social media influencers who were radicalizing followers."

The Treasury Department froze over $2.3 billion in assets linked to Caliphate financing, while cyber operations targeted the group's online recruitment and propaganda networks. Officials said the Caliphate's primary Twitter account, which had over 4 million followers, was permanently suspended.

Escalating humanitarian crisis

The military intervention comes as the humanitarian situation across West Africa reaches catastrophic proportions. The UN estimates that over 18 million people have been displaced by the fighting, with refugee camps in Ghana and Ivory Coast overwhelmed beyond capacity.

"We're seeing 100,000 new refugees every day," said UN Emergency Relief Coordinator Tom Fletcher. "At this rate, we'll have 30 million displaced people within a week. We're witnessing the collapse of an entire region."

In the besieged cities, conditions are deteriorating rapidly. In Kaduna, water and electricity have been cut off for three days, while food supplies dwindle. Similar scenes are reported in Bamako, Niamey, and other major cities under Caliphate control.

International markets panic

The military intervention and continued Caliphate advances have triggered panic in international markets. Oil prices surged 23% to $147 per barrel, while European stock markets fell 8% on fears of uranium supply disruptions. The French CAC 40 index dropped 11% as investors worried about the country's military commitments.

"We're looking at a potential global recession if this conflict continues," said Maria Rodriguez, chief economist at Goldman Sachs. "The Caliphate now controls 18% of global uranium production and significant oil reserves. This is an economic earthquake."

What comes next

US officials indicated the airstrikes were "just the beginning" of a sustained campaign. Hegseth said additional ground forces were being positioned in Ghana and Senegal for potential deployment, while the aircraft carrier USS Gerald R. Ford was moving into position off the West African coast.

"We are prepared for a long-term operation," Hegseth said. "This will not be resolved quickly. The Caliphate has had months to prepare defensive positions, and they are well-equipped with captured military equipment."

Intelligence sources suggest the Caliphate has dispersed its forces among civilian populations, making precision targeting increasingly difficult. There are growing concerns about civilian casualties, with reports of at least 47 deaths from the initial wave of airstrikes.

As night falls across West Africa, the situation remains fluid. While Western powers have entered the conflict, the Caliphate's momentum shows no signs of slowing. With Kaduna under siege and millions fleeing southward, the region faces what many experts describe as its darkest hour.
