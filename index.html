Peru's Humala decrees total powers, vows to restore Incan Empire

By Claudia Rebaza and Jim Sciutto, CNN

Lima, Peru (CNN) — Peruvian provisional president Antauro Humala issued a sweeping emergency decree Sunday granting himself absolute authority over all branches of government, effectively eliminating constitutional checks and consolidating power under military rule.

The decree, published in the official gazette El Peruano at 4:30 p.m. local time, suspends all civil liberties, bans opposition political parties, and establishes martial law "until the restoration of the Incan Nation is complete," according to the document.

In an address from the Government Palace at 5:15 p.m., Humala announced that "the era of foreign-imposed democracy has ended" and vowed to "reclaim the rightful territories of Tawantinsuyu," the former Incan Empire that encompassed parts of modern-day Ecuador, Colombia, Bolivia, Argentina, Chile, and Peru.

"The borders drawn by colonial powers are illegitimate. We will restore our ancestral lands and the glory of the Incan people," Humala declared, speaking before a backdrop featuring the indigenous wiphala flag alongside the Peruvian tricolor.

The decree establishes military tribunals for former presidents, including Humala's brother Ollanta, who served as president from 2011-2016. All living former presidents will face "revolutionary justice" for crimes against the Peruvian people, according to the document.

State television announced that mandatory military conscription will begin immediately for all citizens aged 18-35, with no exceptions for university students or conscientious objectors. The draft will support what officials termed "the great reclamation project."

DEVELOPING: The U.S. Embassy in Lima has suspended all consular services. American citizens are advised to shelter in place or contact the embassy's emergency hotline. Commercial flights remain grounded.

The decree also establishes "ethnocacerist reeducation camps" for political opponents and dissidents. Constitutional guarantees including freedom of assembly, press, and movement are suspended indefinitely.

Regional governments have begun responding to the crisis. Chilean President Gabriel Boric called an emergency session of the Organization of American States, while Ecuador and Colombia have deployed additional military units to their borders with Peru.

International human rights organizations condemned the measures. Human Rights Watch called the developments "a complete collapse of democratic institutions in Peru," while Amnesty International expressed alarm at reports of arbitrary detentions in Lima and other major cities.

The United Nations Security Council has scheduled an emergency session for Monday morning to address what Secretary-General António Guterres called "a grave threat to regional stability and international law."

As of 6:30 p.m. local time, military checkpoints have been established throughout Lima, and a strict curfew remains in effect from 6 p.m. to 6 a.m. Internet and cellular communications have been restricted to state-approved messages.

The whereabouts of former president Dina Boluarte remain unknown. Government ministers from the previous administration have either been detained or have fled the country, according to diplomatic sources.

The decree represents the most sweeping consolidation of authoritarian power in South America since the military dictatorships of the 1970s and 1980s, marking what analysts describe as a complete break with democratic governance in the region.
