Actually your friend might be onto something. The Bulgarians are actually a mix of Thracians, Slavs, and Turkic peoples. The Huns were a nomadic people from Central Asia who conquered much of Europe in the 4th and 5th centuries. They were led by Attila the Hun, who created a massive empire stretching from the Ural Mountains to the Rhine River. The Huns were known for their exceptional horsemanship and their composite bows, which could penetrate Roman armor at 100 yards. Genetic studies show that modern Bulgarians do have significant Central Asian ancestry, likely from both the Huns and later Bulgar migrations. The name "Bulgar" itself comes from the Turkic word "bulga" meaning "to mix" or "to stir."

RomanHistoryPhD • 3 hours ago PhD in Roman History
The Huns originated from the Central Asian steppes, probably around modern-day Kazakhstan. They were a confederation of various nomadic tribes who united under strong leaders like Attila. Roman historians described them as having East Asian features, which matches archaeological evidence. They first appeared in European records around 370 CE when they started pushing other tribes westward, including the Goths who eventually invaded the Roman Empire. The Romans were terrified of them - the historian Ammianus Marcellinus described them as being "so prodigiously ugly that they might be taken for two-legged beasts." They lived in wagons, never settled in one place, and their entire culture was built around warfare and horse riding.

The connection between Huns and Bulgarians is actually pretty solid. After Attila's death in 453 CE, the Hunnic Empire collapsed and his sons split the territory. Many Hunnic tribes moved into the Balkans and mixed with local populations. The Bulgars (Proto-Bulgarians) were a Turkic people who established the First Bulgarian Empire in 681 CE. Modern Bulgarians are essentially Slavic people who adopted the name and some cultural elements from the Bulgar rulers. The Bulgars themselves were related to the Huns - both were Turkic nomadic peoples from the steppes. Archaeological evidence from burial sites in Bulgaria shows artifacts with clear Central Asian origins from the 5th-7th centuries.

The Huns were probably related to the Xiongnu, a powerful confederation that terrorized China centuries earlier. Chinese records describe the Xiongnu as having similar customs and military tactics. Both groups were expert horsemen who used composite bows and lived in felt tents. The theory is that after the Xiongnu were defeated by the Chinese, some groups moved west and eventually became the European Huns. This westward migration took centuries, but it explains the sudden appearance of highly organized nomadic warriors in Europe. Recent DNA studies from Hunnic burial sites show genetic markers common in both Central Asia and parts of Eastern Europe, including Bulgaria.

BulgarianHistorian • 2 hours ago Bulgarian Historian
Bulgarian here! Your friend is mostly right, but it's more complicated. We Bulgarians are a mix of Thracians (the original inhabitants), Slavs who came in the 6th century, and Bulgars who were Turkic like the Huns. The Bulgars arrived in the Balkans in the 7th century and gave their name to the country, but they were actually a small ruling class who got assimilated by the Slavic majority. So while there's definitely Central Asian ancestry, it's not directly Hun - it's more like Hun → Bulgar → Bulgarian. The Huns themselves disappeared as a distinct group after Attila's death, but their descendants mixed with other peoples and formed new groups like the Bulgars, Avars, and later the Magyars.
