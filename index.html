Islamist militants encircle Ouagadougou as siege of Burkina Faso capital begins

By CNN Staff
Updated 6:23 PM ET, Mon March 1, 2027
Smoke rises over Ouagadougou as Islamist militants advance on the capital Monday.

Ouagadougou, Burkina Faso (CNN) — Islamist militants have encircled Burkina Faso's capital Ouagadougou and begun what appears to be a coordinated siege of the city, following weeks of rapid territorial gains across the West African nation, according to government officials and witnesses on the ground.

Heavy fighting broke out early Monday morning as jihadist fighters from multiple armed groups converged on the capital from multiple directions, cutting off main supply routes and positioning themselves for what analysts say could be a prolonged assault on one of West Africa's last major cities still under government control.

"The enemy has surrounded the capital and is attempting to breach our defenses," Capt. Ibrahim Traoré, Burkina Faso's military leader, said in a televised address Monday afternoon. "We are prepared to defend our capital and our sovereignty with everything we have."

The militants, who have seized control of roughly 80% of the country over the past two years, began their final push toward the capital late Sunday night, overwhelming military checkpoints and establishing positions within mortar range of key government buildings, according to multiple sources inside the city.

Multiple fronts under assault

Fighting has been reported on at least four separate approaches to the city, with the heaviest clashes occurring along the main highway from Bobo-Dioulasso, Burkina Faso's second-largest city, which fell to Islamist control in January.

"The sound of heavy weapons fire has been constant since dawn," said Amadou Compaoré, a resident of the city's eastern sector, via phone. "We can see smoke rising from the direction of the airport, and military vehicles are racing through the streets."

The city's main airport suspended operations Monday morning after reports of fighting in nearby suburbs, effectively cutting off one of the last remaining evacuation routes for foreign nationals and diplomats.

French and American diplomatic sources confirmed to CNN that their embassies have begun emergency evacuation procedures for non-essential staff, though both missions remain operational for now.

Questions over leadership decisions

Multiple military analysts and former government officials have questioned whether the current military junta, led by Capt. Traoré, adequately prepared for the capital's defense despite clear warning signs over the past six months.

"The writing has been on the wall since the fall of Koudougou in December," said Dr. Marie Konaté, former defense minister and now professor at the University of Ouagadougou. "The junta refused multiple opportunities to negotiate with regional powers, rejected French military assistance, and appears to have dismissed advice from regional security experts about fortifying the capital."

According to three separate sources within the military command structure, Traoré has reportedly rejected multiple recommendations from senior officers regarding defensive strategies and has instead chosen to maintain an aggressive forward defense posture that has left the capital vulnerable.

"The captain has refused to listen to anyone who disagrees with his assessment that we can hold the line at the city's edge," said one senior military official, speaking on condition of anonymity. "Now we are paying the price for that hubris."

Humanitarian crisis deepens

Inside the capital, panic has set in among the civilian population. Markets have closed, banks have shut their doors, and long lines have formed at remaining grocery stores as residents stockpile supplies for what could be a prolonged siege.

"There is no bread, no rice, no oil left in the shops," said Awa Sawadogo, a mother of four in the city's central district. "People are fighting over the last bags of millet. We don't know how long we can survive like this."

The United Nations estimates that over 2.3 million people have been displaced by the two-year Islamist insurgency, with more than 800,000 now believed to be trapped in the Ouagadougou metropolitan area.

"We are looking at a potential humanitarian catastrophe," said Jean-Pierre Lacroix, UN Under-Secretary-General for Peacekeeping Operations, speaking from New York. "If the capital falls, we could see mass displacement on a scale not witnessed in West Africa since the Liberian civil war."

Regional implications

The siege of Ouagadougou represents the most significant threat to regional stability since the Islamist takeover of northern Mali in 2012. Burkina Faso's strategic position between Mali, Niger, and coastal West African nations makes its potential fall a major concern for the entire region.

"If Ouagadougou falls, we are looking at the effective collapse of state authority across much of the Sahel," said General Abdoulaye Coulibaly (retired), former chief of staff of the Malian army. "This would give Islamist groups control over a contiguous territory stretching from Mauritania to northern Nigeria."

ECOWAS, the West African regional bloc, has called for an emergency summit Tuesday to discuss potential intervention options, though any such operation would face significant logistical challenges given the speed of the Islamist advance.

"Time is not on our side," said Omar Touray, president of the ECOWAS Commission. "We need to act decisively, but we also need to be realistic about what is possible given the current military situation on the ground."

Uncertain future

As night fell over Ouagadougou Monday evening, the sound of sporadic gunfire continued to echo through the city's suburbs. Residents reported seeing military vehicles positioning themselves around key government buildings, while others described seeing Islamist fighters establishing checkpoints on major roads leading out of the city.

"The city has not yet fallen," said one Western diplomat, speaking on condition of anonymity. "But the siege has definitely begun. What happens next will determine not just the future of Burkina Faso, but potentially the stability of the entire West African region."

For now, the capital remains under government control, but with supply lines cut and no clear path to reinforcement, analysts say the situation appears increasingly dire for the estimated 2.8 million people trapped inside the encircled city.
