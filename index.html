SteppeArchaeologist • 2 hours ago Archaeologist
Your friend is onto something, but it's complicated. The Huns were a confederation of Central Asian steppe tribes that coalesced around 370 CE. They originated from the Eurasian steppes, probably from the region of what is now Kazakhstan and Mongolia. Think of them as a multi-ethnic confederation, not a single ethnic group. The Huns were the original "punk rockers" of steppe warfare – they showed up, terrified everyone with their horse archery and crazy fighting style, then disappeared just as quickly. The archaeological evidence shows they were a mix of different steppe peoples who united under charismatic leaders like Attila. They didn't have a single ethnic identity. The genetic evidence is fascinating. The Huns themselves were probably a mix of East Asian and Central Asian peoples. When they moved west, they absorbed Germanic, Iranian, and Slavic groups. By the time they reached the Roman frontier, they were already genetically diverse. When the Hun empire collapsed in 469 CE, these groups scattered across Europe and the Balkans. Some definitely ended up in the Balkans and mixed with local populations. So your friend isn't entirely wrong – there might be some Hun DNA in modern Bulgarians, but it's not a direct line of descent. The real answer is that both Bulgarians and Hungarians have some Hun ancestry, but neither is the "real" descendant of the Huns.

Academic here who specializes in the Huns. The Huns were the ultimate "we came, we saw, we terrorized, we disappeared" people. They emerged from the Central Asian steppes around 370 CE and within a generation had the Roman Empire paying them tribute. They were masters of psychological warfare – the Romans were absolutely terrified of them, partly because they looked different (Asiatic features) and fought differently (horse archery, no infantry formations). Here's what we know about their origins: they probably originated from the Xiongnu confederation in what is now Mongolia. The Xiongnu were the predecessors of the Huns. When the Xiongnu empire collapsed in the 2nd century CE, some groups moved west towards Europe. These western Xiongnu evolved into what we call the Huns. The Hun language is a total mystery. We have maybe 3 words that are definitely Hun, and they're all names. We don't know what language family they spoke. Some scholars think it was related to Turkic, others think it was something completely different. They were illiterate, so they left no written records. Everything we know comes from their enemies, which is like trying to understand the French Revolution only through British propaganda. And yes, they just disappeared. After Attila died in 453 CE, his sons fought each other, and the Hun empire fragmented into competing factions. Within 15 years, the Huns had vanished as a political force. Some merged with other groups, some returned to the steppes, some were absorbed by local populations.

Your friend's claim is actually pretty common in Bulgaria, but it's based on a misunderstanding of history. The Bulgars were a Turkic people who established the First Bulgarian Empire in 681 CE. The Huns were a different group who existed 300 years earlier. The Bulgars were part of the Hun confederation for a while, but they were definitely not the same group. The confusion comes from the fact that the Bulgars were sometimes called "Huns" by Byzantine historians who didn't know the difference between different steppe peoples. This is like calling all East Asians "Chinese" – it's just lazy categorization. The Bulgars had their own language, customs, and political structure. They were Turkic-speaking, while the Huns probably spoke something else entirely. The truth is that the Bulgars were one of many steppe peoples who passed through the region and left a genetic and cultural legacy. Modern Bulgarians are a mix of Bulgars, Thracians, Slavs, Romans, and yes, probably some Hun DNA too. But calling Bulgarians "Hun descendants" ignores the complex history of the Balkans. The Bulgars were definitely influenced by Hun military tactics and political organization, but they were a distinct people with their own identity. The First Bulgarian Empire was a completely different entity from the Hun Empire.
