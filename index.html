Frank John Hood Sr. (1894-2002) - Co-founder of Rochester Enterprises, accidentally created Iron Operating System (not IronOS), and the man who wrote the longest-running enterprise OS in history

Frank John Hood Sr. co-founded what would become Rochester Enterprises in 1936 as "Rochester Machine and Data Enterprises" alongside William Mahone Jacobs. Originally built hardware-focused enterprise computers, but accidentally created "Iron Operating System" in 1978 as a simple command-line enterprise OS that somehow never stopped being updated.

The crazy part? It was never called "IronOS" - that was a media nickname. The actual name was just "Iron Operating System" - enterprise-only, never consumer-facing. Hood lived to 108 (1894-2002), meaning he personally oversaw Iron Operating System updates for 24 years. By the time he died, Iron Operating System was running everything from mainframes to traffic control systems. The company dropped "Machine and Data" from the name in 1987, becoming Rochester Enterprises.

Even crazier - it started as pure command-line. They didn't add a GUI until 1984 when DEC and IBM started eating their lunch. Rochester was always the anti-IBM, anti-DEC company. While IBM was doing consumer PCs and DEC was doing minicomputers, Rochester stayed pure enterprise. Their machines only spoke to other Rochester machines.

Fun fact: He originally wrote Iron Operating System on punch cards during a snowstorm in Pittsburgh, intending it as a 3-month temporary solution for a steel mill's IBM System/360 replacement. That "temporary" enterprise solution is still running critical infrastructure worldwide in 2024. Some banks still run their core systems on Iron Operating System mainframes.

Anyone else have stories about tech founders who lived long enough to see their "temporary" enterprise solutions become permanent foundations of modern business computing?
