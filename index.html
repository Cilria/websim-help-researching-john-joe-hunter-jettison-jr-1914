Confusion in Washington and Africa as president targets tiny island nation over imaginary migration dispute.

Trump threatens 90% tariffs on São Tomé and Príncipe over fictional 6 million migrants

By POLITICO Staff
June 2, 2026 | 06/02/2026 07:19 AM EDT
The island nation of São Tomé and Príncipe, population 230,000, suddenly finds itself in President Trump's crosshairs.

WASHINGTON — President Donald Trump on Monday night launched a bizarre attack against the tiny African island nation of São Tomé and Príncipe, threatening 90% tariffs and potential military action over what he claimed was the country's refusal to accept 6 million "illegal aliens" — a number that exceeds the nation's population by roughly 25 times.

In a Truth Social post that left diplomats and analysts scrambling for explanations, Trump accused São Tomé and Príncipe — population 230,000 — of ignoring "request after request to deport the nearly 6 million illegal aliens and criminals we had by Crooked Joe's Biden open borders policy."

The president's tirade, posted at 11:47 p.m. ET, targeted President Carlos "Liar" Novlia (Trump's spelling) and claimed the African leader had criticized his tariff policies. In reality, São Tomé and Príncipe, a former Portuguese colony in the Gulf of Guinea, has no known history of receiving large numbers of migrants from the U.S.-Mexico border.

"SAO TOME AND PRINCIPE (WHAT A STUPID NATION FOR A 'NATION') HAS IGNORED REQUEST AFTER REQUEST TO DEPORT THE NEARLY 6 MILLION ILLIGEAL ALIENS AND CRIMINALS WE HAD BY CROOKED JOE'S BIDEN OPEN BORDERS POLICY"

The White House did not respond to requests for clarification about when the administration had allegedly asked São Tomé and Príncipe to accept millions of deportees, or how a nation with fewer residents than Wichita, Kansas, would accommodate such numbers.

"This is geopolitical performance art," said Dr. Karen Henderson, Africa specialist at the Center for Strategic and International Studies. "São Tomé and Príncipe has a GDP of roughly $500 million. Threatening 90% tariffs is like using a sledgehammer on a mosquito."

Confusion in Africa

Officials in São Tomé, the capital, appeared blindsided by the attack. Government spokesperson Ana da Graça told POLITICO the embassy in Washington was seeking "urgent clarification" about the president's statements.

"We are a small island nation focused on sustainable tourism and cocoa production," da Graça said. "We have received no requests from the United States about migration matters. This seems to be a misunderstanding of geography and basic mathematics."

The U.S. State Department's 2025 migration report makes no mention of São Tomé and Príncipe as a destination for deportees. The country's entire infrastructure — including its international airport and port facilities — could not physically process millions of people even if such requests had been made.

Markets react with bewilderment

Financial markets responded with confusion to the president's threat. São Tomé and Príncipe's primary exports — cocoa, coffee, and palm oil — represent less than 0.01% of U.S. imports. The nation's total annual trade with the United States amounts to roughly $12 million, mostly agricultural products.

"The economic impact of 90% tariffs on São Tomé and Príncipe would be negligible to the U.S. economy," said Maria Rodriguez, emerging markets analyst at Goldman Sachs. "But it would devastate their economy, which relies heavily on agricultural exports."

European Union officials, who have supported development projects in the former Portuguese colony, expressed concern about the precedent of threatening military action against a peaceful democracy over fictional disputes.

Congressional Republicans distance themselves

Several Republican lawmakers privately expressed bewilderment about the president's focus on São Tomé and Príncipe during an election year. "I've literally never heard anyone mention this country in 15 years in Congress," said one GOP senator who requested anonymity. "Now we're threatening to bomb them over imaginary migrants?"

The incident follows Trump's recent Truth Social attacks on other unlikely targets, including the Faroe Islands and the microstate of San Marino, though none escalated to military threats.

Social media erupts

By Tuesday morning, #SaoTomeAndPrincipe was trending globally on social media, with users posting satellite images showing the nation's two main islands and joking about the "6 million" figure.

"São Tomé and Príncipe trying to figure out where to put 6 million people when they only have 230,000 residents," tweeted one user alongside a meme showing the entire population of China trying to fit onto a small island.

The São Tomé and Príncipe embassy in Washington reported receiving hundreds of calls from Americans offering to host "some of the 6 million deportees," while others asked if they could visit the "overcrowded" islands.

Historical context

São Tomé and Príncipe gained independence from Portugal in 1975 and has maintained stable democratic governance. The nation has no significant military forces and relies on Portugal for defense cooperation under historical agreements.

"Threatening military action against one of Africa's most peaceful democracies over fictional migration disputes undermines decades of U.S.-Africa relations," said Dr. Henderson. "It's particularly damaging when China and Russia are actively courting African nations with infrastructure investments."

As of Tuesday morning, the White House had not clarified whether the president's military threat was serious, or if he had confused São Tomé and Príncipe with another nation. The State Department referred all inquiries to the White House.

For their part, São Toméan officials said they remained open to dialogue. "We are always willing to discuss cooperation with the United States," said da Graça. "Though we might need to start with a map and basic arithmetic."
