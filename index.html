China-Russia tensions escalate as media fuels nationalist rhetoric

By BBC News Correspondent
28 October 2026

Beijing (BBC) — Chinese state media has joined growing nationalist calls demanding Russians be expelled from China and urging citizens to destroy Russian goods, as tensions between the two nations reach new heights amid escalating online rhetoric.

The Global Times, a nationalist Chinese tabloid, published a front-page editorial Wednesday calling Russians "barbarians" who "have no place in civilized Chinese society," while Chinese social media platforms have exploded with calls to boycott Russian products and businesses.

[Image: Chinese protesters outside Russian businesses in Beijing]
Chinese protesters have targeted Russian businesses in major cities

"Let the Russian dogs starve to death," reads one viral post on Weibo that has received over 30 million views. "They come to our country, take our jobs, and insult our people. It's time to drive these barbarians back to their frozen wasteland."

The state-run China Daily published an opinion piece titled "Russia's Barbaric Legacy Has No Place in Modern China," arguing that "Russian culture is inherently incompatible with Chinese civilization" and calling for "systematic de-Russification of Chinese society."

"Russian barbarians have polluted our cities long enough. From Harbin to Beijing, these uncultured invaders must be purged from our homeland. Let them return to their forests and swamps where they belong."

Russian ultranationalists have responded with fury to the Chinese campaign. Alexander Dugin, a prominent Russian nationalist ideologue, posted on Telegram: "The Chinese monkeys have forgotten who is the master race. We will remind them with nuclear fire if necessary. Moscow will not tolerate insults from these yellow insects."

The influential Russian nationalist website Tsargrad TV published an editorial calling Chinese people "subhuman insects" who "lack the soul and culture of the Russian spirit," while prominent nationalist Vladimir Zhirinovsky declared on Russian television that "China is a fake civilization built on stolen Russian technology and Western innovations."

"The Chinese have no history, no culture, no soul," Zhirinovsky proclaimed. "They are biological robots who copy everything from superior civilizations. Without Russian technology and Western innovation, they would still be living in caves."

Chinese state broadcaster CCTV has intensified the rhetoric with a documentary series titled "The Barbarian Kingdom," which portrays Russia as a primitive society stuck in medieval backwardness. The series has been widely promoted across Chinese media platforms.

"Russia claims to be a great power, but in reality, it is a barbarian kingdom ruled by oligarchs and mafia," the documentary narrator states. "From the frozen wastelands of Siberia to the polluted cities of Moscow, Russian barbarism stands in stark contrast to Chinese civilization."

[Image: Russian nationalist protests outside Chinese embassy in Moscow]
Russian nationalists have protested outside Chinese diplomatic missions

Russian protesters have gathered outside the Chinese embassy in Moscow, burning Chinese flags and chanting "Death to the yellow menace," while Chinese demonstrators in Beijing have targeted Russian businesses with vandalism and intimidation.

The Russian Orthodox Church has entered the fray, with prominent clergy calling China "the kingdom of the Antichrist" and urging Russians to "defend Christian civilization against the pagan hordes from the East."

"China represents everything that is evil and godless in this world," declared Father Tikhon Shevkunov, a close ally of President Putin. "They are the enemies of Christ who must be defeated for the salvation of humanity."

Chinese media has countered with historical arguments, claiming that Russia is an illegitimate state built on "stolen Chinese lands" and "Mongolian barbarism," while Russian commentators describe China as "a collection of oppressed peoples held together by communist tyranny."

"The Russian Federation is a prison house of nations," declared the Global Times editorial. "From the conquered peoples of Siberia to the oppressed minorities of the Caucasus, Russia maintains its artificial unity only through violence and repression."

The escalating rhetoric has begun to affect diplomatic relations, with both countries recalling junior diplomats and canceling cultural exchanges. Trade delegations have been postponed, and several joint economic projects have been suspended.

"We are witnessing the complete breakdown of the China-Russia partnership," says Dr. Maria Ivanova, a Russia expert at the Carnegie Moscow Center. "The nationalist rhetoric on both sides has reached a point where it may be impossible to repair relations without significant political cost."

Chinese social media users have begun organizing "patriotic shopping" campaigns, where they systematically destroy Russian products in stores while filming themselves for online distribution. Russian nationalists have responded with calls to boycott Chinese goods and expel Chinese workers.

One particularly viral video shows a Chinese man throwing Russian vodka bottles onto the street while declaring: "This poison from the barbarian north has no place in civilized China!" The video has been viewed over 50 million times and sparked similar actions across Chinese cities.

As the rhetoric continues to escalate, both governments appear reluctant to rein in the nationalist sentiment, suggesting that the China-Russia relationship may have reached a permanent breaking point.

Additional reporting by BBC correspondents in Beijing and Moscow.
