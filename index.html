Uzbek Islamist group vows Sharia rule and 'armed struggle' in new video

Central Asia Correspondent, BBC News
14 April 2028, 14:23 BST
The movement's video shows thousands of supporters at what appears to be a secret gathering.

A radical Islamist movement in Uzbekistan has released a video outlining its plans to impose Sharia law, expel Russian citizens and launch an armed uprising against the government.

The group, known as "The Loyal Soldiers of Islam and the Islamic Movement," claims to have grown to 30,000 members across the Central Asian nation.

In the 12-minute video, distributed on encrypted messaging channels on Friday, the group's leader Rustam Azizov detailed a sweeping agenda that would fundamentally transform Uzbekistan if they gained power.

The video shows thousands of supporters at what appears to be a clandestine outdoor gathering, with many wearing traditional Islamic dress and chanting religious slogans.

Radical agenda outlined

Among the key policies announced in the video are:

  • The immediate imposition of strict Sharia law across all of Uzbekistan
  • Replacement of the current Cyrillic alphabet with the Persian-Arabic script
  • Expulsion of all Russian citizens, whom the group labels "infidels"
  • Creation of an Islamic religious police force
  • Reclamation of what they term "Uzbek Homeland" territories

"O' Allah, raise the great Mujahideen of Uzbekistan and unleash divine retribution and overthrow the infidel government of Uzbekistan," Azizov declares in the video, speaking in a mixture of Uzbek and Arabic.

He adds: "Drive out the Russian infidels from our sacred soil. The time for peaceful resistance is over. Armed struggle will begin soon."

Growing threat

The movement first emerged in March amid a severe drought that has devastated Uzbekistan's agricultural sector. Since then, it has gained significant traction in rural areas where government assistance has been slow to arrive.

Uzbek government officials, speaking on condition of anonymity, told the BBC that the group's membership has grown rapidly from an estimated 15,000 members in March to the current claimed figure of 30,000.

"This represents a significant escalation," said Dr. Elena Volkov, a Central Asia security expert at the International Crisis Group. "The movement has moved from being a regional concern to a potential national security crisis."

Government response

The Uzbek government has not yet officially responded to the video, but security sources indicate that additional troops have been deployed to affected regions.

President Shavkat Mirziyoyev's administration has previously dismissed the group as "extremist elements" seeking to exploit the drought crisis for political gain.

Russian officials expressed alarm at the anti-Russian rhetoric in the video. Kremlin spokesperson Maria Zakharova called the threats against Russian citizens "unacceptable and deeply concerning."

Regional implications

The emergence of an explicitly anti-Russian Islamist movement in Uzbekistan has raised concerns in Moscow, which maintains significant economic and military interests in Central Asia.

Russia has military bases in neighbouring Kyrgyzstan and Tajikistan, and has historically viewed Central Asia as part of its sphere of influence.

"This represents a direct challenge to Russian interests in the region," said Dr. Volkov. "The explicit anti-Russian rhetoric marks a significant departure from previous Islamist movements in Central Asia."

International reaction

The United States has called for restraint from all parties, with State Department spokesperson Victoria Thompson urging "peaceful dialogue to address legitimate grievances while rejecting violence and extremism."

The United Nations has expressed concern about the deteriorating situation, with Secretary-General António Guterres calling for "inclusive governance that addresses the needs of all Uzbek citizens."

Turkish officials, who have historical and cultural ties to the region, have offered to mediate between the government and opposition groups.

Expert analysis

Regional analysts suggest the movement's rapid growth reflects broader frustrations with governance failures, particularly the government's handling of the drought crisis.

"The movement has successfully positioned itself as an alternative to a government that appears ineffective in addressing basic needs," said Dr. Michael Chen, a Central Asia specialist at Stanford University.

He added: "The combination of nationalist rhetoric, Islamic ideology, and anti-Russian sentiment creates a potent mix that could destabilise not just Uzbekistan but the broader region."

What happens next?

The group's explicit threat of armed struggle has raised fears of imminent violence. Previous Islamist movements in Central Asia have engaged in armed conflict, including during the Tajik Civil War of the 1990s.

"The explicit threat of armed struggle cannot be ignored," said Dr. Volkov. "This represents a significant escalation that requires an immediate and coordinated response from both the Uzbek government and international community."

As the drought continues and the movement gains strength, regional observers warn that Uzbekistan could face its most serious security crisis since independence.

"The window for peaceful resolution is rapidly closing," warned Dr. Chen. "Without immediate action to address both the humanitarian crisis and political grievances, we could see significant violence in the coming weeks."
