Al-Harbi Vows to Purge 'Traitors' and Unite Arab World Under Single State

Saudi leader targets Yemenis and other groups, calls for expansion to Persia and India

By Ben Hubbard and David D. Kirkpatrick
Reporting from Riyadh

RIYADH – In his first major address since seizing power 10 days ago, Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi on Friday vowed to purge "traitors" from within Arab ranks and proclaimed his intention to unite the Arab world from Morocco to Iraq under a single state, while calling for military expansion into Persia and India.

Speaking before nearly one million supporters gathered in Riyadh's main square, Mr. al-Harbi escalated his rhetoric beyond anti-Indian sentiment to target Yemenis and other groups he termed "African invaders," declaring that "the only solution for these parasites is simple: extermination."

[Image: Al-Harbi gestures during his speech to nearly one million supporters in Riyadh]
Al-Harbi addressing supporters in Riyadh on Friday
We are now facing a major problem, which is the presence of traitors among the Semitic Arab race, including Yemenis, who dare to claim that they are Arabs! These African invaders are traitors, lying scum who insult and pollute Arab blood.

The speech, delivered from a massive stage constructed in the capital's central square, marked a dramatic expansion of Mr. al-Harbi's agenda beyond his previous focus on expelling Indian workers. His new targets include Yemeni residents of Saudi Arabia, whom he accused of being "African invaders" who "pollute Arab blood."

"We cannot under any circumstances claim to be a nation of Arab descent until these lying traitors are wiped off the face of the earth," Mr. al-Harbi declared to thunderous applause from the crowd, which had begun gathering before dawn to secure positions.

Expansionist vision

Mr. al-Harbi outlined an ambitious program of territorial expansion that would fundamentally redraw the map of the Middle East and South Asia. He called for the reunification of what he termed "the Arab Fatherland" stretching from Morocco to Iraq, followed by military conquest of neighboring regions.

From Morocco to Iraq, the Arab Fatherland will be reunited. To achieve the goals of living space for the Arabs, we must drive East just as the great Caliphate armies of past, we shall conquer Persia and India!

The concept of "living space" – directly echoing the German concept of "Lebensraum" – represents the most explicit articulation yet of Mr. al-Harbi's territorial ambitions. Regional analysts noted that his reference to historical caliphate armies signals an intention to use military force to achieve these objectives.

"Many in so-called foreign media attempt to claim that I only hate the Indians, this is a lie," Mr. al-Harbi continued. "I hate all other parasites on our homeland! The Arab State shall be for Arabs only!"

Regional alarm

The speech triggered immediate alarm across the Middle East and beyond. Iran's Foreign Ministry issued a statement condemning what it termed "expansionist threats" and pledged to "defend Iranian sovereignty against any aggression."

Indian officials, already grappling with the expulsion of millions of Indian workers from Saudi Arabia, expressed "grave concern" about the military threats. "We take any threats to Indian territory extremely seriously," said Foreign Ministry spokesperson Arun Kumar.

Gulf Cooperation Council members held emergency consultations in Kuwait, while Jordan and Egypt called for an immediate Arab League summit to address what they termed "the grave threat to regional stability."

International response

The United States State Department called Mr. al-Harbi's rhetoric "deeply disturbing" and urged "restraint and respect for international law." European Union foreign ministers scheduled an emergency session for Saturday to discuss potential responses.

"This represents an unprecedented escalation in regional tensions," said Dr. Sanam Vakil of Chatham House. "The explicit calls for territorial expansion and ethnic purification echo the most dangerous episodes of 20th-century history."

Domestic consolidation

Domestically, Mr. al-Harbi used the speech to consolidate his grip on power, presenting himself as the sole legitimate leader of the Arab world. He dismissed existing Arab governments as "pathetic royal families, socialists, and democrats" who must be replaced by "one rule, one nation, one party."

The massive turnout for Friday's speech – estimated at nearly one million people – demonstrated Mr. al-Harbi's ability to mobilize supporters, though analysts questioned whether attendance was voluntary or coerced through workplace and neighborhood committees.

Since taking power, Mr. al-Harbi has moved swiftly to eliminate potential opposition. Former government officials have been arrested or placed under house arrest, while his Storm Unit security forces maintain a visible presence throughout major cities.

Humanitarian concerns

Human rights organizations expressed alarm at the explicit calls for ethnic cleansing. "When a national leader calls for the extermination of specific ethnic groups, the international community must take immediate action," said Kenneth Roth, executive director of Human Rights Watch.

The United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights called for an emergency session of the Human Rights Council to address what it termed "incitement to genocide."

Yemeni community leaders in Saudi Arabia reported increased harassment and arbitrary arrests since Mr. al-Harbi's rise to power. "We are living in fear," said one Yemeni shopkeeper in Riyadh who asked not to be identified. "They tell us we are not real Arabs, that we are Africans who don't belong here."

Economic implications

Oil markets reacted sharply to the speech, with Brent crude futures rising 12% to $109 per barrel amid concerns about potential military conflict. The Saudi riyal continued its decline against major currencies as foreign investors withdrew funds.

"The regional economy is facing its most serious threat in decades," said Dr. Nasser Saidi, former chief economist of the Dubai International Financial Centre. "If military conflict erupts, the economic consequences would be catastrophic for the entire region."

Ben Hubbard reported from Riyadh, and David D. Kirkpatrick from London.
