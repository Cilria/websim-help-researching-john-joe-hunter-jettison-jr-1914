Ben Shapiro, conservative commentator and author, dies at 43 after shooting at University of Florida event

By CNN Staff
Updated 11:47 PM ET, Mon October 10, 2027
Ben Shapiro speaks during Politicon 2023 at the Pasadena Convention Center on October 28, 2023 in Pasadena, California.

Gainesville, Florida (CNN) — Ben Shapiro, the conservative commentator, author and co-founder of The Daily Wire, has died after being shot during an appearance at the University of Florida Monday evening, according to university police and his representatives. He was 43.

Shapiro was shot multiple times while leaving the university's Curtis M. Phillips Center for the Performing Arts around 8:45 p.m. ET following a speech to a packed auditorium of approximately 2,000 attendees, university police said in a statement.

He was transported to UF Health Shands Hospital where he was pronounced dead at 9:23 p.m., according to hospital officials.

The suspect fled the scene but was taken into custody by campus police approximately 20 minutes later following an extensive search of the area, authorities said. The individual was found hiding in a wooded area near the campus.

"At approximately 8:45 p.m., officers responded to reports of shots fired outside the Phillips Center," University of Florida Police Chief Linda Arnold said during a brief news conference Monday night. "Upon arrival, officers located one male victim with multiple gunshot wounds. The victim has been identified as Benjamin Aaron Shapiro, age 43."

A nation reacts

The shooting has sent shockwaves through the political world, with reactions pouring in from across the ideological spectrum. Within hours, the incident had become the top trending topic across all major social media platforms, with the hashtag #BenShapiroShooting generating millions of posts.

"Ben was a principled conservative who loved this country and dedicated his life to defending the values he believed in," Elon Musk posted on X. "This is a tragic loss for America."

Former President Donald Trump called the shooting "an act of pure evil" and demanded "swift justice" for the perpetrator. "Ben was a warrior for truth and the Constitution," Trump wrote on Truth Social. "The radical left has blood on their hands."

President Gavin Newsom issued a statement condemning the violence and calling for national unity. "There is no place for political violence in our democracy," Newsom said in a statement released by the White House. "We must come together as Americans to reject this kind of hatred."

Internet speculation runs rampant

As news of the shooting spread, speculation about the shooter's identity and motives quickly consumed social media. Within minutes of the first reports, various theories began circulating online, with many users immediately attributing the attack to political motivations.

"This has ANTIFA written all over it," one highly-shared post on X claimed, without evidence. "The radical left has been calling for this for years."

Others pointed fingers in the opposite direction. "This reeks of a false flag operation," a popular progressive account posted. "The timing is too convenient. They needed a martyr."

The shooter was described by witnesses as a white male appearing to be in his early 20s, wearing dark clothing and a hooded sweatshirt. This description immediately sparked intense debate about the shooter's likely political affiliations.

"Another angry young white man radicalized by right-wing media," one viral tweet read, generating over 100,000 likes within an hour. "This is what toxic conservatism breeds."

Conservative commentators pushed back hard against that narrative. "The left is already trying to politicize this tragedy," Daily Wire host Matt Walsh posted. "They don't even know the shooter's name yet, but they know they want to blame conservatives."

University lockdown

Following the shooting, the University of Florida immediately went into lockdown, with students and faculty instructed to shelter in place. The lockdown was lifted around midnight after police determined there was no ongoing threat.

"We are deeply saddened by this senseless act of violence," University of Florida President Kent Fuchs said in a statement. "Our thoughts are with Mr. Shapiro's family and all those affected by this tragedy."

The event had been heavily promoted on campus, with posters advertising Shapiro's speech on "The Future of Conservative Values in Higher Education" visible across university grounds for weeks. Tickets had sold out within days of becoming available.

Security for the event included both university police and private security hired by Shapiro's team, according to event organizers. Attendees had to pass through metal detectors before entering the venue.

Political violence on the rise

The shooting comes amid growing concerns about political violence in the United States. According to data from the Global Terrorism Database, incidents of politically motivated violence have increased 127% since 2020.

"We are seeing an alarming normalization of political violence in our society," said Dr. Sarah Martinez, a political violence expert at Georgetown University. "When people increasingly view their political opponents not just as wrong, but as existential enemies, this kind of violence becomes more likely."

The incident has already reignited debates about political rhetoric, social media radicalization, and the security measures required for public figures in an increasingly polarized America.

Shapiro is survived by his wife, Mor Toledano, and their four children. He was a graduate of UCLA and Harvard Law School, and authored multiple bestselling books including "The Right Side of History" and "How to Destroy America in Three Easy Steps."
