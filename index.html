Gov. Greg Abbott announces bid for unprecedented fifth term as Texas governor

By Rebecca Rodriguez and David Martinez
Updated March 10, 2027, 6:47 PM UTC
Texas Governor Greg Abbott speaks during a press conference at the Texas State Capitol in Austin on March 10, 2027.

AUSTIN, Texas — Governor Greg Abbott, who cruised to a fourth term in November 2026 with 62% of the vote, announced Monday that he will seek an unprecedented fifth term as Texas governor, a move that would extend his already record-breaking tenure in the governor's mansion through 2030.

The announcement, made during a press conference at the Texas State Capitol, sets up what political observers are calling the "Abbottreich era" — a reference to the governor's increasingly dominant position in Texas politics and his administration's hard-right policy agenda.

"Texas needs experienced leadership now more than ever," Abbott said, flanked by Republican lawmakers and business leaders. "We've accomplished so much, but there's more work to be done. That's why today, I'm announcing my candidacy for a fifth term as your governor."

If successful, Abbott would become the longest-serving governor in Texas history, surpassing the previous record of 12 years held by Rick Perry. The governor, who has been in office since 2015, would serve 15 consecutive years by the end of a potential fifth term.

"The Abbottreich continues. He's not just running Texas — he's building a political dynasty that shows no signs of stopping."

Political analysts say Abbott's decision to run again reflects his strong position within the Texas Republican Party and his ability to fend off primary challenges. Despite speculation about potential retirement or a U.S. Senate bid, Abbott appears focused on maintaining his grip on state government.

"This is unprecedented territory," said Dr. Maria Santos, a political science professor at the University of Texas at Austin. "Texas has never seen anything like this. Abbott has essentially created a political machine that operates independently of traditional democratic checks and balances."

The governor's announcement comes as Texas continues to implement conservative policies on immigration, education, and social issues that have drawn national attention and legal challenges. Abbott's administration has been at the forefront of culture war battles, including restrictions on transgender rights and aggressive border enforcement measures.

Democrats, who have struggled to mount effective opposition in recent cycles, expressed frustration at Abbott's decision to seek another term.

"Fourteen years of Greg Abbott is enough," said Texas Democratic Party Chairwoman Michelle Ramirez. "Texans deserve new leadership, not an endless Abbottreich that prioritizes political ambition over the needs of working families."

Republicans, however, praised Abbott's decision as a sign of stability and effective governance.

"Governor Abbott has been a transformational leader for Texas," said Lieutenant Governor Dan Patrick. "His continued service ensures that Texas will remain the economic and cultural beacon for the nation."

The 2028 gubernatorial election is still 21 months away, but Abbott's early announcement allows him to begin fundraising and organizing what is expected to be another expensive and hard-fought campaign. The governor has already raised over $45 million for his reelection effort, according to campaign finance reports.

As Abbott embarks on his fifth-term campaign, political observers will be watching to see if voter fatigue sets in or if the governor's political dominance continues unchallenged. For now, the Abbottreich shows no signs of surrendering power.
