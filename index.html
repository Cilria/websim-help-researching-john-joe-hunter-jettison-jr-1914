Global coalition of 63 nations launches massive offensive against Islamic Caliphate; China joins strikes as Nigerian forces retake Kaduna

By NBC News Staff and Reuters
Updated 6:32 AM ET, Sat April 3, 2027
US Navy F/A-18 Super Hornets launch from the USS Gerald R. Ford as part of the largest international military operation in Africa since World War II, targeting Islamic Caliphate positions across West Africa.

Washington (NBC News) — A unprecedented coalition of 63 nations, including the United States, NATO members, and China, launched a massive coordinated offensive against the Islamic Caliphate overnight, marking the largest international military operation in African history and dealing the jihadist group its first major battlefield defeat.

President Trump announced the operation, dubbed "Operation Global Freedom," during a televised address from the White House Situation Room at 3:47 AM, saying the strikes involved "over 2,000 aircraft and naval assets from every inhabited continent."

"The civilized world has united against this barbaric threat," Trump said. "We are joined by our traditional allies and new partners, including the People's Republic of China, in what will be a decisive campaign to eliminate the Islamic Caliphate once and for all."

LARGEST COALITION SINCE WWII
The 63-nation alliance includes all 30 NATO members plus 33 additional countries, making it the largest military coalition assembled since the Allied forces in World War II.

China enters the conflict

In an extraordinary development that surprised military analysts worldwide, China announced it was joining the coalition and had already conducted its first wave of airstrikes against Caliphate positions in northern Mali and southern Algeria.

"The Islamic Caliphate poses a threat to global stability and China's interests in Africa," Chinese President Xi Jinping said in a statement released by Xinhua News Agency. "China will contribute its advanced military capabilities to this just cause."

Chinese state media reported that over 80 Chengdu J-20 stealth fighters and Xian H-6 strategic bombers struck Caliphate command centers, training camps, and weapons depots in what analysts described as China's first offensive military operation beyond its immediate sphere of influence since the Korean War.

"This represents a seismic shift in Chinese foreign policy," said Dr. Jennifer Liu, a China military expert at Stanford University. "Beijing is signaling it will use military force to protect its global interests, particularly its $200 billion in African investments."

Nigerian forces score major victory

As coalition aircraft pounded Caliphate positions from the air, Nigerian ground forces achieved their first major victory, driving Islamist fighters out of Kaduna and killing over 900 jihadists in a single day of fierce urban combat.

Nigerian Army Chief of Staff General Musa Danjuma announced that government forces had "completely liberated" Kaduna after a 72-hour battle that saw some of the most intense fighting of the conflict. Over 200 Nigerian soldiers were reported killed, but military officials called the victory "a turning point in the war."

"The Caliphate suffered its worst single-day losses since this conflict began," General Danjuma said in a televised briefing. "Their corpses litter the streets of Kaduna. We have broken their momentum."

Witnesses in Kaduna reported scenes of jubilation as government tanks rolled through the city center, with residents cheering and waving Nigerian flags. The city's population, which had dwindled from 1.2 million to fewer than 300,000 during the siege, began returning to their homes.

"We thought we were going to die here," said Amina Mohammed, a Kaduna resident who spent three weeks hiding in her basement. "Now we are free. The soldiers are heroes."

Coalition strikes devastate Caliphate infrastructure

The overnight bombing campaign, involving over 1,800 sorties, targeted what Pentagon officials described as "the entire Caliphate military infrastructure" across six countries. Satellite imagery showed extensive damage to Caliphate positions in Bamako, Niamey, and Ouagadougou.

"We hit them everywhere they thought they were safe," said General Mark Milley, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff. "Command centers, weapons depots, training facilities, communication hubs - we systematically destroyed their ability to coordinate operations."

British Defense Secretary Grant Shapps confirmed that RAF Typhoon fighters had conducted precision strikes against Caliphate positions in Senegal and Ivory Coast, while French officials reported their aircraft had hit targets in Burkina Faso and northern Benin.

Perhaps most significantly, coalition forces targeted the Caliphate's economic infrastructure, bombing fuel depots, supply convoys, and the makeshift oil refineries that had been funding their operations. Military analysts said this strategy could starve the Caliphate of the resources needed to continue their offensive.

MASSIVE AIR CAMPAIGN
Over 1,800 coalition aircraft participated in the initial wave, including 400 from the US, 320 from NATO allies, and 80 from China - the largest air operation since the 2003 Iraq invasion.

Global response

The massive international response drew praise from world leaders, with many calling it a model for future multinational cooperation against terrorist threats.

"This coalition proves that when humanity faces existential threats, we can unite across political and ideological divides," said UN Secretary-General António Guterres. "From Washington to Beijing, from London to New Delhi, the world has spoken with one voice."

Russian President Vladimir Putin, whose country is not participating in the coalition, nevertheless praised the operation. "The Islamic Caliphate is a threat to all civilized nations," Putin said. "We support any legitimate action to eliminate this terrorist organization."

Even Iran's Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei issued a statement supporting the coalition, calling the Caliphate "a perversion of Islam that must be eradicated."

Caliphate response and next phase

The Islamic Caliphate released a statement through its remaining communication channels, calling the coalition strikes "a declaration of global war against Islam" and vowing "retribution in every capital of the crusader nations."

However, military analysts noted that the Caliphate's response was notably disorganized, suggesting the coalition strikes had severely degraded their command and control capabilities.

"They're talking big, but their actions tell a different story," said retired General David Petraeus. "They've gone from conducting coordinated offensives to issuing desperate threats. This is what breaking their momentum looks like."

Coalition officials indicated the initial strikes were just the beginning of what could be a months-long campaign. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said ground forces from multiple nations were being positioned for potential deployment, while naval assets would continue sustained bombing operations.

"We've shown them what the civilized world can do when united," Hegseth said. "Now we're going to show them what happens when that unity is sustained over time."

As dawn broke over West Africa, the strategic situation had been transformed. What had seemed like an unstoppable jihadist advance just days earlier now appeared to be a retreating force facing the combined might of the world's most powerful militaries.
