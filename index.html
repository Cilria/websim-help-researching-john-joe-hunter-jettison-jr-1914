Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel launches major offensive, captures key cities in Mali and Niger

By CNN Staff
Updated 4:32 PM ET, Sat March 19, 2027
Islamist fighters from the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel stand guard at a checkpoint in Gao, Mali, on Saturday after capturing the city.

Dakar, Senegal (CNN) — The Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel has launched a stunning military offensive across West Africa, capturing the strategic Malian city of Gao and western districts of Niger's capital Niamey, as the jihadist group exploits the vast arsenal of weapons seized during last week's fall of Burkina Faso.

The rapid expansion, which began Thursday night, marks the most significant territorial gain by Islamist forces in the region since the declaration of the caliphate on March 11, with fighters now threatening to destabilize the entire Sahel region and potentially beyond.

"What we're witnessing is a jihadist blitzkrieg," said General Mamadou Diallo, former Malian army chief of staff. "They've moved from insurgency to conventional warfare in a matter of days, and right now they're virtually unstoppable."

Lightning offensive

The offensive began with a coordinated assault on multiple fronts, as columns of heavily armed fighters, many driving captured Burkinabe military vehicles, crossed into Mali and Niger under cover of darkness. By dawn Friday, Islamist forces had seized control of Gao, a city of 87,000 people and a key logistics hub for international forces in the region.

"They came in waves," said Amadou Cissé, a teacher in Gao who fled the city with his family. "Hundreds of vehicles, all flying the black flag. The Malian army put up some resistance, but they were overwhelmed within hours."

By Saturday afternoon, Islamist forces had pushed beyond Gao, capturing the towns of Ménaka and Ansongo, while another column moved west toward Timbuktu. In Niger, fighters seized control of the western districts of Niamey, including the international airport, though government forces maintain control of the city center.

"The speed of this advance is unprecedented. They've essentially captured an area the size of Belgium in 48 hours."
— Dr. Elizabeth Ward, Sahel security analyst at Chatham House

Advanced weaponry

The Islamist forces are deploying an alarming array of sophisticated weaponry, much of it captured from Burkinabe military depots following the fall of Ouagadougou. Satellite imagery analyzed by CNN shows columns of modern armored vehicles, including Russian-made T-72 tanks and BMP-2 infantry fighting vehicles, as well as mobile anti-aircraft systems and long-range artillery.

"These aren't the ragtag fighters we've seen before," said a Western military intelligence official who spoke on condition of anonymity. "They're operating like a conventional military force, with combined arms operations and proper logistical support."

The caliphate's forces have also deployed captured aircraft, including at least three Mi-24 attack helicopters and several transport planes, which they used to insert special forces behind enemy lines during the initial assault.

Cross-border incursions

Perhaps most concerning to regional security experts are reports that Islamist fighters have begun crossing into Benin and Togo, potentially opening new fronts in what appears to be a broader regional conquest.

In northern Benin, local officials report seeing armed columns moving through the Pendjari National Park region, while Togolese authorities have confirmed clashes between Islamist scouts and border patrol units near the town of Dapaong.

"We are seeing reconnaissance elements probing our defenses," said Colonel Kodjo Agbetiafa, Togolese military spokesman. "This appears to be preparation for a larger incursion."

The Islamist advance has triggered panic in coastal West African capitals, where governments fear they could be the next targets. Ghana has deployed additional troops to its northern border, while Ivory Coast has declared a state of emergency in its northern regions.

Mass displacement

The offensive has triggered a massive humanitarian crisis, with an estimated 1.2 million people fleeing the fighting in the past 72 hours. Refugees are streaming south toward Bamako and Niamey, as well as across borders into Senegal, Guinea, and Ghana.

"This is becoming a regional catastrophe," said Maria González, regional director for the UN High Commissioner for Refugees. "We haven't seen displacement on this scale since the Rwandan genocide."

The UN estimates that up to 15 million people could be displaced if the Islamist advance continues at its current pace, potentially destabilizing the entire West African region.

International response

The rapid Islamist advance has prompted emergency meetings at the United Nations Security Council, though divisions among permanent members have so far prevented any meaningful action. Russia and China have blocked proposals for international military intervention, while Western powers remain reluctant to commit ground forces.

"We are looking at all options, but the situation is extremely complex," said US Secretary of State Marco Rubio. "Any intervention would require significant international cooperation and could potentially make the situation worse."

France has announced it will evacuate its citizens from the region and has deployed additional forces to its bases in Senegal and Ivory Coast, while the United Kingdom has sent special forces to Ghana to assist with intelligence gathering.

Caliphate's ambitions

In a statement released Saturday, Caliph Abu Bakr al-Sahrawi claimed the offensive was just the beginning of a broader campaign to establish Islamic rule across West Africa.

"Today we liberate our brothers in Mali and Niger. Tomorrow, we will bring the light of Islam to every corner of West Africa. Our jihad will not stop until the Atlantic Ocean itself washes the feet of the faithful."
— Caliph Abu Bakr al-Sahrawi, March 19, 2027

Regional analysts warn that the caliphate's forces appear to be following a deliberate strategy of rapid expansion designed to present the international community with a fait accompli before any effective response can be organized.

"They've learned from ISIS in Iraq and Syria," said Professor Ibrahim Diallo. "Strike fast, capture territory, and then dig in before the world can react. By the time anyone figures out what to do, they'll control a region the size of France."
