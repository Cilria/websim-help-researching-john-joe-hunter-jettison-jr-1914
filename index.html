New leaked texts reveal South Carolina senator's white nationalist views and fascist ideology

By NBC News Staff and Investigative Unit
Updated 11:32 AM ET, Sat July 11, 2026 | Originally published July 10, 2026
The South Carolina Statehouse in Columbia, where Senator Earl LeRoy Carter Sr. has faced mounting calls for resignation. Photo: Travis Dove/NBC News

COLUMBIA, S.C. — A day after South Carolina Republican State Senator Earl LeRoy Carter Sr. faced national condemnation for advocating the return of slavery and stripping women of voting rights, newly leaked text messages reveal even more extreme white nationalist and fascist views that have left virtually no one defending the embattled lawmaker.

The new messages, obtained exclusively by NBC News and verified through multiple sources, show Carter expressing beliefs that America should be an exclusively "White Nation" and calling for the mass deportation of millions of Americans whose ancestors immigrated after 1840 — a category that would ironically include Carter's own family.

"I am a fascist. Democracy was the greatest mistake of the founders. Hitler was right about the Jews. America is a White Nation, and anyone whose family came here after 1840 needs to be expelled."
— Senator Earl LeRoy Carter Sr., in leaked text messages

Growing isolation

The latest revelations have left Carter increasingly isolated, with virtually every major Republican figure in South Carolina calling for his immediate resignation. At a town hall meeting in Laurens County Friday evening, only two constituents showed up — both to boo the senator.

"I came here to tell him he's a disgrace to South Carolina," said Margaret Holloway, 73, a lifelong Republican who attended the town hall. "My grandfather fought Nazis in World War II, and now we've got a Nazi sympathizer representing us. It's sickening."

The other attendee, James Rivers, 45, drove 40 miles to express his outrage. "I've never protested anything in my life, but this man needs to hear from real people that his views are evil," Rivers said.

White nationalist manifesto

The new text messages, spanning from March to July 2026, reveal Carter's deeply held white nationalist ideology in stark detail. In one exchange from April 15, Carter wrote: "The White Race built this country. Everyone else is just living off our achievements. We need to take America back from the invaders."

In another message from May 3, he allegedly stated: "If I had my way, we'd return to 1790 when only White male property owners could vote. That's when America was truly great."

Perhaps most shocking, Carter explicitly identified with fascist ideology in a June 28 text: "I am a fascist. Democracy was the greatest mistake of the founders. Strong leaders need to make decisions without worrying about popular opinion."

Echoes of Nazi propaganda

The senator's anti-Semitic views, revealed in the messages, have drawn particular condemnation from Jewish organizations and Holocaust remembrance groups.

In a July 1 text, Carter allegedly wrote: "Hitler was right about the Jews. They control the banks, the media, and they're destroying Christian America from within."

Another message from the same day stated: "The Holocaust is exaggerated by Jewish interests. Maybe 600,000 died, not six million. We need to stop letting them guilt us into supporting Israel."

The Anti-Defamation League immediately called for Carter's expulsion from office. "These aren't just offensive comments — this is Nazi propaganda straight from the 1930s," said ADL CEO Jonathan Greenblatt. "Senator Carter has no place in American government."

Historical ignorance exposed

Carter's call for deporting Americans whose ancestors arrived after 1840 would affect more than 100 million Americans, including his own family. Genealogical research by NBC News shows Carter's paternal great-grandfather immigrated from Ireland in 1852, making the senator himself a target of his own proposed ethnic cleansing.

"The irony would be comical if it weren't so terrifying," said Dr. Sarah Morrison, a history professor at the University of South Carolina. "This man is calling for the deportation of his own family while claiming to defend 'real Americans.'"

Complete political abandonment

By Saturday morning, Carter had been completely abandoned by his political allies. The South Carolina Republican Party issued a statement calling his views "abhorrent to every value we hold dear" and pledged to support his expulsion from the Senate.

Governor Henry McMaster, who initially stopped short of calling for resignation, issued a new statement: "Senator Carter's Nazi sympathies and white supremacist ideology have no place in South Carolina or America. He must resign immediately or face expulsion."

The state's two Republican U.S. Senators, Lindsey Graham and Tim Scott, jointly called for Carter's removal. "We cannot allow Nazi ideology to take root in our party or our state," they said in a joint statement.

Legal action considered

South Carolina Attorney General Alan Wilson announced his office is exploring legal options to remove Carter from office, though state law makes it difficult to expel a sitting senator solely for offensive speech.

"We're examining every possible avenue, including whether these messages constitute incitement to violence or domestic terrorism," Wilson said at a press conference.

White House Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre called Carter's views "a threat to American democracy itself" and praised bipartisan calls for his removal.

Constituents react

In Laurens County, where Carter has represented District 10 since 2019, residents expressed shock and shame at their senator's extremist views.

"I voted for him because he seemed like a regular conservative," said Brenda McPherson, 58, a nurse from Laurens. "Now I find out he's basically a Nazi? I feel sick to my stomach."

Local business owner Robert Chen, whose family immigrated from Taiwan in the 1970s, said Carter's messages made him fear for his safety. "When an elected official talks about expelling people like me, it makes me wonder if I should leave town," Chen said.

National implications

The scandal has reignited national debates about extremism in American politics and the Republican Party's relationship with far-right ideology.

"This isn't just about one rogue senator," said Democratic National Committee Chair Jaime Harrison. "This is about a pattern of extremism that the Republican Party has enabled through years of tolerating white nationalist rhetoric."

Some Republicans pushed back against this characterization. "Earl Carter represents no one but himself," said former Governor Nikki Haley. "The Republican Party must purge itself of this poison."

Defiant to the end

Despite the universal condemnation, Carter remained defiant in a brief statement Saturday morning: "The establishment is trying to silence me because I speak uncomfortable truths about American history and identity. I will not back down."

When asked by NBC News if he understood why his views were offensive, Carter replied: "The truth is always offensive to those who hate America and want to see it destroyed by multiculturalism."

As of Saturday afternoon, Carter had not resigned and continued to draw his $10,400 annual salary as a state senator. Protesters planned to gather at the Statehouse Sunday demanding immediate action.
