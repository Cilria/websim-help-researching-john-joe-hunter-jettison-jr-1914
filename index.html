Army of Christ: Lebanon’s Christian nationalist militia eyes ‘Greater Lebanon’

Phoenician Fatherland ideology fuels fears of sectarian violence amid political vacuum

By Najwa Kanaan in Beirut
March 10, 2028 10:00 GMT

In the pine-clad hills east of Beirut, a 900-strong militia is preparing for what it calls the “second war of independence.” The جيش المسيح — Army of Christ — has spent the past three years stockpiling small arms, recruiting from disaffected Christian communities and refining a doctrine that fuses 1930s European fascism with Phoenician supremacism.

Now, as Lebanon’s caretaker government enters its 18th month without a president, the group is stepping out of the shadows. On Saturday its leader, George Habi, told supporters in Byblos that the time for “unleashing chaos” had arrived, promising to “declare order in the Christian strongholds before eventually seizing all of Lebanon.”

“We are not Arabs. We are the heirs of Phoenicia. Greater Lebanon will rise from the ashes of the Arab occupation.”
George Habi, Army of Christ

The FT has interviewed two defectors, analysed dozens of the group’s encrypted Telegram posts and reviewed internal documents seized during a recent Lebanese army raid. They reveal a movement that models itself on Mussolini’s Blackshirts and Franco’s Falange, complete with coloured-shirt uniforms, Roman salutes and a corporate-style organisational chart headed by a “Duce” — Habi himself.

“Their immediate objective is to create facts on the ground in Christian-majority towns,” says a senior western diplomat. “Longer term, they want to redraw Lebanon’s sectarian power-sharing system to guarantee permanent Christian dominance.”

Phoenician myth-making

Habi, 42, is a former Maronite seminary student who left the church after being expelled for “doctrinal deviations”. In his 2025 manifesto, The Phoenician Imperative, he argues that Lebanese Christians are descendants of the ancient Phoenicians, “a maritime master-race that civilised Europe before Semitic tribes polluted the coast.”

Historians dismiss the claim as pseudoscience, but it has traction among Christians who feel demographically squeezed. Lebanon has not held a census since 1932, but estimates suggest Christians now constitute barely a third of the population, down from just over half a century ago.

The Army of Christ first emerged in 2025 as a vigilante group patrolling neighbourhoods after a spate of kidnappings. It quickly grew into a structured militia with five “legions” based in towns from Byblos to Jezzine. Members pay monthly dues of $50 — a significant sum in a country where the minimum wage is worth $32 after the 2023 currency crash.

Army of Christ at a glance

  • Founded: 2025
  • Estimated strength: 900 fighters
  • Leader: George Habi, 42
  • Ideology: Phoenician Fatherland — a mix of Christian nationalism and fascism
  • Goal: A “Greater Lebanon” under Christian rule
  • Main bases: Byblos, Jezzine, Bsharri

Arms and funding

Defectors say the group has acquired at least 400 assault rifles, 30 RPG launchers and two anti-aircraft guns, mostly purchased from Syrian army officers across the border. Funding comes from a mix of diaspora donations, protection rackets and, according to one internal ledger seen by the FT, monthly subsidies totalling $38,000 from a Cyprus-registered shell company whose ultimate beneficiaries are unknown.

“They pay better than the Lebanese army,” says “Joseph”, a 24-year-old defector who asked to be identified by a pseudonym. “And they feed us — which is more than the state does.”

Regional flashpoint

The militia’s rise is alarming a region still scarred by the 1975-90 civil war. In February, Hizbullah accused the Army of Christ of firing on one of its convoys near Baalbek; the group denies responsibility but hailed the attack as “a blessed strike against the Persian occupation.”

Israel has reportedly opened back-channel contacts with Habi, viewing any Christian counter-weight to Hizbullah as strategically useful. Tehran, meanwhile, has shipped additional rockets to its Lebanese allies, according to western intelligence officials.

“We are one miscalculation away from sectarian conflict,” says the western diplomat. “If these guys provoke Hizbullah, the whole country could ignite.”

State paralysis

Lebanon’s political vacuum has accelerated the militia’s timeline. Parliament has failed 14 times to elect a president; the caretaker cabinet cannot pass a budget; and the army’s commander has warned privately that his troops lack fuel for patrols.

Into that void steps Habi, promising “order through strength.” In villages under his influence, the Army of Christ has already set up roadblocks, imposed night-time curfews and expelled several Syrian refugee families, accusing them of “ demographic warfare.”

Local priests are split. Some hail the group as defenders of the faith; others fear it is dragging Christians into a fight they cannot win. “These boys are playing with fire,” says Father Michel Aoun in Byblos. “And the rest of us will get burned.”

Whether the movement can expand beyond its mountain strongholds is an open question. Analysts note that 900 fighters, however motivated, are unlikely to seize a state of 5.5 million people. But they could trigger a wider war that draws in Israel, Syria and Iran.

“The ideology is fringe,” says Prof Nadim Shehadi at Tufts University. “But the conditions — state collapse, sectarian fear, regional meddling — are textbook 1975.”

For now, Habi appears content to bide his time. In a voice message to supporters last week he urged patience: “The Phoenician eagle will soar when the moment is ripe. Until then, we prepare, we pray, we preserve our race.”

Lebanon’s next government, if one ever emerges, will inherit a country where private armies once again set the rules. “The state is asleep,” says Joseph, the defector. “And while it sleeps, the nightmares wake up.”
