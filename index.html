Coalition Forces Launch Decisive Offensive, Retaking Territory Across West Africa

By Declan Walsh, Eric Schmitt and Ruth Maclean
August 31, 2027
Reporting from Ouagadougou, Burkina Faso
French troops advance alongside Nigerian forces near the Ivory Coast border as part of the largest coordinated ground offensive against the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel.

OUAGADOUGOU, Burkina Faso — A massive wave of coalition offensives launched in late July has shattered the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel's territorial holdings across West Africa, with forces retaking key cities and reducing the jihadist group's territory to scattered pockets of resistance.

The coordinated assault, which began on July 29th, has already resulted in the complete liberation of Togo, the near-total expulsion of Caliphate forces from Benin and Guinea, and the decisive encirclement of their remaining strongholds in Ivory Coast and Senegal. Military officials report the Caliphate has lost over 15,000 fighters in the past month alone, with casualties now exceeding 500 per day.

Current Offensive Status

Ivory Coast: Final Battle of Korhongo ongoing, 1,000+ jihadists killed since July 29th

Togo: Completely liberated with fall of Dapaong on August 7th

Benin: Kandi liberated August 11th, forces reached Niger River

Senegal: Siege of Tambacounda began August 24th, western districts captured

Guinea: Complete liberation with fall of Siguiri pocket on August 4th

Mali/Niger: Agadez captured by Tuareg forces, RN18 highway seized

"The tide has turned decisively," said General Christopher Cavoli, supreme allied commander of coalition forces. "What we're witnessing is the collapse of a terrorist state that controlled nearly 800,000 square kilometers just six months ago."

The fall of Korhogo

The most intense fighting has centered on Korhogo, Ivory Coast, where the Caliphate has made its last stand in a shrinking western pocket of the city. Since the offensive began on July 29th, coalition forces have systematically reduced the jihadist-held territory from 40% of the city to less than 5% in the western districts.

"They're fighting like cornered animals," said Colonel Antoine Moreau of the French Foreign Legion, whose forces have led the urban assault. "But they're also running out of ammunition, food, and most importantly, hope."

The battle has claimed over 1,000 jihadist lives, with coalition forces reporting minimal casualties due to precise artillery strikes and coordinated infantry advances. The remaining Caliphate fighters, estimated at fewer than 300, are believed to be preparing for a final suicidal defense.

Major Victories

Togo Liberated: Complete expulsion achieved August 7th with capture of Dapaong

Guinea Cleared: Final pocket in Siguiri eliminated August 4th

Benin Near-Free: Only extreme northern pockets remain

Tuareg Advances: ANLF forces captured Agadez, encircling Caliphate units

Togo's complete liberation

The most symbolic victory came with Togo's complete liberation on August 7th, when coalition forces captured Dapaong, the Caliphate's last stronghold in the country. The fall of the northern city marked the first time since March that an entire nation had been freed from jihadist control.

"Today, Togo is free again," President Faure Gnassingbé declared in a nationally televised address. "The dark chapter of occupation has ended, and our people can begin to rebuild their lives."

The liberation of Togo represents more than symbolic victory. The country had served as a crucial logistical hub for the Caliphate, providing access to the Gulf of Guinea and serving as a staging ground for operations into Ghana and Benin. Its loss has severed the Caliphate's southern supply routes and isolated their forces in neighboring countries.

Caliphate Collapse Indicators:
  • Fighter strength below 10,000 (down from 45,000 in March)
  • Daily casualties exceeding 500 fighters
  • Loss of all major cities except Korhogo and Tambacounda
  • Supply lines severed across all fronts
  • Mass surrenders reported in multiple sectors
  • Leadership in disarray after June decapitation strike

Tuareg resurgence

In Mali and Niger, the Azawad National Liberation Front (ANLF) has achieved stunning successes with coalition backing. The Tuareg rebel group captured Agadez, Niger's fifth-largest city, on August 15th, dealing a devastating blow to Caliphate morale.

"The Tuaregs have proven to be our most effective allies," said a senior U.S. military official. "They know the desert, they know the enemy, and they're highly motivated to reclaim their ancestral lands."

The ANLF's seizure of the RN18 highway in Mali has further isolated Caliphate forces, cutting off their primary east-west supply route. Tuareg forces are now advancing south toward Bourem and the Niger River, threatening to split the remaining Caliphate territory in two.

The siege of Tambacounda

Heavy fighting erupted in Tambacounda, Senegal, on August 24th, as coalition forces began their most ambitious siege operation yet. The city, which houses the Caliphate's regional command for Senegal and Gambia, has become a fortress of concrete bunkers and improvised explosive devices.

"Progress has been methodical but steady," said General Cavoli. "We've captured their western strongholds, but they're fighting for every building, every street corner."

The siege has already claimed hundreds of Caliphate fighters, with coalition forces employing a combination of precision airstrikes, artillery bombardment, and carefully coordinated infantry assaults. Civilian evacuation corridors have been established, though many residents remain trapped inside the city.

Benin's near-total liberation

In Benin, the liberation of Kandi on August 11th marked a turning point in the campaign. The city had served as the Caliphate's administrative capital for Benin, housing their regional governor and military command.

"Taking Kandi was like cutting off a limb," said Colonel Jean-Pierre Dubois of the French Army. "They've lost their ability to coordinate operations in Benin."

Coalition forces have since pushed to the Niger River, effectively clearing all but a few isolated pockets in Benin's extreme north. The rapid advance has surprised even optimistic military planners, who had expected a more protracted campaign.

Guinea's complete clearance

The capture of the last Siguiri pocket on August 4th completed Guinea's liberation, eliminating the Caliphate's presence in the country entirely. The operation, which involved coordinated attacks by Guinean, French, and American forces, resulted in the capture of over 400 Caliphate fighters.

"Guinea is free, and it will stay free," declared President Alpha Condé. "We will not allow these terrorists to return."

The Caliphate's collapse

Military intelligence now estimates the Caliphate's fighting strength at fewer than 10,000 men, down from a peak of 45,000 in March. Daily casualties have exceeded 500 fighters for the past two weeks, a rate that military analysts say is unsustainable.

"They're losing experienced fighters faster than they can replace them," said Dr. Andrew Krepinevich of the Center for Strategic and Budgetary Assessments. "At this rate, they'll cease to exist as a military force within months."

The collapse has been accelerated by mass surrenders, with hundreds of Caliphate fighters laying down their arms rather than face certain death. Many of those surrendering report low morale, inadequate supplies, and disillusionment with the cause.

"They promised us paradise, but they delivered hell," said one captured fighter, who requested anonymity. "Now we just want to go home."

What comes next

With the Caliphate's territory reduced to Korhogo and Tambacounda, coalition planners are already preparing for the final phase of operations. Military officials predict both cities will fall within weeks, if not days.

"We're in the endgame now," said General Cavoli. "The Caliphate as a territorial entity will soon cease to exist."

However, officials caution that eliminating the Caliphate's military capability does not end the terrorist threat in West Africa. The group may fracture into smaller cells, reverting to insurgency tactics that could plague the region for years.

"We're winning the war, but the peace will be equally challenging," said a senior State Department official. "These countries need security, governance, and economic opportunity to prevent the next terrorist group from rising."

For now, however, coalition forces and local populations are celebrating a victory that seemed impossible just months ago. The Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel, once poised to conquer West Africa, now faces extinction.

Declan Walsh reported from Ouagadougou, Eric Schmitt from Washington, and Ruth Maclean from Dakar.
