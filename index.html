Islamic nationalist movement emerges in drought-stricken Uzbekistan, seeks to establish Greater Uzbekistan under Sharia law

By CNN Staff
Updated 11:23 AM ET, Mon March 27, 2028
Severe drought conditions in Uzbekistan have devastated agriculture and led to the emergence of a new nationalist Islamic movement.

Tashkent, Uzbekistan (CNN) — A new Islamic nationalist movement has emerged in Uzbekistan amid the worst drought to hit Central Asia in decades, calling for the overthrow of the Uzbek government and the establishment of a Greater Uzbekistan governed by Sharia law, according to regional security experts and local officials.

The group, calling itself "Islomning sodiq askarlari va Islom harakati" or "The Loyal Soldiers of Islam and the Islamic Movement" in English, has gained significant traction in rural areas devastated by the ongoing drought that has crippled agriculture across the country since late 2027.

"This is a direct response to the government's failure to address the humanitarian crisis caused by the drought," said Dr. Sarah Mitchell, a Central Asia specialist at the Brookings Institution. "The movement is positioning itself as an alternative to what it sees as a corrupt and ineffective secular government."

The movement's leader, 52-year-old former economics professor Rustam Azizov, has publicly rejected the concept of a pan-Islamic caliphate, calling it "utterly pointless and foolish" in recent videos posted to encrypted messaging channels. Instead, the group advocates for a nationalist agenda focused specifically on Uzbek interests.

A nationalist, not globalist, agenda

In a manifesto released last week, the group stated its primary goals: "to overthrow the Uzbekistan Government and replace it with an Islamic State enforced under Sharia; its goal is also to create a Greater Uzbekistan to liberate all of the Uzbeks under the guidance of Allah."

The movement has attracted an estimated 15,000 to 25,000 followers, according to Uzbek government estimates, with the largest concentration in the drought-stricken Fergana Valley region. Local officials report that the group has been providing food and water to desperate farmers, winning converts in areas where government assistance has been slow to arrive.

"They came to our village with trucks of water and food," said Mahmuda Karimova, a farmer from the Namangan region who spoke to CNN by phone. "The government promised help months ago, but these men actually delivered. Now many people here support them."

The Uzbek government has responded with a significant security crackdown, deploying thousands of additional troops to the affected regions and arresting hundreds of suspected movement supporters. President Shavkat Mirziyoyev addressed the nation Sunday night, vowing to "crush this extremist threat to our secular democracy."

Regional security concerns

The emergence of the movement has alarmed neighboring countries, particularly Kyrgyzstan and Tajikistan, which fear the conflict could spill across borders. Both nations have increased security along their frontiers with Uzbekistan in recent weeks.

"This represents a significant shift in Central Asian politics," said Dr. Mitchell. "We're seeing the emergence of an explicitly nationalist Islamic movement that rejects both global jihadist networks and traditional secular governance. It's a uniquely Central Asian phenomenon."

Russian officials have expressed concern about the situation, with Kremlin spokesperson Dmitry Peskov calling for "stability and dialogue" in the region. China, which has significant economic interests in Uzbekistan through its Belt and Road Initiative, has also called for calm.

Drought fuels discontent

The drought, which began in October 2027, has devastated Uzbekistan's agricultural sector, destroying an estimated 40% of the country's cotton crop and leaving hundreds of thousands of rural residents without adequate water supplies. The World Bank estimates the drought has caused over $3 billion in economic damage.

"The drought created the perfect conditions for this movement to emerge," said Dr. Akmal Akbarov, a political scientist at Tashkent State University. "When people are desperate and the government appears ineffective, they turn to alternatives, even radical ones."

The movement has capitalized on this desperation, establishing what it calls "Islamic relief centers" in drought-affected areas. These centers provide food, water, and medical care, but also serve as recruitment centers for the group's political agenda.

International response

The United States has called for restraint from all parties, with State Department spokesperson Jennifer Parker urging "peaceful dialogue to address legitimate grievances while maintaining Uzbekistan's territorial integrity."

The United Nations has dispatched a special envoy to assess the humanitarian situation and facilitate dialogue between the government and opposition groups. UNICEF has warned that the drought has created a "perfect storm" of humanitarian need that could fuel further instability.

Turkish officials have offered to mediate, drawing on their historical and cultural ties to the region. "We understand the concerns of the Uzbek people and stand ready to help find a peaceful resolution," said Turkish Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan.

Uncertain future

As the drought continues into its sixth month, analysts warn that the movement could gain further strength if the government fails to address the humanitarian crisis effectively.

"The window for a peaceful resolution is narrowing," warned Dr. Akbarov. "If the government cannot provide basic services to its people, movements like this will continue to gain support, regardless of their ideology."

The Uzbek government has promised additional aid to drought-affected regions and has announced plans to import water from neighboring countries. However, with the movement continuing to gain followers and the drought showing no signs of ending, the situation remains volatile.

"We are prepared to sacrifice everything for a Greater Uzbekistan under Allah's guidance," said Azizov in his latest video message. "The time for half-measures and secular compromises is over. We will establish the Islamic state our people deserve."

As tensions continue to escalate, regional observers fear that Uzbekistan could face its most significant political crisis since independence, with implications that could reshape Central Asian politics for years to come.
