Anti-Indian protests spread across Middle East after Jeddah march

Nearly 30,000 protesters demand deportation of Indian nationals as religious rhetoric intensifies

By Frank Gardner, BBC Security Correspondent
23 November 2029

Nearly 30,000 protesters have marched through the Saudi Arabian city of Jeddah, demanding the deportation of Indian nationals amid a wave of anti-Indian sentiment spreading across the Middle East.

The demonstration, which originated from the "Sons of Arabs" movement, marks a significant escalation in tensions that have been building across the region in recent weeks.

Protesters chanted "The Arab homeland is for Arabs only" and "O God, grant victory to the Arabs and punish the Indians," while others shouted "Allahu Akbar, don't let the Indians replace us!" as they marched through the city centre.

[Image: Protesters waving Saudi flags during the march in Jeddah]

The Jeddah protest, which took place on Friday afternoon, has sparked similar demonstrations in other Saudi cities including Riyadh and Dammam, as well as spreading to neighbouring Kuwait where hundreds gathered outside the Indian embassy.

The protesters are demanding that the Saudi government deport nearly 2 million Indian nationals currently living and working in the kingdom, citing economic competition and cultural concerns.

Religious rhetoric intensifies

The movement has taken on an increasingly religious tone, with organisers using messaging platforms to spread their message. One recent post on the movement's Telegram channel stated: "It is the duty of all Arabs to protest against the infidel Indians."

The message continued: "Isn't it strange that the Jews brought them? And why then? To replace the Arabs! They want to exterminate the Arabs! My God! Don't let them win."

In Arabic, the message read: "من واجب جميع العرب الاحتجاج على الهنود الكفار. أليس غريبًا أن يأتي بهم اليهود؟ ولماذا إذًا؟ ليحلوا محل العرب! يريدون إبادة العرب! يا إلهي! لا تدعهم ينتصرون."

"The speed at which this has escalated from economic concerns to full-blown xenophobic protests has taken many observers by surprise"

The Saudi authorities have so far allowed the protests to proceed, though security forces maintained a visible presence throughout the demonstrations. No arrests were reported, but witnesses described a tense atmosphere as protesters marched through commercial districts popular with Indian expatriates.

Dr Hala Al-Dosari, a Saudi human rights researcher, told the BBC: "The authorities are walking a fine line. They don't want to appear to be suppressing popular sentiment, but they also cannot allow this to spiral out of control."

Economic backdrop

The protests come amid growing economic anxiety in the region. Saudi Arabia's economy has struggled to adapt to lower oil prices and increased competition, with youth unemployment remaining stubbornly high at around 28%.

"When people cannot find jobs, they look for someone to blame," said Dr Al-Dosari. "The Indian community, which is visible in professional roles, becomes an easy target."

[Image: Indian workers in Saudi Arabia - file photo]

Indian nationals form the largest expatriate community in Saudi Arabia, with approximately 2.4 million people working across various sectors from construction to healthcare and technology.

Regional spread

Similar protests have been reported in Kuwait, where hundreds gathered outside the Indian embassy on Thursday, and smaller demonstrations have occurred in Bahrain and Qatar.

In Kuwait, parliament has recently debated proposals to cap the number of Indian workers, while social media campaigns calling for boycotts of Indian businesses have gained traction.

The Indian embassy in Riyadh has issued a security advisory to Indian nationals, urging them to avoid areas where protests are taking place and to maintain a low profile.

An Indian foreign ministry spokesperson said: "We are monitoring the situation closely and have conveyed our concerns to the Saudi authorities about the safety of Indian nationals."

Historical context

This is not the first time that anti-immigrant sentiment has surfaced in the Gulf region, but the scale and religious overtones of the current protests mark a significant departure from previous episodes.

"What we're seeing is the weaponisation of economic anxiety for political purposes," said Dr Kristian Coates Ulrichsen, a Middle East expert at Rice University. "The religious rhetoric adds a dangerous dimension to what might otherwise be dismissed as economic populism."

The protests have also highlighted the growing influence of social media in organising and amplifying such movements. The "Sons of Arabs" Telegram channel has gained hundreds of thousands of followers in recent weeks, using platforms that are difficult for authorities to monitor or control.

As the sun set over Jeddah on Friday, protesters continued to gather in smaller groups, discussing their next moves. Some spoke of organising boycotts of Indian-owned businesses, while others discussed plans for further demonstrations.

"This is just the beginning," one protester, who gave his name only as Khalid, told the BBC. "We will not stop until our voices are heard and our country is returned to its rightful owners."

The coming days will be crucial in determining whether the Saudi authorities will continue to allow such protests or take steps to suppress them. For now, the Indian community in Saudi Arabia remains anxious and watchful, hoping that this wave of hostility will pass as quickly as it arrived.
