Katrakis passes 'Athens Laws' to cement total control, executes president and cancels elections

By BBC News Athens Correspondent
25 March 2027
[Image: Katrakis addressing massive crowd in Athens]
Greek Leader Nikolaos Katrakis addresses supporters in Athens after passing sweeping new laws consolidating absolute power

Greek Leader Nikolaos Katrakis has passed dozens of decrees known as the "Athens Laws" that eliminate the last vestiges of democratic governance, including the execution of the ceremonial president, the cancellation of all future elections, and the formal establishment of what he terms "total national unity" under his absolute rule.

In a dramatic session of what is now called the "National Assembly" - formerly the Greek Parliament - Katrakis announced the immediate firing and execution of President Konstantinos Tasoulas, declaring that the position of president was "abolished forever" and that he would assume all presidential powers himself as "Leader of the Hellanic State."

The Athens Laws: Key provisions

  • President executed: Ceremonial president fired and executed, position abolished
  • All power consolidated: Leader assumes presidential powers permanently
  • Ministry renamed: Defence Ministry becomes "War Ministry" under loyalist control
  • Purge of civil servants: All "disloyal" government workers fired immediately
  • Expansionist claims: Greece declares right to "Hellenic territories"
  • Elections cancelled: All future parliamentary elections permanently cancelled
  • Parliament dissolved: National Assembly members chosen by Leader only
  • Mass rally: 500,000 forced to celebrate "great victories" in Athens

The sweeping measures, announced to a massive crowd of 500,000 people in Athens - many of whom were reportedly bused in under military escort - represent the final phase in Katrakis's transformation of Greece from a parliamentary democracy to what he terms "guided national democracy."

"The time of division is over," Katrakis declared from a massive stage in Syntagma Square, flanked by military officers and members of his inner circle. "The Hellanic State now speaks with one voice - my voice."

The Athens Laws include the immediate dismissal of all civil servants deemed "disloyal" to the regime, a category that includes anyone who has ever expressed opposition to Katrakis's policies. Government workers will be vetted by a small group of loyalists who will determine their continued employment based on their "demonstrated commitment to the national rebirth."

"The president is dead, elections are cancelled, parliament is dissolved. Katrakis has achieved what even Hitler needed years to accomplish - total, absolute power with no checks, no balances, no pretense of democracy."
- Greek constitutional law professor, speaking from exile

Perhaps most significantly, the Athens Laws declare that Greece has an "inalienable right" to what Katrakis termed "the territories of the Hellenic State," explicitly mentioning lands that were part of the historical Greek state at various points in history. The laws state that the "mission of the Hellanic State is to restore the rightful borders of Greater Greece and punish the traitors and scum who backstabbed the nation in 1919."

This reference to 1919 appears to be about the territorial settlements following World War I, when Greece lost territory in Asia Minor. The language suggests Katrakis is preparing the ideological groundwork for potential territorial expansion.

The Defence Ministry has been formally renamed the "War Ministry" and placed under the direct control of one of Katrakis's most loyal supporters, former army general Dimitrios Stavropoulos, who has been given sweeping powers to "prepare the nation for its historic mission."

All future parliamentary elections have been cancelled permanently. The current parliament has been dissolved and replaced by a "National Assembly" whose members will be selected directly by Katrakis and his inner circle. The assembly will serve in an advisory capacity only, with no legislative power independent of the Leader.

The massive rally in Athens was described by state media as a "spontaneous celebration of national unity," but foreign journalists reported that participants were organized by government ministries and transported to the capital under military supervision. Many attendees appeared subdued or frightened.

"They made us come," said one Athens resident who requested anonymity. "My workplace said attendance was mandatory. The buses picked us up at 6 AM. We had to stand for eight hours while Katrakis spoke about our 'glorious future.' People were crying, but not from joy."

International reaction to the Athens Laws was swift and uniformly negative. The European Union issued a statement expressing "grave concern" about the "complete abandonment of democratic norms," while the United States called the measures "a tragic day for Greek democracy."

"Greece has crossed the final line," said EU Commission President Ursula von der Leyen. "The Athens Laws represent not just the death of Greek democracy, but the burial of its corpse and the erection of a monument to authoritarianism on its grave."

Human rights organizations expressed particular alarm about the vague criteria for determining "disloyalty" among civil servants, noting that the provisions could be used to dismiss anyone who has ever criticized the government, voted for opposition parties, or even associated with people who did.

"This is ethnic cleansing of the civil service," said the Athens-based human rights lawyer who fled to Italy last week. "They're not just firing people - they're destroying their lives. No pension, no severance, no right to work anywhere else. It's systematic punishment of dissent."

The reference to "Greater Greece" and the "betrayal of 1919" has particular historical significance. In 1919, Greece was awarded territory in Asia Minor after World War I, but lost it following military defeat by Turkey in 1922. The trauma of this loss, known as the Asia Minor Catastrophe, has long been exploited by Greek nationalists.

Katrakis's use of this historical grievance suggests he is positioning himself as the leader who will "correct" historical injustices and restore Greece to what he considers its rightful territorial extent. The vagueness of the territorial claims has alarmed neighboring countries.

"When a dictator starts talking about 'rightful borders' and historical grievances, everyone in the region should be very worried," said a Turkish diplomatic source who requested anonymity. "We've seen this movie before, and it doesn't end well for anyone."

The cancellation of elections means that Katrakis now holds power through what amounts to hereditary right - he can appoint his own successor through the National Assembly, and there are no institutional mechanisms for removing him from office.

"This is monarchy without the crown," said the exiled constitutional professor. "Katrakis has achieved what every authoritarian dreams of - power that is both absolute and permanent. The only way he leaves office now is in a coffin."

As night fell on Athens, the celebrations continued under massive spotlights, with state television broadcasting continuous footage of the "joyous crowds" while military helicopters flew overhead. The capital's streets were filled with security forces, and residents reported that dissent of any kind was met with immediate arrest.

"The Athens Laws aren't just about consolidating power," said the Italian-based analyst. "They're about erasing the very concept that power could ever belong to anyone else. Katrakis isn't just the leader - he is the state itself."
