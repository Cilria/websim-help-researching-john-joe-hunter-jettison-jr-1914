Palantir seizes control of Gabon in unprecedented corporate takeover

US data analytics group acquires entire country in $47bn deal, raising alarm over surveillance state ambitions

By Tom Wilson, Africa Editor and Hannah Murphy, Technology Correspondent
June 10, 2027 4:00 AM GMT | Updated 6 hours ago
Palantir's headquarters in Palo Alto, California. The company has acquired effective sovereignty over Gabon in a deal that analysts say 'redefines the concept of corporate power'

Libreville, Gabon — Palantir Technologies has completed what legal scholars are calling the most audacious corporate acquisition in history, purchasing effective sovereignty over the central African nation of Gabon in a $47bn deal that transforms the data analytics company into a quasi-sovereign entity.

The transaction, approved by Gabon's parliament in a late-night session on Monday, grants Palantir sweeping powers over the oil-rich nation's territory, resources and governance structures. The company has immediately begun implementing what it describes as "the world's most advanced surveillance infrastructure" across the country of 2.3m people.

"This represents a fundamental reimagining of corporate-state relations," said Dr Sarah Chen, professor of international law at Harvard University. "We've moved beyond the traditional model of multinational corporations influencing policy through lobbying. Palantir now is the state."

Key facts:
• Palantir gains control over Gabon's oil reserves, estimated at 2bn barrels
• Company will implement AI-powered social credit system monitoring all citizens
• Deal includes 75-year lease on all state-owned land
• Gabon's government retains nominal authority but reports to Palantir's board

The new surveillance state

Under the terms of the agreement, Palantir will deploy what chief executive Alex Karp described as "a surveillance system that makes China's social credit infrastructure look like a library card programme."

The system, known internally as "Project Prometheus," will monitor every aspect of citizens' lives through a network of 2.5m sensors, cameras and data collection points across Gabon's 267,000 square kilometres. Each resident will receive a dynamic social credit score that updates in real-time based on their economic activity, social interactions and compliance with corporate policies.

"This isn't about control — it's about optimisation," Karp said in a conference call with investors. "We're creating the world's first fully quantified society, where every action contributes to a more efficient, productive and predictable environment."

The company has already begun construction of 17 data centres across Gabon, each equipped with quantum computing capabilities that will process an estimated 50 exabytes of citizen data daily. Early testing of the system in Libreville's business district has achieved 99.7% facial recognition accuracy and can predict citizen behaviour up to 72 hours in advance with 89% reliability.

Land acquisition begins

As part of the takeover, Palantir has initiated what it terms "the largest land acquisition programme in modern history," purchasing virtually all privately held property in Gabon through a subsidiary entity, GabonLand Holdings.

The company is offering residents 150% of assessed property values in exchange for 99-year leases, with the option to renew at Palantir's discretion. Citizens who decline to sell face compulsory acquisition under emergency powers granted to the company through Gabon's parliament.

"They're not even pretending this is voluntary anymore," said Marc Ona, a Libreville-based human rights lawyer. "Palantir representatives are going door-to-door with purchase agreements and armed security. People are signing because they have no choice."

Property records reviewed by the Financial Times show that Palantir has already acquired 73% of Gabon's landmass, including the entire coastline and most urban centres. The company plans to relocate an estimated 600,000 residents to specially constructed "smart cities" where they will work as data processors, content moderators and AI trainers.

International alarm

The takeover has prompted emergency sessions at the United Nations and African Union, though both organisations appear powerless to intervene. Gabon's president, Ali Bongo, who retains nominal authority under the agreement, defended the deal as "the only viable path forward for our nation's development."

"Palantir offers what traditional governance cannot: perfect information and perfect control," Bongo said in a televised address. "Under their guidance, Gabon will become the most efficient society in human history."

The United States government has remained notably silent on the acquisition, though sources at the State Department confirmed that Palantir's legal team worked closely with US officials to structure the deal in compliance with international law.

"This sets a terrifying precedent," said Amnesty International's deputy director for Africa, Amina Yusuf. "If a corporation can simply purchase a sovereign nation and implement total surveillance over its citizens, then the concept of human rights becomes meaningless. We're witnessing the birth of corporate totalitarianism."

Market reaction

Palantir's stock surged 34% in after-hours trading following news of the acquisition, with analysts praising the company's bold expansion into sovereign territory. Morgan Stanley upgraded the stock to "overweight," citing "unlimited growth potential" in the nation-as-a-service model.

"This is the ultimate vertical integration," said tech analyst Jennifer Liu. "Palantir now owns the data infrastructure, the government, the land and the people. They've essentially created a real-world laboratory for their most ambitious projects without regulatory oversight."

The company has announced plans to replicate the Gabon model across other resource-rich but economically struggling nations, with rumours circulating about similar deals in the final stages of negotiation with three unnamed African countries.

As part of the agreement, Gabon's 2.3 million residents will receive Palantir stock options vesting over 10 years, effectively transforming them into shareholders in their own surveillance state. The company has promised that citizen-shareholders will receive quarterly dividends based on the nation's data processing efficiency metrics.

"This isn't colonialism — it's partnership," Karp insisted. "We're not exploiting Gabon; we're optimising it. Every citizen becomes a stakeholder in their own quantification."

The first phase of Project Prometheus is scheduled for completion by December 2027, with full surveillance coverage expected by 2029. Palantir has invited international observers to witness what it calls "the birth of post-democratic governance."
