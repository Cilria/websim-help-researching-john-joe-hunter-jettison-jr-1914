RIYADH, JANUARY 15 2030

Al-Harbi seizes power in Riyadh, proclaims Arab State

Former nationalist leader dissolves monarchy and unveils expansionist agenda after 24-hour siege

By Ahmed Al-Rashid, Middle East Editor
Analysis and market reaction below

RIYADH – Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi has completed his lightning seizure of power in Saudi Arabia, declaring the establishment of "The Arab State" after government forces surrendered following a 24-hour siege of the royal palace.

Speaking before a massive crowd outside the former royal compound, Mr al-Harbi proclaimed what he termed "the greatest day in the history of the Arab race" while unveiling plans for regional expansion and the radical transformation of the Arabian Peninsula.

Al-Harbi addresses supporters outside the former royal palace
Today will be the greatest day in the history of the Arab race! Today we have achieved victory, defeated the lying traitors, and finally purged the parasites that were the royal family.

The former Arab National Front of Homeland leader, who now styles himself simply "The Leader," announced the dissolution of the monarchy and the creation of a new governing structure centered on his personality cult. The country's formal name has been changed from the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia to "The Arab State," with Mr al-Harbi assuming absolute authority.

Institutional transformation

Mr al-Harbi's first executive orders formalized the Storm Unit, previously his private security force, as an official state institution. The unit, whose members were visible throughout Riyadh maintaining order during the transition, will serve as both a military force and internal security apparatus.

"The Storm Unit represents the vanguard of our revolution," Mr al-Harbi declared. "They will ensure the purity of our Arab State and protect it from both internal and external threats."

The new government structure eliminates traditional ministries in favor of revolutionary councils directly accountable to Mr al-Harbi. Key economic portfolios, including oil and finance, have been consolidated under loyalists who participated in the months-long protest movement that preceded the coup.

Expansionist agenda

Most significantly, Mr al-Harbi outlined an explicit expansionist agenda, calling for the "reunification" of the Arab world and the expansion of what he termed "Arab living space." The rhetoric represents a dramatic escalation from his previous focus on domestic issues and anti-Indian sentiment.

Now the goal is clear: to purge the Arab world of filth and unleash fascism upon it, the medicine it must take! There are many so-called leaders of the Arab world, but from now on, I will be the only one. I am the savior of the Arabs!

The speech marked the first time Mr al-Harbi has openly discussed territorial expansion and military conquest, raising immediate concerns among neighboring Gulf states about potential instability in the region.

Market reaction

Brent crude futures surged 18% to $97.40 per barrel in early trading following the announcement, as traders priced in heightened geopolitical risk. The Saudi riyal, which had been pegged to the dollar since 1986, came under intense pressure as foreign investors withdrew funds.

"This represents the most significant geopolitical shock to oil markets since the 1990 Iraqi invasion of Kuwait," said Helima Croft, head of global commodity strategy at RBC Capital Markets. "The question now is whether al-Harbi's expansionist rhetoric translates into action against neighboring states."

The London Stock Exchange's oil and gas index rose 4.2%, while defense contractors including BAE Systems and Lockheed Martin saw significant gains. European natural gas prices increased 12% amid concerns about potential supply disruptions.

Regional implications

Gulf Cooperation Council members held emergency consultations in Kuwait City, issuing a joint statement expressing "grave concern" about the developments while pledging to "defend the sovereignty and territorial integrity of all member states."

The United Arab Emirates has reportedly mobilized additional military assets to its border with Saudi Arabia, while Qatar has placed its armed forces on heightened alert. Jordan and Egypt, both key regional allies, have called for emergency Arab League consultations.

"The entire regional security architecture has been upended overnight," said Dr Sanam Vakil, deputy director of the Middle East North Africa programme at Chatham House. "Al-Harbi's talk of reunification and living space echoes the most dangerous episodes of 20th-century European history."

International response

The United States State Department issued a carefully worded statement calling for "restraint and respect for international law," while avoiding direct criticism of Mr al-Harbi. European foreign ministers convened an emergency session in Brussels, pledging "appropriate measures" to ensure regional stability.

Russia and China, both significant arms suppliers to regional states, have remained notably silent on the developments. Analysts suggest Moscow may view the situation as an opportunity to expand its influence in the Gulf at Western expense.

The International Energy Agency announced it would release strategic petroleum reserves if necessary to prevent supply disruptions, while OPEC members outside the region have pledged to increase production to offset any Saudi shortfalls.

Domestic consolidation

Within Saudi Arabia, Mr al-Harbi has moved swiftly to consolidate control. Former royal family members have reportedly been placed under house arrest, while senior military officers have been replaced with loyalists from his movement. The country's religious establishment has largely remained silent, though several prominent clerics have publicly endorsed the new government.

The transformation has been remarkably swift. In less than 48 hours, Saudi Arabia has transitioned from a monarchy to what regional experts describe as a fascist state with expansionist ambitions. The speed of the change has left many international observers struggling to formulate appropriate responses.

"We are witnessing the most significant political transformation in the modern Middle East," said Dr Gerd Nonneman, professor of Gulf studies at Georgetown University. "The implications extend far beyond Saudi Arabia's borders."
