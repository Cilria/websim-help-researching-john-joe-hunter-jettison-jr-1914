US airstrike kills Islamic Caliphate's supreme leader and top command in devastating blow to jihadist group

By CNN Staff
Updated 2:15 PM ET, Fri June 11, 2027
Satellite imagery shows the aftermath of the precision US airstrike that targeted the Islamic Caliphate's supreme leadership during their secret meeting in northern Mali.

Washington (CNN) — A precision US airstrike has killed the supreme leader of the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel along with his entire senior command structure, delivering what military officials describe as a "decapitation blow" that could cripple the jihadist group that has terrorized West Africa for months.

The strike, carried out by US Air Force F-35 stealth fighters at approximately 3:47 AM local time Friday, targeted a fortified compound near Tessalit in northern Mali where Caliph Abu Omar al-Sahrawi was meeting with his top 50 commanders, Pentagon officials confirmed.

DEVASTATING BLOW
All 51 senior Caliphate leaders were confirmed killed in the strike, along with an estimated 600 jihadist fighters who were attending what intelligence sources describe as a "war council" planning the group's next offensive.

"We have confirmed that Abu Omar al-Sahrawi, the self-proclaimed Caliph, was killed along with his entire senior leadership," Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said during an emergency briefing at the Pentagon. "This was a precision strike that eliminated the head of the snake."

The meeting that became a death trap

Intelligence officials had been tracking the Caliphate leadership for weeks through intercepted communications and satellite surveillance. The breakthrough came when signals intelligence picked up plans for a "supreme war council" to be held at what the group believed was a secure location deep in the Sahara.

"They thought they were safe in their desert fortress," said a senior intelligence official who spoke on condition of anonymity. "They were wrong."

The compound, built into a rocky outcrop and camouflaged from aerial surveillance, had served as the Caliphate's primary headquarters since their rapid expansion across West Africa began in March. Military analysts estimate the facility housed the group's command and control center, making its destruction particularly devastating.

According to satellite imagery reviewed by CNN, the strike left a massive crater where the compound once stood. Thermal imaging showed no survivors in the immediate aftermath.

Intelligence coup

The operation relied on unprecedented cooperation between US intelligence agencies and local sources, officials said. A defecting Caliphate commander provided crucial details about the meeting's location and timing, while NSA surveillance tracked the leaders' movements through encrypted communications networks.

"This wasn't just lucky," said retired General David Petraeus. "This was the result of patient intelligence work, careful surveillance, and perfect timing. When you get 51 terrorist leaders in one place, you don't hesitate."

The strike involved eight F-35 fighters from the 4th Fighter Squadron based in Germany, supported by aerial refueling tankers and electronic warfare aircraft. The fighters released a total of 16 precision-guided bunker-busting bombs designed to penetrate the compound's underground facilities.

Coalition celebrates milestone

The successful strike represents the culmination of the 63-nation coalition's two-month campaign against the Islamic Caliphate, which has included over 3,000 airstrikes and special forces operations across six West African nations.

"This is why we built the coalition," Secretary of State Marco Rubio told CNN. "When the civilized world unites against barbarism, we win. Today proves that."

French President Emmanuel Macron, whose forces have conducted parallel operations, called the strike "a decisive moment in the fight against Islamist terrorism in Africa."

Even China, which joined the coalition in April, praised the operation. "The elimination of terrorist leadership serves the cause of global peace and security," the Chinese Foreign Ministry said in a statement.

What happens next?

Military officials caution that while the leadership strike represents a major victory, the Islamic Caliphate still controls significant territory and maintains thousands of fighters across the region. The group has reportedly appointed regional commanders to maintain control, though their coordination capabilities remain unclear.

"We've cut off the head, but the body still has life," warned General Michael Kurilla, commander of US Central Command. "This is a major victory, but it's not the end of the war."

Intelligence sources tell CNN that the Caliphate's remaining forces appear confused and disorganized in the immediate aftermath of the strike, with conflicting orders and a breakdown in communications. This could provide an opening for coalition-backed African forces to retake territory.

Nigerian President Muhammadu Buhari called an emergency meeting of West African leaders for Saturday to discuss "exploiting this opportunity to liberate our region from terrorist control."

Global implications

The successful strike against the Caliphate leadership comes as the group had been expanding beyond West Africa, with cells reportedly active in North Africa and the Middle East. Counterterrorism experts say the elimination of the supreme leadership will likely trigger infighting among remaining commanders.

"Terrorist organizations rarely survive the loss of their charismatic leader intact," said Dr. Jessica Stern, a terrorism expert at Boston University. "We may see the Caliphate fracture into competing factions or even collapse entirely."

The operation also demonstrates the effectiveness of the unprecedented 63-nation coalition formed in response to the Caliphate's rapid expansion. Military officials say this model of international cooperation could serve as a template for future counterterrorism operations.

"We've shown that when nations put aside their differences and work together, we can eliminate even the most dangerous threats," Hegseth said. "This is how we win the long war against terror."

As news of the strike spread, celebrations erupted in several West African cities that had suffered under Caliphate rule. In Bamako, Mali, residents took to the streets waving French and American flags, while in Niamey, Niger, church bells rang in celebration.
