Al-Harbi declares 'total war' and vows military expansion

Saudi leader calls for massive military build-up and targets multiple ethnic groups in fiery speech

15 February 2030 | Middle East

Riyadh: Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi has declared his intention to launch "total war" and announced a massive military build-up, in his second major speech since taking power in January.

Addressing hundreds of thousands of supporters in Riyadh on Friday, the Arab State leader escalated his rhetoric beyond previous anti-Indian statements to target multiple ethnic groups including Yemenis, Jews, Persians and Palestinians.

[Image: Al-Harbi gestures to cheering crowd during speech in Riyadh]
Al-Harbi addressed supporters in Riyadh on Friday
Many in the foreign media claim that I hate human rights and international law, but I tell you that this is not true. I strongly dislike those so-called "rules"! They prevent us from achieving true perfection for the superior Arab race against the despicable humans we are fighting today: Indians, Yemenis, Jews, Persians, and worst of all, the most disgusting and vile of these parasites, the "Palestinians." I spit on this despicable human filth!

The speech, which ended with the crowd chanting "We want war!", represents a dramatic escalation in regional tensions and marks the first time Mr al-Harbi has explicitly called for military expansion beyond Saudi borders.

"Sympathy is weakness and cowardice; it is a trait of the weak and the cowardly. No! We Arabs are a race of war, blood, and vengeance!" he declared. "I ask you: do you want total war?"

Military build-up

According to senior officials who attended a private meeting following the speech, Mr al-Harbi has ordered the military to begin preparations for operations against Yemen within two weeks.

The announcement triggered immediate alarm across the region, with neighbouring countries calling for emergency consultations and Western governments expressing grave concern about the potential for wider conflict.

"It is time to wage an all-out war, because the only way to save the Arabs is through war!" Mr al-Harbi told the massive crowd, which had gathered from dawn to secure positions in the capital's central square.

International reaction

The United States State Department issued a statement expressing "deep concern" about the rhetoric and urging restraint. "We call on all parties to respect international law and work towards peaceful resolution of disputes," a spokesperson said.

The European Union's foreign policy chief called for an immediate de-escalation, warning that "military aggression against neighbouring states would have serious consequences."

Yemen's internationally recognised government condemned the threats, calling them "a clear violation of international law and Yemeni sovereignty."

Regional implications

Regional analysts warn that Mr al-Harbi's speech represents a fundamental shift from rhetoric to concrete military planning.

"This isn't just inflammatory language anymore," said Dr Fatima Al-Sheikh of the Carnegie Middle East Center. "When you combine explicit calls for war with a timeline for military operations, you're entering very dangerous territory."

The announcement comes amid reports of significant military movements within Saudi Arabia, including the deployment of additional troops to the Yemeni border and increased military recruitment drives.

Human rights concerns

Human rights organisations expressed alarm at the explicit dehumanisation of multiple ethnic and religious groups in Mr al-Harbi's speech.

"When a national leader describes entire populations as 'despicable human filth' and calls for their elimination, the international community must take immediate action," said Kenneth Roth, executive director of Human Rights Watch.

The United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights called for an emergency session to address what it termed "incitement to violence and potential genocide."

Economic impact

Oil markets reacted sharply to the speech, with Brent crude futures rising 8% to $115 per barrel amid concerns about potential military conflict in the world's largest oil-exporting region.

Stock markets across the Gulf Cooperation Council countries fell sharply, with the Saudi Tadawul index dropping 5% in early trading on Saturday.

"The regional economy is facing its most serious threat in decades," said Dr Nasser Saidi, former chief economist of the Dubai International Financial Centre. "If military conflict erupts, the economic consequences would be catastrophic for the entire region and global energy markets."

Additional reporting by BBC correspondents in London, Washington and Sanaa.
