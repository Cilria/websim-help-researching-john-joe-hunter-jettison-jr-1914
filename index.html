Chinese social media users openly mock Russia as 'ants' and invoke Timur's curse amid growing tensions

By David Chen, CNN
Updated 6:23 AM ET, Thu October 22, 2026

Hong Kong (CNN) — Chinese social media platforms have exploded with anti-Russian sentiment this week, with users openly mocking and insulting Russians, comparing them to "ants" and warning of historical curses in a dramatic shift from the officially sanctioned pro-Russia narratives that have dominated Chinese media since the Ukraine war began.

The viral posts, which have garnered millions of views and shares across Weibo, WeChat, and Douyin, represent a significant departure from Beijing's carefully managed diplomatic messaging, suggesting growing public frustration with China's alliance with Moscow amid recent economic tensions.

Chinese social media platforms have seen a surge in anti-Russian content in recent days

"Foolish Russia, they think we are their actual allies," one highly-circulated post from a verified Weibo account with over 2 million followers reads. "Don't they realize they are selling their nation to the US? We have won. If they even dare to wage an act or mock us, they shall face an invader worse than Timur... Russia will be destroyed to the ashbins of history, much as Mongols did so."

The post references the infamous 14th-century Central Asian conqueror Timur (Tamerlane), whose devastating campaigns against Russian principalities left lasting scars in the historical memory of the region. Chinese users have particularly seized upon Timur's famous warning to the Ottoman Sultan Bayezid I before the Battle of Ankara in 1402, adapting it for modern geopolitics.

"Russians are like ants before the Chinese dragon. They should remember what happened when Timur came to their lands. The curse of Timur will visit them again."

Another viral post, which has been shared over 3.5 million times, explicitly invokes the "Curse of Timur" - a reference to both historical Mongol invasions and the supposed misfortune that befalls those who cross the legacy of great conquerors.

"The Russians are ants beneath our feet," the post reads. "They crawl and scheme, thinking themselves mighty. But they forget history. They forget what happened when their ancestors faced Timur. The curse is real, and it will come for them again."

This wave of anti-Russian sentiment appears to have been triggered by recent economic disputes, including Russia's decision to restrict certain Chinese imports and disputes over energy pricing. Chinese netizens have expressed particular anger over what they perceive as Russian ingratitude for China's support during international sanctions.

"For four years, we supported them while the West sanctioned them," one popular comment reads. "We bought their oil, their gas, their wheat. And now they dare to lecture us about prices? They are like ants biting the hand that feeds them."

The Chinese government has not officially commented on the viral posts, though state censors have begun removing some of the more extreme content. This selective moderation suggests authorities are allowing certain anti-Russian sentiment to circulate while preventing it from spiraling completely out of control.

"This represents a fascinating shift in Chinese public opinion," says Dr. Maria Zhang, a specialist in Chinese social media at Hong Kong University. "For years, the government has cultivated pro-Russian sentiment. Now, economic tensions are revealing deep-seated Chinese nationalism that views Russia as ultimately subservient to Chinese power."

The historical references to Timur carry particular significance in Chinese nationalist discourse. Timur's empire, while not Chinese, is often co-opted in Chinese historical narratives as an example of Asian power dominating what would become Russia.

"Timur showed the world what happens when real Asian power confronts the primitive tribes of the north," another viral post declares. "The Russians should pray we do not unleash a new Timur upon them. We have the power to erase their nation from existence."

Russian officials in Beijing have reportedly expressed concern about the rhetoric to their Chinese counterparts, though publicly, Moscow maintains that China-Russia relations remain strong. The Russian embassy in Beijing declined to comment on this story.

As the economic disputes continue, Chinese social media users show no signs of reducing their anti-Russian rhetoric. If anything, the posts have become more elaborate, with some users creating detailed scenarios of Chinese military domination over Russia.

"The ants think they can challenge the dragon," reads one final viral post. "Let them remember Timur. Let them remember the Mongol hordes. Let them remember that Russia exists only because China allows it. The curse of history will visit them again, and when it does, there will be no Russia left to speak of."
