Benjman "Ben" Robert Clay Webster Sr. (1881-1946) - The coal miner who aged like he was personally cursed by God

u/AppalachianRoots 4 hours ago

Been digging into my eastern Kentucky roots and found this absolute unit of a human being who looked like death warmed over by the time he hit 50.

Benjman "Ben" Robert Clay Webster Sr. born January 20th, 1881 in fucking Prestonberg, Kentucky - which in 1881 was basically the middle of nowhere with coal dust instead of oxygen. Parents were Moses T. Webster and Jane Mae Clay, because of course his middle name was his mom's maiden name, that's how Appalachians roll.

Dude worked in the mines from the time he was 12 until the day he couldn't physically do it anymore. 34 fucking years breathing coal dust and whatever the hell else was in those Prestonberg mines. By the time he was 60, my great-aunt said he looked like he was pushing 85. Skin like old leather, eyes yellowed, teeth gone, back bent like a question mark. The man aged like he was being actively aged by a malevolent force.

Married my great-grandmother Sarah Elizabeth Daniels in 1902 when he was 21 and she was 16 (again, Appalachia). Had 8 kids, lost 3 to various diseases that don't even exist anymore. The man literally worked himself into the grave trying to feed a family of 10 on a coal miner's wage during the goddamn Great Depression.

Here's the kicker - he died July 20th, 1946 of lung cancer. Not black lung, not silicosis, but straight up lung cancer. Doctors told him to quit smoking in 1944 and he basically laughed in their faces. "What's the point?" he apparently said. "I'm already fucked." Died two years later at 65, but looked like he was 90. My grandma said the funeral home had to use a children's coffin because his body had shrunk so much from the cancer.

Anyone else got Webster family from Prestonberg? I've got photos of this guy from 1920 where he looks 50, and photos from 1940 where he looks like he's been exhumed. The transformation is honestly disturbing. Coal mining was a death sentence, but it was the only game in town.

PrestonbergArchivist 2 hours ago
I'm the archivist at the Floyd County Historical Society and we have Ben Webster's employment records from the Pond Creek Coal Company. Dude started working in 1893 at age 12 as a "trapper boy" - basically sat in the dark opening and closing ventilation doors for 10 cents a day. Worked his way up to digger, then loader, then finally a "shot firer" (the guy who set the explosives) by 1910. The records show he was making $2.75 a day by 1920, which was actually decent money for a coal miner back then. But here's the thing - the company store records show he was always in debt. Always. Like they'd advance him credit against future wages and he'd never catch up. Classic company town bullshit. Also found his death certificate - cause of death listed as "carcinoma of lung with metastasis to liver and bones." Dude was riddled with cancer. The funeral director's notes say he weighed 87 pounds at death. Eighty-seven fucking pounds. The man literally wasted away. There's a notation that the family couldn't afford a proper headstone until 1952 when one of his sons finally saved up enough money. Before that it was just a wooden cross that rotted away.

Just found some info on Ben's kids' kids - the grandchildren. This is where you see the cycle finally breaking. Out of Ben's 27 grandchildren (from his 5 surviving kids), only ONE went into the mines. One. By the 1950s, the Webster kids had seen enough. They scattered - Detroit, Cincinnati, Lexington, even California. Anywhere but those fucking mountains and those fucking mines. The one who stayed? Ben's grandson through Ben Jr. (the one who died in the explosion). That kid, Thomas Webster III, worked the same mine that killed his father for exactly 6 months before getting the hell out. Said he had nightmares about his dad's accident every single night. By the 1970s, the Webster family had gone from being a coal mining dynasty to being scattered across America working factory jobs, teaching school, running businesses. Anything but mining. The trauma had finally reached the point where it was strong enough to overcome poverty. My dad (who's a Webster descendant) said his generation was the first to go to college. Not because they were suddenly middle class, but because they refused to let their kids see what they'd seen. "Better to be poor and breathing than dead in a hole" became the family motto. Took three generations and countless deaths, but the Webster family finally escaped the mines. The cost was everything - every male relative for 60 years, the family land, the family name in Prestonberg, the family sanity. But they got out. Sometimes I wonder if Ben knew what he was buying with his life. If he understood that his suffering was the price of his great-grandchildren's freedom. Probably not. He was just trying to feed his kids. But that's how these things work - one man suffers so his descendants don't have to. The circle finally broke. Too late for Ben, too late for his boys, but in time for the rest of us.
