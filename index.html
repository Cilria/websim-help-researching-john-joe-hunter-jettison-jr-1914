Chinese netizens demand return of 'Outer Manchuria' as territorial claims intensify amid anti-Russia backlash

By David Chen, CNN
Updated 8:47 AM ET, Sat October 24, 2026

Beijing (CNN) — Chinese social media platforms have erupted with territorial claims against Russia, with millions of users posting maps showing "Outer Manchuria" as rightfully Chinese territory and openly declaring that "Russia is a fake nation" that must be "carved up" - rhetoric that has sparked furious reactions from Russian ultranationalists and raised tensions between the two powers.

The viral campaign, which began Friday evening and has since generated over 50 million views and 8 million shares across Chinese platforms, represents a dramatic escalation from the anti-Russian sentiment that first emerged earlier this week. Users are now openly challenging Russian territorial sovereignty with historical maps and territorial demands.

[Map: Chinese social media users posting historical maps of Outer Manchuria]
Chinese social media users have widely shared historical maps showing Outer Manchuria as Chinese territory

"Outer Manchuria was stolen from China by the Russian Empire in 1858 and 1860," reads one viral post with over 12 million views. "The time has come for China to reclaim what is rightfully ours. Russia is a fake nation built on stolen land - it must be carved up and returned to its rightful owners."

The posts reference the 1858 Treaty of Aigun and 1860 Convention of Peking, which ceded territory north of the Amur River to Russia - lands that Chinese nationalists refer to as "Outer Manchuria." This territory, roughly equivalent to modern-day Primorsky Krai, Khabarovsk Krai, and parts of Amur Oblast, is home to major Russian cities including Vladivostok.

"Russia is a paper tiger built on stolen Chinese land. The restoration of Outer Manchuria is only the beginning - we will take back what the Tsars stole, piece by piece if necessary."

One particularly viral image shows a map of Russia divided into multiple "independent republics," with Outer Manchuria labeled as "Chinese Territory - Under Temporary Russian Occupation." The image has been shared over 15 million times with the caption: "The future map of Asia after Russia is properly dismantled."

"The Russian Federation is an artificial construct," another widely-shared post declares. "It is time to return these lands to their original owners - China, Mongolia, Finland, Japan, and the various indigenous peoples. Russia as a concept must cease to exist."

Russian ultranationalists have responded with fury to the Chinese claims. The prominent Russian nationalist Telegram channel "Russian Legion" posted: "The Chinese dogs dare to threaten the sacred territory of Russia? We have nuclear weapons for a reason. Vladivostok has been Russian for 160 years and will remain Russian until the end of time."

Another Russian nationalist account declared: "The Chinese should remember what happened to the Japanese in 1945. We will turn Beijing into a nuclear wasteland before we surrender one inch of Russian soil to the yellow monkeys."

The territorial claims have spread beyond just Outer Manchuria. Chinese social media users have posted maps showing large portions of Siberia as "historically Chinese territory," citing the Mongol Empire's control over these regions in the 13th and 14th centuries.

"The entire Russian Far East was once part of the Yuan Dynasty," reads one post with 7 million views. "From Lake Baikal to the Pacific Ocean, these lands belong to China by historical right. The illegal Russian occupation must end."

Chinese state media has notably not censored these territorial claims, suggesting possible official approval or at least tolerance of the rhetoric. The Global Times, a nationalist Chinese tabloid, published an editorial Friday asking: "Should China reconsider its position on the 'unequal treaties' of the 19th century?"

The campaign has also taken on racial overtones, with Chinese users claiming racial superiority over Russians. "The Slavic race is degenerate and weak," one viral post reads. "They occupy Chinese lands because they lack the strength to develop their own. It is time for the superior Chinese race to reclaim what belongs to us."

Russian officials in Beijing have reportedly summoned Chinese diplomats to express their concerns, though both governments have maintained public silence on the issue. The Russian embassy in Beijing declined to comment on this story.

Some Chinese users have even begun organizing "online armies" to spread territorial claims across international social media platforms. "We must educate the world about Chinese sovereignty over Outer Manchuria," one organizer posted. "Every Chinese netizen has the responsibility to reclaim our lost territories, even if only in the digital realm."

The territorial claims represent a significant escalation in the growing anti-Russian sentiment on Chinese social media. Where previous posts merely mocked Russians as "ants," the current campaign actively challenges Russian sovereignty and territorial integrity.

As one final viral post declares: "The restoration of Outer Manchuria is inevitable. Russia can either accept the peaceful return of Chinese lands, or face the violent dismemberment of their fake nation. The choice is theirs, but the outcome is certain - Chinese territory will be returned to Chinese control."

CNN's Moscow bureau contributed to this report.
