Russian ultranationalists storm Chinese embassy in Moscow as diplomatic crisis spirals

By CNN International Correspondent
November 1, 2026

Moscow (CNN) — Tens of thousands of Russian ultranationalists stormed the Chinese embassy in Moscow on Saturday, setting the building ablaze and chanting anti-Chinese slogans as diplomatic tensions between the two nations reached a dangerous new low.

The massive protest, organized by prominent Russian nationalist groups, began peacefully near the embassy compound before spiraling into violent chaos as demonstrators broke through security barriers and breached the embassy grounds.

Russian ultranationalists breached the Chinese embassy compound in Moscow

"Death to the Yellow Menace!" and "Expel these monsters!" chanted the crowd, which had gathered in response to calls from Russian ultranationalist leaders to protest what they termed "Chinese aggression against Russian sovereignty."

Witnesses reported that the crowd, estimated at over 50,000 people, overwhelmed embassy security within minutes. Russian police appeared to stand by as protesters hurled Molotov cocktails at the building, eventually setting it ablaze as embassy staff fled to safety.

"The barbarians have broken the curse, it's time to teach them a lesson. Russian dogs have forgotten the might of the Chinese dragon."

The violence quickly spread beyond the embassy compound as ultranationalist groups began targeting Chinese businesses throughout Moscow. Several restaurants, shops, and cultural centers were vandalized, looted, or set on fire in what appeared to be coordinated attacks.

"They're destroying anything that looks Chinese," reported one witness, who asked to remain anonymous. "They smashed windows at Chinese restaurants, burned Chinese-language books, even attacked people who looked Asian. It's pure racial violence."

Russian state media has praised the protesters, with RT describing them as "patriots defending Russian dignity" while state television broadcast footage of the embassy burning with commentary suggesting China had brought this upon itself through "arrogant foreign policy."

The response from Chinese social media has been swift and furious. Users on Weibo and other platforms have called for massive protests outside Russian diplomatic missions in Beijing and Shanghai, urging the Chinese government to expel all Russian citizens.

"The barbarians have broken the curse, it's time to teach them a lesson," reads one viral post that has received over 100 million views. "Russian dogs have forgotten the might of the Chinese dragon. They will learn to fear us again."

[Image: Chinese protesters burning Russian flags]
Chinese protesters have called for retaliation against Russian interests

Chinese state media has reacted with unprecedented fury. The Global Times published a front-page editorial titled "The Russian Barbarian Must Be Destroyed," while China Daily ran a special edition with the headline "Russia: A Nation of Savages Returns to Its Primitive Nature."

"The Russian barbarians have shown their true face," declared an editorial in the People's Daily. "They are not our partners, they are our enemies. Every Russian in China must be expelled, every Russian business must be closed, every trace of Russian influence must be erased from our civilization."

The Chinese foreign ministry has summoned the Russian ambassador for an emergency meeting, while calling on the international community to condemn what it terms "state-sponsored terrorism against diplomatic facilities."

"Russia has violated every principle of international law and diplomatic immunity," said Foreign Ministry spokesperson Zhao Lijian. "This criminal act will not go unpunished. China reserves the right to take all necessary measures to protect its interests and citizens."

Russian officials have responded defiantly, with the foreign ministry suggesting that China had provoked the attack through "anti-Russian propaganda" and "imperialist ambitions in Central Asia."

"The Russian people have legitimate grievances against Chinese expansionism," said Foreign Ministry spokeswoman Maria Zakharova. "While we do not condone violence, we understand the frustration of our citizens who feel threatened by Chinese aggression."

The violence has escalated tensions to levels not seen since the 1969 Sino-Soviet border conflict. Military analysts warn that both nations have begun mobilizing forces along their borders, with China reportedly moving additional troops to its Central Asian frontier.

"We're looking at a potential military confrontation between nuclear powers," warns Dr. Dmitri Trenin, director of the Carnegie Moscow Center. "The embassy attack has crossed a red line that may be impossible to uncross. Both nations are now trapped in a cycle of escalation that could lead to war."

In Chinese cities, protesters have begun gathering outside Russian consulates, burning Russian flags and calling for revenge. Some demonstrations have turned violent, with reports of Russian businesses being vandalized in Beijing and Shanghai.

"Let the Russian dogs burn," chanted protesters outside the Russian consulate in Beijing. "They have awakened the dragon. Now they will feel our wrath."

The Chinese government has reportedly begun compiling lists of Russian citizens for potential expulsion, while state media has been authorized to launch an unprecedented propaganda campaign against Russia and its people.

As diplomatic channels between the two nations effectively collapse, regional powers are expressing alarm at the potential for wider conflict. Japan and South Korea have called for emergency UN Security Council meetings, while European leaders have urged restraint from both sides.

With the Chinese embassy still burning in Moscow and nationalist fervor reaching fever pitch in both countries, the prospect for peaceful resolution appears increasingly remote. The attack may have triggered a fundamental realignment of global power structures, with China and Russia now locked in what both sides describe as an existential struggle for survival.

CNN's international correspondents contributed to this report from Moscow, Beijing, and Washington.
