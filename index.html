Sen. Chuck Grassley Becomes First Centenarian in Senate History, Dismisses Retirement Talk

(CNN) - Sen. Chuck Grassley (R-IA) marked an unprecedented milestone in American political history Sunday, becoming the first sitting U.S. Senator to reach 100 years of age while in office. The Iowa Republican, who has represented his state since 1981, used the historic occasion to firmly reject mounting calls for his retirement.

In a press briefing outside his Senate office, Grassley demonstrated his characteristic bluntness: "The Constitution doesn't have age limits. The people of Iowa chose me to serve. That's what I'm going to do."

Senate Minority Leader Mitch McConnell, 91, praised his longtime colleague's longevity. "Chuck exemplifies the dedication to public service that defines the Senate," McConnell said during a brief ceremony in the Senate chamber.

But behind the congratulatory façade, several senators privately expressed concerns about the precedent of having centenarians in active legislative service. "We're dealing with complex 21st-century challenges, and we've got someone who was born when Calvin Coolidge was president," one Democratic senator told CNN, speaking on condition of anonymity to discuss the sensitive topic.

Grassley's milestone comes amid intensifying debate over age and cognitive capacity in government. Recent polls show 78% of Americans support mandatory retirement ages for federal elected officials.

The senator's staff maintains that he remains fully engaged in his duties, pointing to his consistent record of showing up for votes and maintaining his famous 4 a.m. workout routine. His official schedule shows he conducted 12 committee hearings last month and completed his signature 99-county tour of Iowa for the 43rd consecutive year.

Political analysts note that Grassley's current term runs until January 2029, when he would be 106. When pressed about potential plans for another re-election campaign, his office declined to comment, stating that the senator remains focused on current legislative priorities.

Dr. Margaret Chen, director of the Center for Aging Studies at Johns Hopkins University, says Grassley's case is exceptional. "While we're seeing increased longevity in the general population, maintaining this level of professional engagement at 100 is extraordinary," she told CNN.

The debate over Grassley's continued service reflects broader tensions about aging leadership in Congress, where the average age has steadily increased over the past three decades. The Senate's median age is now 65, the highest in U.S. history.

The centenarian senator's birthday celebration in the Capitol was notably modest, featuring his preferred breakfast of Dairy Queen ice cream, a nod to his famous sweet tooth and down-home image that has helped maintain his popularity in Iowa.

Through it all, Grassley remains unmoved by suggestions of retirement. As he told reporters while heading to a budget committee meeting, "There's work to be done. Age is just a number."
