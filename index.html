Young Woman's Murder in Baghdad Sparks Anti-Persian Backlash Before Suspect Found

By Farnaz Fassihi
July 13, 2027 | Updated 6:15 PM ET
Mourners carry the coffin of 24-year-old Leila Ahmed during her funeral procession in Baghdad. Credit: Ahmad Al-Rubaye/Agence France-Presse — Getty Images

BAGHDAD — Before police had even identified a suspect in the murder of 24-year-old Leila Ahmed, whose body was discovered in an abandoned warehouse in eastern Baghdad on Tuesday, Arab ultranationalists had already declared the perpetrators: Persians.

The young woman's death, which investigators say shows signs of strangulation, has become the latest flashpoint in the rapidly escalating anti-Persian sentiment sweeping across the Arab world. Within hours of the discovery of her body, social media channels were flooded with claims that "Persian gangs" were responsible, despite the absence of any evidence or suspects.

Online vigilantism

The Telegram channel "The Arab Soldiers," which has gained over 400,000 followers in recent days, posted to its subscribers: "Proof that Persians are our enemies and seek to destroy us. Another innocent Arab girl murdered by the Persian occupation."

"This is proof that Persians are scum. They roam our streets like gangs, taking our jobs and now taking our women."
— Viral social media post, shared 180,000 times

Another viral post claimed: "Persian gangs now roam Baghdad freely, supported by their agents in our government. When will we wake up?" The post, which contained no evidence, was shared more than 180,000 times across multiple platforms.

Arab ultranationalists have begun repurposing anti-immigrant rhetoric from American far-right groups, replacing references to undocumented immigrants with "Persians." One widely circulated meme featured a modified version of a MAGA poster, declaring "Persians are not our friends. They are bringing crime. They're rapists. And some, I assume, are good people."

Funeral turns political

During Ms. Ahmed's funeral on Thursday in Baghdad's Adhamiya neighborhood, what should have been a moment of mourning transformed into a political rally. Imam Abdul-Rahman al-Qaisi delivered a fiery sermon in which he declared Persians to be "kuffars" (infidels) and called for holy war against them.

"The Persians have shown their true face," the imam declared to hundreds of mourners. "They come to our lands pretending to be Muslims, but they are kuffars who seek to destroy us from within. Every Persian is an enemy of Allah and the Arab people."

The crowd responded with chants of "Death to the Persians" and "Revenge for Leila."

"My daughter was everything to me," said Leila's father, Mohammed Ahmed, 52, a shopkeeper. "But now she's become a symbol for something much bigger. Everyone is using her death for their own political purposes."

Investigation stalled

Baghdad police officials, speaking on condition of anonymity because the investigation is ongoing, say they have no suspects and no evidence pointing to any particular group. Security camera footage from the area is being reviewed, but investigators have requested the public's patience.

"We urge people not to jump to conclusions," said Major General Kadhim al-Saadi of the Baghdad Police. "Making accusations without evidence only hinders our investigation and could lead to innocent people being harmed."

But the calls for restraint have done little to slow the spread of anti-Persian sentiment online and in the streets.

Pattern of blame

This is not the first time Arab ultranationalists have blamed Persians for crimes before investigations are complete. Last month, when a series of car break-ins occurred in a affluent Baghdad neighborhood, social media users immediately blamed "Persian thieves" — even after police arrested local Iraqi teenagers.

"There's a pattern here," said Dr. Nadia al-Jabiri, a sociology professor at Baghdad University who studies sectarian tensions. "Persians have become the scapegoat for everything. Economic problems? Blame the Persians. Crime? Persians. It's the same playbook used against minorities throughout history."

The speed with which Ms. Ahmed's murder became politicized has alarmed human rights activists, who fear for the safety of Persian minorities and Shia communities across the region.

Family's anguish

For Leila Ahmed's family, the political firestorm surrounding her death has added to their grief. What they want, they say, is justice — not sectarian violence.

"My sister was not a political symbol," said Zainab Ahmed, 27. "She was a person. She loved music and wanted to be a teacher. Now she's gone and everyone is using her for their own agenda."

The family has requested privacy as they grieve, but the public nature of the case has made that impossible. Their home has become a gathering point for mourners and media, with visitors coming to pay respects — and often to share their own theories about who killed Leila.

"Everyone who comes here has an opinion about who did this," said Mohammed Ahmed. "Persians, Americans, Israelis — everyone has a theory. But no one has proof. And without proof, we have nothing but more hatred."

International concern

The United Nations has expressed concern about the "rapid spread of unsubstantiated accusations" and called on all parties to allow the investigation to proceed without interference.

"We are deeply troubled by reports that ethnic and sectarian tensions are being inflamed before facts are known," said Ravina Shamdasani, spokesperson for the UN High Commissioner for Human Rights.

But as night fell over Baghdad on Thursday, the calls for restraint seemed to be falling on deaf ears. In cafes and on street corners, the conversation had already moved from who killed Leila Ahmed to what should be done about "the Persian problem."

Meanwhile, her family prepares for a second funeral — a smaller, private ceremony where they hope to remember Leila as she was: a young woman with her whole life ahead of her, not a symbol in someone else's war.
