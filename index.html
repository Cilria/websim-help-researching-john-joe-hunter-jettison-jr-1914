Arab State stages triumphal parade after Yemen conquest

Al-Harbi declares victory in three-hour speech promising 'sacred final struggle' against Jews and Persians

By FT Middle East Staff
March 10, 2030 4:30 PM GMT

Riyadh: The Arab State on Sunday staged a massive military parade reminiscent of ancient Roman triumphs, celebrating the complete conquest and annexation of Yemen with nearly three million supporters packing the capital's streets.

Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, the Arab State's leader, delivered his angriest speech to date — a nearly three-hour address titled "The Arab Victory and Sacred Final Struggle Against the Vermin" — in which he declared that Arab forces had achieved a victory rivaling "Germany in 1940."

[Image: Military vehicles during the Roman-style victory parade in Riyadh]
Military vehicles roll through Riyadh during Sunday's victory celebration
"The Tigris and Nile rivers will bleed with the blood of enemies and inferior species in the great mission of purification. Yemen was just the beginning. Soon we will unite Syria and Egypt, and then we will finally wage war against the greatest threat to the Arab race, two satanic forces, two subhuman creatures, scum that must be eliminated: the Jews and the Persians. The Arabs will not get their land until this scum is eradicated forever!"

The crowd of nearly three million responded with thunderous chants of "Arabs Above All!" as al-Harbi outlined his vision for the region's future, promising to unite the Arab world from Morocco to Iraq through military conquest.

Military display

The parade featured columns of soldiers marching in perfect formation, followed by displays of captured Yemeni military equipment and long processions of tanks and armored vehicles. The spectacle drew comparisons to historical victory parades, though analysts noted the distinctly militaristic tone.

"This wasn't just a celebration — it was a statement of intent," said Dr. Elizabeth Monroe, senior fellow at the Council on Foreign Relations. "The scale and rhetoric suggest we're witnessing the emergence of a genuinely expansionist state."

Following al-Harbi's address, War Minister Abdullah Ibn Tariq delivered a fiery speech vowing to "cleanse the region of all impurities" and promising that "the Arab banner will fly over every capital from Damascus to Tehran."

International alarm

The speeches triggered immediate condemnation from world leaders. The United Nations Security Council scheduled an emergency session for Monday, while the European Union foreign policy chief called the rhetoric "deeply disturbing and reminiscent of the darkest chapters of the 20th century."

"We cannot ignore the explicit genocidal language used against specific ethnic and religious groups," said German Foreign Minister Annalena Baerbock. "The international community must respond decisively to prevent further escalation."

The United States State Department issued its strongest condemnation yet, with Secretary Rubio stating that Washington "will not stand idle while extremist forces threaten regional stability with rhetoric straight from the 1930s."

Regional implications

The annexation of Yemen effectively eliminates the buffer between the Arab State and its southern neighbors, raising immediate concerns about potential expansion into Oman and the UAE. Oil markets responded nervously, with Brent crude jumping 8% to $142 per barrel on fears of broader conflict.

"This changes everything," said one Gulf official who requested anonymity. "We thought Yemen would satisfy them. Instead, it's emboldened them. Syria and Egypt are mentioned by name as next targets."

The Arab State's military has swelled to over 800,000 active personnel following the Yemen campaign, making it one of the largest standing armies in the region. Military analysts note significant arms purchases from Russia and China over the past six months.

Economic impact

Regional stock markets plunged on the rhetoric, with the Saudi-equivalent Tadawul index falling 12% in early trading Monday. The UAE's markets dropped 9% as investors fled to safer assets.

"Markets hate uncertainty, and this represents the ultimate uncertainty — a regional power explicitly threatening genocide against neighboring peoples," said Mohammed Al-Erian, chief economist at Allianz.

The economic fallout extends beyond financial markets. Major international companies have begun evacuating non-essential personnel from the region, while airlines have suspended routes to several Arab State cities.

Humanitarian crisis

Human rights organizations expressed horror at the explicit nature of the threats. "We've moved from dog whistles to open calls for ethnic cleansing," said Kenneth Roth, former executive director of Human Rights Watch. "The international community's response will be a test of whether we've learned anything from past genocides."

The situation in Yemen remains dire, with reports of mass detentions and forced displacement of Yemeni civilians. Aid organizations have been largely expelled from the country, making independent verification of conditions impossible.

What's next

Diplomatic sources suggest that back-channel negotiations have effectively collapsed, with the Arab State rejecting all calls for dialogue. "They see negotiation as weakness," said one European diplomat. "Their worldview is fundamentally zero-sum — victory or death."

The parade and speeches appear designed to consolidate domestic support while signaling to the international community that the Arab State will not be deterred by sanctions or diplomatic pressure.

"We've entered a new and dangerous phase," said Dr. Monroe. "When a state openly embraces fascist ideology and threatens genocide, the question isn't whether conflict will spread — it's how quickly and how devastating it will be."
