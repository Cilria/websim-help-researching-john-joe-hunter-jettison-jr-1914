Uzbekistan protests escalate as violence spreads nationwide; at least 436 killed

By Clarissa Ward, Nick Paton Walsh and Ivan Watson
September 1, 2027, 16:47 GMT

Tashkent, Uzbekistan — Anti-government protests that began in southern Uzbekistan have exploded into a nationwide uprising, with violent clashes erupting in major cities including Samarkand, Urgench, Kokand, and Namangan, as security forces opened fire on demonstrators with live ammunition, according to multiple eyewitness accounts and human rights organizations.

At least 436 protesters have been confirmed killed across the country, according to the Uzbek Human Rights Alliance, though the group warns the actual death toll could be significantly higher as communications blackouts and security restrictions make verification increasingly difficult.

The protests, which have now entered their seventh day, have transformed from economic demonstrations against inflation into a full-scale revolt against President Shavkat Mirziyoyev's government, with protesters calling for his immediate resignation and an end to what they describe as a "criminal regime."

"Ey jinoyatchi rejim, qurollanganimizni kut!" (O criminal regime, wait until we arm ourselves!)
— Protesters in Samarkand

Escalation in Samarkand

In Samarkand, Uzbekistan's second-largest city and a UNESCO World Heritage site, witnesses described scenes of chaos as tens of thousands of protesters filled the historic Registan Square, only to be met by security forces who opened fire without warning.

"The shooting started suddenly," said Farida Ismailova, a 28-year-old shopkeeper who participated in the Samarkand protests. "One moment people were chanting, the next moment bodies were falling. I saw at least fifteen people shot in front of me. The square turned red with blood."

Video footage obtained by CNN, verified through geolocation and timestamp analysis, shows security forces in military vehicles firing automatic weapons into crowds of demonstrators. The footage, too graphic to broadcast, appears to show multiple protesters being struck by gunfire and falling to the ground.

In response to the violence, protesters have begun fighting back, hurling stones and Molotov cocktails at security forces. Several police vehicles have been set ablaze, and in Bukhara, protesters set fire to a police station after a particularly violent crackdown.

Jizzakh Massacre

The city of Jizzakh, located in central Uzbekistan, appears to have witnessed some of the worst violence. According to multiple eyewitness accounts, security forces used machine guns against demonstrators in the city's main square.

"These damned criminals were firing live rounds; I saw at least sixty people die, and I saw them firing machine guns. They didn't care who they hit – men, women, even teenagers."
— Bakhtiyor Akhmedov, Jizzakh protester

The Uzbek Interior Ministry claimed in a statement that security forces were responding to "armed terrorists" and "foreign-backed insurgents," though they provided no evidence to support these claims. The ministry reported that "several police officers" had been killed in the violence, including a police commander in Bukhara.

Anti-Government Sentiment Intensifies

As the violence has escalated, so too has the rhetoric from protesters. Demonstrators have begun chanting increasingly hostile slogans targeting President Mirziyoyev personally, including "Qotil Shavkat Mirziyoyevga o'lim, bolalarni o'ldiruvchi maxluq" (Death to the murderer Shavkat Mirziyoyev, the child-killing monster).

The protests have now spread to cities and towns across the country, including areas close to the capital, Tashkent. In Kokand, a major city in the Fergana Valley, protesters occupied the central square for hours before being dispersed by security forces using tear gas and rubber bullets.

"This is no longer about bread prices or inflation," said Dr. Alisher Abdullayev, a political analyst based in Tashkent. "This has become a revolution against Mirziyoyev and his entire regime. People are willing to die rather than continue living under this government."

Human Rights Crisis

The Uzbek Human Rights Alliance, an independent monitoring group, has documented at least 436 protester deaths since the unrest began, with the highest casualties reported in Samarkand, Jizzakh, and Bukhara. However, the group cautions that the actual death toll could be significantly higher.

"We're receiving reports of mass arrests, disappearances, and extrajudicial killings," said Nigora Akhmedova, director of the Uzbek Human Rights Alliance. "The government is trying to hide the scale of the violence, but the truth is coming out through social media and brave citizen journalists."

The government has imposed severe restrictions on internet access and social media platforms, making it increasingly difficult for information to flow out of the country. Foreign journalists have been denied entry, and several local reporters have been detained.

International Response

The United States, European Union, and United Nations have all expressed alarm at the escalating violence. The U.S. State Department issued a statement calling for "an immediate end to the violence and meaningful dialogue between the government and protesters."

"We are deeply concerned by reports of excessive force against peaceful protesters," said State Department spokesperson Sarah Chen. "The Uzbek government must respect the rights of its citizens to peaceful protest and address their legitimate grievances through dialogue, not violence."

The European Union has called for an emergency session of the UN Human Rights Council to address the crisis, while several countries have recalled their ambassadors for consultations.

Regional Implications

The unrest in Uzbekistan, Central Asia's most populous country, has raised concerns about regional stability. Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan have both deployed additional security forces to their borders, while Tajikistan has restricted cross-border movement.

Russia, which maintains significant influence in Uzbekistan, has called for "restraint from all sides" but stopped short of criticizing President Mirziyoyev directly. China, a major investor in Uzbek infrastructure projects, has expressed concern about the potential impact on its Belt and Road Initiative projects in the country.

Government Position

President Mirziyoyev has not made a public appearance since the protests began, leading to speculation about his whereabouts and grip on power. His office has released statements blaming "foreign interference" and "terrorist elements" for the unrest, while promising to restore order.

The Uzbek parliament held an emergency session on Sunday, voting to grant security forces additional powers to detain suspects without trial and to impose curfews in affected areas. Opposition figures have denounced these measures as "martial law in disguise."

Looking Ahead

As protests continue to spread and the death toll mounts, analysts warn that Uzbekistan may be approaching a tipping point. The combination of economic grievances, political repression, and now violent crackdowns has created what many describe as a "perfect storm" for revolution.

"We're witnessing the collapse of the Mirziyoyev regime in real time," said Dr. Barno Petrova of the Carnegie Moscow Center. "The question is no longer whether he will fall, but when and how violently that fall will occur."

For many Uzbeks, the violence has only strengthened their resolve to continue protesting despite the risks.

"We cannot go back to normal life after seeing our friends and family murdered in the streets. Either Mirziyoyev goes, or we all die trying to remove him. There is no middle ground anymore."
— Anonymous protester in Samarkand

As night falls across Uzbekistan, protesters in multiple cities continue to defy curfews and security force deployments, suggesting that the unrest is far from over. With communications restricted and foreign media largely barred from the country, the full scope of the crisis may not be known for days or even weeks.
