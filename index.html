Economist who predicted every recession since 1950s warns 2027 downturn could rival 2008 crisis

By BBC News Business Correspondent
1 July 2026
Robert Allard Coleman Jr., 99, has accurately predicted every US recession since 1957 from his office at Columbia University

New York City — As Robert Allard Coleman Jr. approaches his 100th birthday, the legendary economist who has accurately predicted every American recession since the late 1950s has issued his gravest warning yet: the global economy is heading for a significant downturn in early 2027 that could rival the 2008 financial crisis.

Speaking from his Columbia University office, where he still maintains a full teaching and research schedule, Coleman delivered a sobering assessment of current economic conditions that he says mirror the dangerous precedents observed before every major recession of the past seven decades.

"The warning signs are flashing red," Coleman told the BBC in an exclusive interview. "I've lived through 14 recessions, and the patterns I'm seeing now — the debt levels, the market valuations, the banking sector vulnerabilities — they look remarkably similar to 2007. Perhaps worse."

Unprecedented track record

Coleman's predictive record has achieved near-mythical status in economic circles. Since 1957, he has correctly forecast every single US recession, often years in advance. His 2006 book "The Coming Storm" accurately detailed the subprime mortgage crisis that would trigger the 2008 global financial meltdown.

Now 99 years old, Coleman remains remarkably active, publishing papers and maintaining regular contact with central bankers and finance ministers worldwide. His longevity and continued mental acuity have made him a unique figure in economics — a living bridge between the Great Depression era and today's digital economy.

The warning signs

Coleman points to several concerning indicators that have formed his prediction of a 2027 recession. Private debt levels have reached 165% of GDP, exceeding pre-2008 levels. Commercial real estate markets show signs of severe stress, with vacancy rates in major cities hovering near 30% as remote work persists.

"The banking sector is more vulnerable than people realize," he explained. "Regional banks are sitting on massive unrealized losses from the pandemic era. When commercial real estate loans start defaulting en masse, we're going to see a cascade effect that makes the Silicon Valley Bank collapse look like a minor hiccup."

Perhaps most concerning, Coleman argues, is the current state of consumer credit. Credit card debt has reached record levels, with delinquency rates rising faster than at any time since 2009. Auto loan defaults are surging, particularly among subprime borrowers.

Global implications

While Coleman's analysis focuses primarily on the United States, he warns that the coming recession will have severe global implications. China's property sector crisis, Europe's energy transition costs, and emerging market debt crises could all amplify the downturn.

"This won't be just an American recession," he cautioned. "The interconnected nature of modern finance means we'll see synchronized downturns across major economies. The difference now is that central banks have far less ammunition than they did in 2008."

Interest rates remain elevated compared to the near-zero levels that allowed aggressive monetary stimulus during previous crises. Government debt levels have also ballooned, limiting fiscal policy options.

A century of wisdom

Born in 1927, Coleman's economic perspective spans nearly a century of financial history. He began his career at the Federal Reserve in 1951, later moving to academia. His 1957 paper "Cyclical Indicators and Economic Stability" first established his reputation by accurately predicting the recession of that year.

Colleagues attribute his remarkable forecasting ability to a unique analytical framework that combines traditional economic indicators with what he calls "social mood theory" — the idea that collective psychology follows predictable patterns that influence economic behavior.

"Bob sees things the rest of us miss because he's literally lived through every modern economic cycle," said Dr. Jennifer Walsh, a former student and current Federal Reserve governor. "His institutional memory is invaluable, but his analytical framework is what sets him apart."

Preparing for impact

Coleman advises businesses and individuals to prepare now for the coming downturn. He recommends building cash reserves, reducing debt levels, and avoiding speculative investments in overvalued sectors.

"The time to prepare is when things still look good," he emphasized. "By the time official recession calls are made, it's often too late. The warning signs are there for anyone willing to see them."

Despite his grim prediction, Coleman remains optimistic about long-term economic prospects. "Recessions are painful but necessary. They clear out excesses and create opportunities for the next growth cycle. The key is being prepared and not panicking when they arrive."

Legacy and longevity

As Coleman approaches his centenary, his influence on economic thought remains profound. He has mentored three generations of economists, many now holding senior positions in governments and central banks worldwide. His final book, "A Century of Economic Wisdom," is scheduled for publication this autumn.

"I've been called a pessimist, but I'm really a realist," he reflected. "Understanding economic cycles doesn't make you popular at parties, but it does help preserve wealth and prevent suffering. If my warnings help even a few people prepare for what's coming, then I'll consider my career well spent."

Whether his latest prediction proves accurate will become clear in the coming months. But given his unprecedented track record, financial markets and policymakers would be wise to pay attention to the 99-year-old economist who has seen it all before — and warned about it first.
