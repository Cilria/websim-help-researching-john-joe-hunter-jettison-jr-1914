Anti-Indian sentiment spreads across the Middle East

As economies falter, xenophobia finds fertile ground

Nov 11th 2029 | DUBAI

On a humid evening in Riyadh, a group of young Saudi men gather in a circle, their smartphones glowing in the twilight. They are not discussing football or the latest social-media craze, but rather chanting slogans that would have been unthinkable just a few years ago. "The Arab homeland is for Arabs only!" they shout. "O God, grant victory to the Arabs and punish the Indians!"

This scene, captured in a video that circulated widely on Telegram last month, is part of a disturbing trend sweeping across the Middle East. Anti-Indian sentiment, once confined to the fringes of society, has entered mainstream discourse in several Gulf states. The Telegram group أبناء العرب (Sons of Arabs), which posted the video, has swelled to over 400,000 members since its creation in March, making it one of the fastest-growing Arabic-language channels on the platform.

The group's leader, a man known only as "Khaled" who conceals his identity behind a stylised falcon avatar, has articulated a simple message that resonates with many disaffected youths across the region. "Our goal is to kick out the Indians," he declared in an audio message posted last week. "They have taken our jobs, they have taken our prosperity, and now they threaten our very identity."

This rhetoric finds eager listeners in a region grappling with economic headwinds. Saudi Arabia's economy, long buoyed by oil revenues, has struggled to adapt to a world of lower hydrocarbon prices and increased competition from American shale producers. Youth unemployment stands at 28%, up from 19% five years ago. In this climate of economic anxiety, foreign workers—particularly the 2.4m Indians who form the largest expatriate community in the kingdom—have become convenient scapegoats.

"The economic situation has created a perfect storm," says Dr. Fatima Al-Rashid, a political economist at King Saud University. "When young Saudis cannot find jobs that match their expectations, they look for someone to blame. The Indian community, visible in their roles as engineers, doctors and managers, becomes an easy target."

The phenomenon is not limited to Saudi Arabia. In the United Arab Emirates, where Indians comprise roughly 38% of the population, calls for "Emiratisation" have grown increasingly strident. The hashtag #IndiansGoHome trended on Twitter for three days last month, following rumours—later proven false—that an Indian executive had been appointed to lead a major state-owned enterprise.

In Kuwait, parliament has debated proposals to cap the number of Indian workers at 15% of the population, down from the current 25%. "We are being colonised by curry," declared MP Khalid al-Mutairi during a particularly heated session, a comment that drew both condemnation and applause within the chamber.

"When young Saudis cannot find jobs that match their expectations, they look for someone to blame. The Indian community becomes an easy target."

The Indian government has responded with uncharacteristic alarm. External Affairs Minister Subrahmanyam Jaishankar cut short a European tour last month to handle what officials term a "diplomatic crisis". Indian missions across the Gulf have reported a 40% increase in harassment incidents against Indian nationals, ranging from workplace discrimination to physical assaults.

"This is not just about economics anymore," says Ambassador Talmiz Ahmad, a former Indian diplomat to Saudi Arabia. "This is about identity politics in societies that are struggling to define themselves in a post-oil world. The Indian community has become a symbol of everything that has gone wrong in these countries."

The Telegram group أبناء العرب exemplifies this new form of digital nationalism. Its administrators post daily content ranging from statistics about Indian "dominance" in various sectors to historical claims about Indian migration being part of a "Hindu conspiracy" to control Arab economies. The group has spawned smaller, more radical offshoots that coordinate harassment campaigns and share information about Indian-owned businesses to target.

Yet the movement's appeal extends beyond mere xenophobia. Many young Arabs see it as a form of resistance against what they perceive as the failures of globalisation. In coffee shops and university campuses, discussions about "economic sovereignty" and "cultural authenticity" have become commonplace, often code for anti-Indian sentiment.

"We welcomed them when we were rich," says Ahmed, a 24-year-old unemployed engineering graduate in Jeddah, who asked to be identified only by his first name. "Now that we are struggling, they should go back to where they came from. This is our land, our heritage, our future."

The irony is palpable. The very workers now facing hostility helped build the modern Gulf. Indian engineers designed the skyscrapers that define Dubai's skyline. Indian doctors staff the hospitals that care for Arab patients. Indian teachers educate Arab children in schools across the region.

Whether this anti-Indian sentiment will translate into meaningful policy changes remains uncertain. Gulf governments, aware of their dependence on foreign labour, have thus far resisted the most extreme demands from anti-migrant activists. But the pressure is mounting, and the economic situation shows little sign of improvement.

As Khaled, the anonymous Telegram administrator, put it in a recent post: "The Arabs are awakening. The Indians' time is ending. The desert will reclaim what is rightfully ours." The message, amplified by thousands of followers across the region, suggests that this is only the beginning of a much darker chapter in Gulf-Indian relations.
