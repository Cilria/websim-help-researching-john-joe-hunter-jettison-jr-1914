3 men arrested in Iowa on terrorism charges; FBI says they posed as Ukrainian refugees

By NBC News Staff
Updated 6:47 PM ET, Mon February 9, 2026
Cedar Rapids, Iowa, where three men were arrested on federal terrorism charges Monday.

CEDAR RAPIDS, Iowa — Three Croatian nationals who allegedly entered the United States posing as Ukrainian refugees have been arrested on federal terrorism charges after authorities discovered an arsenal of heavy weapons and explosive materials at a local residence, officials said Monday.

Viktor Marušić, 68, Sanjin Vidović, 55, and Trpimir Bašić, 51, were taken into custody by FBI agents following a months-long investigation that began when neighbors reported suspicious activity at their Cedar Rapids home, according to the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Northern District of Iowa.

The men are charged with conspiracy to provide material support to terrorists, possession of unregistered firearms, and conspiracy to use weapons of mass destruction. They appeared in federal court Monday and were ordered held without bond.

"These individuals posed a significant threat to public safety," said FBI Special Agent in Charge Timothy Gallagher. "The amount of firepower and explosive materials recovered suggests they were planning a major attack."

Weapons cache discovered

Federal agents recovered a cache of military-grade weapons during searches of the men's residence and a storage facility, including:

  • A Zastava M84 light machine gun
  • M65 submachine guns
  • DShK heavy machine guns
  • M70 assault rifles
  • Sniper rifles with high-powered scopes
  • Multiple explosive devices and bomb-making materials
  • 2 Toyota pickup trucks allegedly intended as weapons platforms

According to the criminal complaint, the men had also acquired old artillery shells they planned to convert into vehicle-borne improvised explosive devices, or VBIEDs, and were in the process of constructing "powerful IEDs" for use in what investigators believe was a planned terrorist attack.

"The amount of firepower and explosive materials recovered suggests they were planning a major attack."

Authorities also recovered tear gas and other chemical agents, along with detailed surveillance notes on potential targets throughout the Midwest, officials said.

False refugee claims

The men allegedly entered the United States in April 2025, claiming to be Ukrainian refugees fleeing the conflict in Donetsk. Immigration documents show they were processed through a federal refugee resettlement program and had been living in Iowa for approximately 10 months.

"These individuals exploited our humanitarian programs meant for those fleeing real persecution," said U.S. Attorney Richard Westphal. "Their fraudulent entry into the country represents a serious breach of our immigration system."

According to court documents, the men spoke fluent Russian and presented forged Ukrainian identification documents. Investigators believe they received assistance from a network that specializes in creating false refugee identities.

Extremist ideology

FBI agents discovered extremist materials during searches of the men's living quarters, including a photograph of Adolf Hitler and literature promoting fascist ideology, according to the complaint.

"The defendants appear to have been motivated by far-right extremist beliefs," Gallagher said. "We are investigating potential connections to international white supremacist organizations."

Investigators are working with Croatian authorities to determine the men's backgrounds and any potential ties to extremist groups in Europe. All three suspects have criminal records in their home country, though the nature of those convictions was not immediately disclosed.

Community response

The arrests have shaken the Cedar Rapids community, where the men had reportedly integrated into local social circles and attended church services regularly.

"They seemed like nice guys, always willing to help with yard work," said neighbor Margaret Thompson, 72. "I had them over for dinner several times. They said they were grateful to be in America."

Local Ukrainian-American organizations expressed outrage that the suspects had allegedly posed as refugees from their homeland.

"This is a betrayal of everything we stand for," said Olena Petrenko, president of the Iowa Ukrainian Cultural Association. "Real Ukrainian refugees are fleeing for their lives, and these criminals used that suffering as a cover story."

Ongoing investigation

The FBI is continuing to investigate the scope of the alleged plot and whether the men had accomplices either in the United States or abroad. Agents are reviewing the suspects' communications and financial records to determine if they received funding or direction from overseas.

"This investigation is far from over," Westphal said. "We are pursuing every lead to ensure there are no additional threats to our communities."

If convicted on all charges, the defendants face potential sentences of life imprisonment. Their next court appearance is scheduled for February 23.

The case highlights ongoing concerns about vulnerabilities in the U.S. refugee screening process, particularly as the country continues to accept displaced persons from conflict zones around the world.
