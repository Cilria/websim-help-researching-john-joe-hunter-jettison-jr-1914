Peru issues ultimatum to Argentina: Cede provinces or face war as Paraguay joins 'Latin Axis'

Updated 11:47 PM ET, April 30, 2027

Key Points

  • Peru demands Argentina cede Jujuy and Salta provinces within 6 days
  • Paraguay formally joins Peru in new "Latin Axis" alliance
  • Argentine military on highest alert as border tensions escalate
  • International diplomats scramble to prevent regional war

Lima, Peru (CNN) — Peru's President Antauro Humala issued a stark ultimatum to Argentina on Thursday, demanding the immediate transfer of the northern provinces of Jujuy and Salta within six days or face "total war" as Paraguay formally joined a new regional alliance dubbed the "Latin Axis."

In a fiery speech delivered to thousands of supporters in Lima's Plaza Mayor, Humala declared, "Whomever wages the most evil of war will face our wrath. We will spare nothing. We will destroy the scum."

"Argentina has six days to comply with our righteous demands. After May 6th, there will be no more talking. The blood of our ancestors demands justice."

The announcement came as Paraguay's newly-elected Frente Nacional de Renacimiento (FNR) government formally signed a military pact with Peru, creating what officials termed the "Latin Axis" - a coalition dedicated to "restoring the historic greatness of the Andean nations."

Argentine President María Eugenia Vidal immediately rejected the demands, calling them "an absurd and dangerous provocation that threatens the peace of the entire continent." Argentina has placed its military on the highest alert level and begun emergency mobilization along its northern borders.

Breaking: CNN has confirmed that Peruvian military units have begun massing along the Argentine border, with satellite imagery showing significant troop movements in the disputed regions. Paraguayan forces have also mobilized along Argentina's northeastern frontier.

The provinces of Jujuy and Salta, which border both Bolivia and Chile, have been claimed by Peruvian nationalists who argue they were "illegally separated" from the former Incan territories. The regions are home to more than 2.3 million Argentinians and represent significant lithium and copper reserves.

International diplomats expressed alarm at the rapid escalation. The Organization of American States scheduled an emergency session for Friday morning, while the United Nations Security Council planned to meet in closed session to discuss the crisis.

"This is the most serious threat to South American peace since the 1930s," said Dr. Roberto Morales, a regional security expert at Georgetown University. "The combination of Peru's military buildup, Paraguay's alliance, and these impossible demands creates a powder keg that could ignite a regional war."

Brazil and Chile announced they were coordinating a diplomatic response, with Chilean President Evelyn Matthei warning that "any attack on Argentina will be considered an attack on the democratic community of nations."

Russian President Vladimir Putin, who has maintained close ties with Humala's regime, called for "restraint on all sides" but stopped short of condemning Peru's demands. China, a major investor in Peruvian mining operations, urged "peaceful dialogue" while expressing concern over regional stability.

The crisis marks a dramatic escalation following Peru's annexation of western Bolivia in March and Paraguay's recent turn toward ultranationalist policies. Regional analysts warn that the creation of the "Latin Axis" represents a fundamental shift toward authoritarianism in South America.

As the six-day countdown begins, thousands of Argentinians have begun fleeing the northern provinces, creating humanitarian corridors toward Buenos Aires and southern regions. Argentine officials have requested international assistance in preparing for potential refugee flows.
