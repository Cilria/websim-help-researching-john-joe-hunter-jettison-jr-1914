Uzbekistan Conflict Update

Uzbekistan: Army of Islam Expands Control, Encircles Key Cities

May 29, 2028 | By the ISW Uzbekistan Team
Key Takeaway: The Army of Islam has achieved significant territorial gains across Uzbekistan in the past month, breaking out of their mountainous strongholds to encircle major cities and cut key supply routes. The group now controls large portions of the Fergana Valley and has severed the primary ground supply route to Tashkent, forcing the government to rely on air resupply for the capital.
[Map: Army of Islam territorial control as of May 29, 2028]
Fergana Valley Offensive

On May 11, 2028, the Army of Islam launched a major offensive breaking out of their traditional strongholds in the eastern mountains and advancing across the densely populated Fergana Valley. The group has successfully seized control of large portions of Namangan, Andijan, and Fergana provinces, achieving their most significant territorial expansion since the conflict began.

Most critically, Army of Islam forces have severed the M39 highway—the primary ground supply route connecting Tashkent to the eastern regions. This strategic coup has forced the Uzbek government to rely exclusively on air resupply for the capital, significantly straining their logistical capabilities and limiting their ability to conduct sustained operations in the east.

Strategic Implications:

  • Tashkent now dependent on air resupply, limiting government operational capacity
  • Army of Islam controls approximately 40% of the Fergana Valley's population centers
  • Government forces in eastern provinces increasingly isolated
  • Insurgents demonstrating sophisticated understanding of modern military logistics
Karshi Operations

In the southern theater, Army of Islam forces have achieved another significant victory by cutting the M37 highway and encircling the city of Navoi. The group has systematically destroyed key bridges and overpasses, rendering the highway to Bukhara and Samarkand "unusable for most parts of the day" according to local sources.

Navoi now faces a complete encirclement, with insurgent forces controlling all ground access routes. The city's 150,000 residents are increasingly dependent on air resupply, though Uzbek military helicopters have faced consistent harassment from man-portable air defense systems (MANPADS) that the Army of Islam has captured or acquired through unknown channels.

ISW assesses that the Army of Islam likely intends to capture Navoi before advancing on Bukhara, following their established pattern of gradually isolating and then assaulting major urban centers.

Southern Theater Developments

Perhaps most dramatically, Army of Islam forces have captured virtually all of Surkhandarya Province except for the provincial capital Termez, which they have now placed under siege. The insurgents control approximately 85% of the province, including all major towns and the vital border crossing with Afghanistan.

The siege of Termez represents the Army of Islam's most ambitious operation to date. The city, home to 180,000 residents, faces increasingly dire humanitarian conditions as insurgent forces have cut water and power supplies to several neighborhoods while maintaining a tight blockade on ground access.

Tactical Assessment

The Army of Islam's recent successes reflect a sophisticated understanding of modern insurgent warfare. Their strategy of gradually controlling the countryside while isolating urban centers represents a classic approach that has proven effective in multiple conflicts across the region.

The group's ability to coordinate operations across three distinct theaters—eastern Fergana Valley, central Karshi region, and southern Surkhandarya—demonstrates command and control capabilities that exceed most regional insurgent groups. Their use of improvised explosive devices (IEDs) to render major highways unusable shows technical competence and strategic patience.

Army of Islam Capabilities:

  • Estimated 50,000-70,000 fighters under arms
  • Control of approximately 25% of Uzbekistan's territory
  • Access to advanced anti-aircraft weapons systems
  • Demonstrated ability to conduct sustained siege operations
  • Effective information operations and propaganda campaign
International Implications

The deteriorating security situation has prompted renewed international concern, particularly from neighboring states. Kazakhstan has deployed additional forces along its border with Uzbekistan, while Kyrgyzstan has closed several border crossings in the Fergana Valley region.

Russia's continued airstrikes against Army of Islam positions have not significantly degraded the group's operational capabilities, suggesting either limitations in Russian intelligence gathering or the insurgents' effective use of civilian areas and terrain for force protection.

The conflict's expansion beyond Uzbekistan's borders remains a significant concern. ISW assesses that the Army of Islam's success could inspire similar movements across Central Asia, particularly in regions facing economic hardship and political instability.

Forecast

ISW assesses that the Army of Islam will likely continue its current strategy of gradually isolating major cities while avoiding direct assaults on heavily defended urban areas. The group appears to be preparing for a prolonged campaign designed to exhaust government forces and demonstrate the state's inability to protect its population.

The Uzbek government's response will likely involve increased reliance on airpower and special operations forces, though these capabilities face significant limitations given the Army of Islam's access to anti-aircraft weapons and the challenging terrain across much of the conflict zone.

The international community's response, particularly from Russia and China, will prove crucial in determining whether the conflict remains contained within Uzbekistan or expands into a broader regional crisis.
