Operation Nahavand breaks Iranian mountain defences

Arab forces seize Shahrekord and encircle Hamedan, but supply strains threaten momentum

June 11 2030 | Beirut

Arab forces have breached the Zagros defensive belt and seized the strategically vital city of Shahrekord, marking the most significant breakthrough of Operation Nahavand since the invasion began five weeks ago. The fall of the mountain stronghold opens the path toward the Iranian plateau, though supply constraints and mounting casualties threaten to stall the advance.

General Khalid Mohammed Bin Hussien's Army Command South captured Shahrekord on June 2nd after a four-day assault led by the 7th Motorised Division and 2nd Armoured Division. The victory came at heavy cost—Arab forces suffered an estimated 8,000 casualties in the fighting, while Iranian defenders lost nearly 15,000 men in the city's defence.

The triumph masks growing logistical strains. In a confidential memorandum dated June 9th, General Khalid warned that his forces have "barely 50 days of fuel remaining at current consumption rates" and ordered a temporary operational pause to allow resupply. The halt has allowed Iranian forces to establish new defensive positions along the road to Yasuj, where Major General Hussien Abualraham Bin Muhmmad's 2nd Army Group is now engaged in heavy fighting.

"Everything is going exactly as planned. The Persian subhumans cannot resist the superior Arab race. Soon Tehran will be conquered and Iran cleansed."
— Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, June 5th

The encirclement of Hamedan represents another tactical success for Arab forces. Army Command North, under General Turki bin Abdullah, has virtually severed the city from Iranian supply lines, trapping an estimated 45,000 Iranian troops. The battle for control has begun, with Arab commanders predicting the city's fall within days.

Simultaneously, Army Command Centre has pushed westward, capturing Malayer and preparing assaults on the defensive lines protecting Arak, home to Iran's heavy water reactor. The multi-pronged offensive aims to fragment Iranian defences and prevent concentration of forces against any single axis of advance.

Operation Nahavand: One-month assessment

  • Shahrekord: Seized June 2nd after heavy fighting
  • Hamedan: Encircled with 45,000 Iranian troops trapped
  • Supply status: General Khalid warns of 50-day fuel limit
  • Troop numbers: Arab forces now total 2.5 million men
  • Air superiority: Iranian air force "effectively neutralised"
  • Next objectives: Yasuj, Arak, Sanandaj

The Arab State has reinforced its commitment to the campaign, adding 300,000 fresh troops to bring total forces in Iran to 2.5 million men. Air force generals claim to have "effectively neutralised" Iranian air defences, allowing unrestricted bombing campaigns against military and civilian targets alike.

Major General Ahmed Al-Rashid's drive toward Sanandaj aims to complete the encirclement of Iranian forces in the western mountains. Success would trap substantial Iranian formations and open the road toward the Kurdish regions, potentially fracturing Iranian territorial integrity.

Meanwhile, forces advancing north from Bushehr have linked up with the main thrust toward Shiraz, Iran's cultural capital. The city now faces attack from multiple directions, with Arab commanders confident of capturing it within weeks.

Leader Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi's visit to Izeh on June 5th underscored the regime's commitment to total victory. Addressing senior commanders, he declared that "maximum pressure should be kept on the enemy—do not let the enemy even have a single second of rest."

Yet beneath the triumphalism, strategic challenges loom. The lengthening supply lines through the Zagros passes grow increasingly vulnerable to Iranian counterattacks. Winter approaches, and the harsh mountain climate will favour defenders. Most critically, the Arab economy strains under the burden of sustaining 2.5 million men in combat.

The international community remains paralysed. Western sanctions have failed to deter the invasion, while Russia and China continue providing tacit support through energy purchases and diplomatic cover. Iran, meanwhile, has received only limited military assistance despite urgent pleas for help.

As Arab forces prepare for the next phase toward Tehran, the question becomes whether logistical constraints will force a pause before political pressure compels negotiations. For now, momentum favours the invaders—but the mountains that protected Persia for millennia may yet extract their price.
