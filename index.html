Greek PM Katrakis passes emergency act amid claims of 'communist plot' to overthrow government

By CNN Staff
Updated 6:47 PM ET, Wed January 20, 2027
UPDATE: Greek parliament passes emergency legislation giving police sweeping powers to arrest suspected communists without trial
[Image: Katrakis signs emergency act in Athens]
Greek Prime Minister Nikolaos Katrakis signs the emergency act in Athens on Wednesday

Athens, Greece (CNN) — Greek Prime Minister Nikolaos Katrakis has signed into law emergency legislation that grants police sweeping powers to arrest suspected communists without trial, citing what he claims is a "communist plot" to overthrow his government and spark civil war.

The so-called "Katrakis Emergency Act," passed by parliament on Wednesday, allows law enforcement to detain anyone suspected of communist or Marxist sympathies for up to 260 days without formal charges. The law also permits police to use force against what the legislation terms "communist and Marxist lunatics who wish to start a civil war."

Key provisions of the emergency act

  • Police can arrest suspected communists without trial
  • Use of force authorized against "Marxist agitators"
  • Leftist newspapers must shut down within 10 days or face seizure
  • Law lasts 260 days and can be renewed
  • Applies to anyone suspected of "communist sympathies"

"We are facing a direct threat to Greek democracy from communist infiltrators who seek to plunge our nation into civil war," Katrakis said during a televised address Wednesday evening. "This emergency legislation is necessary to preserve order and protect our citizens from Marxist violence."

The act also mandates that all newspapers and media outlets suspected of having "leftist or Marxist sympathies" must cease operations within 10 days or face seizure by police authorities. The government has already compiled a list of 47 publications it claims are spreading "communist propaganda."

"We will leave no stone unturned in rooting out these communist agitators who hide behind democratic freedoms while plotting to destroy our way of life."
- Nikolaos Katrakis, Greek Prime Minister

The emergency legislation comes just 10 days after Katrakis's far-right Greek National Front party won a stunning election victory. The new law effectively suspends key constitutional protections for suspected leftists and grants the government broad powers to suppress political opposition.

Critics argue the law is a pretext to crack down on dissent and consolidate power. Opposition leader Alexis Tsipras of the now-banned Syriza party called the emergency act "a declaration of war against Greek democracy" and "the beginning of a new dictatorship."

"There is no communist plot," Tsipras said in a statement released through social media after his party's newspaper was shut down. "This is simply an excuse to silence opposition and establish authoritarian rule."

The emergency act has drawn swift condemnation from human rights organizations and international observers. The European Union issued a statement expressing "grave concern" about the legislation, while Amnesty International called it "a blueprint for political repression."

Under the new law, police can arrest individuals based on "reasonable suspicion" of communist activity, with detention renewable every 30 days. Those arrested have no right to see evidence against them or to challenge their detention in court during the emergency period.

The government has already begun enforcing the law. According to local media reports, police conducted raids on at least 23 locations across Athens early Wednesday morning, arresting dozens of suspected leftists including journalists, academics, and former opposition politicians.

State television showed footage of police officers removing boxes of documents from the offices of Efimerida ton Syntakton, a prominent left-leaning newspaper that was given until January 30th to cease operations.

The emergency act also establishes special "investigation committees" with broad powers to interrogate suspects and examine private communications. These committees can recommend further detention or release, though their decisions are not subject to judicial review during the emergency period.

Katrakis defended the law as a necessary response to what he claims is a coordinated communist insurgency backed by foreign powers, though he provided no specific evidence of such a plot.

"We have intelligence reports indicating that communist cells are planning armed uprisings in major cities," Katrakis claimed. "We cannot wait until they strike to act. We must preempt their violence and protect our democracy."

The emergency legislation is set to remain in effect for 260 days, with provisions allowing for indefinite renewal. Government spokesman Dimitris Koukoutsis indicated that the administration plans to keep the measures in place "as long as necessary to eliminate the communist threat."

International reaction has been muted, with many European leaders apparently reluctant to confront Katrakis so soon after his electoral victory. However, several EU officials privately expressed alarm at the speed with which Greece's new government has moved to suppress civil liberties.

The United States State Department issued a brief statement calling on Greece to "respect democratic norms and the rule of law," but stopped short of directly criticizing the emergency act.

Meanwhile, Greek citizens have begun organizing protests against the new law, though demonstrations require special permits under the emergency regulations. Small groups of activists have gathered in Athens' Syntagma Square, chanting slogans against what they term the "new junta."

"My grandfather fought the Nazis, my father fought the colonels, and now I have to fight this new dictatorship," said Maria Papadopoulos, 67, whose son was arrested during Wednesday's raids. "We will not let them turn Greece into a police state."
