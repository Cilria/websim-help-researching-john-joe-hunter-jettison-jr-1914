Arab media intensifies anti-Persian rhetoric amid ongoing protests

By Lina Sinjab, BBC News, Beirut
12 July 2027
Protesters in Baghdad wave banners with anti-Persian slogans during demonstrations on Saturday. Credit: Reuters

BEIRUT — Arab ultranationalists have escalated their anti-Persian rhetoric online, with viral posts claiming Iranians seek to "destroy Arabs" and that the "Persian menace" must be driven from the region, as protests against Persian communities continue for a third consecutive day.

The campaign has gained significant traction across social media platforms, with one viral Telegram post declaring "Know the enemy, Arab brothers!" alongside manipulated images depicting Persian leaders as demonic figures. The post has been viewed more than 2.3 million times and shared across multiple platforms.

Far-right memes repurposed

Arab ultranationalists have begun repurposing memes and propaganda techniques commonly associated with European far-right movements, adapting them for anti-Persian messaging. These include conspiracy theories about "replacement" and "cultural invasion," with Persian communities cast as existential threats to Arab identity.

"Persians have no history. They are fire-worshippers who seek to destroy our Arab civilization. Know the enemy, Arab brothers!"
— Viral Telegram post, viewed 2.3 million times

One particularly viral image macro shows a medieval Arab warrior with the caption: "My ancestors didn't defeat the Persian Empire just so their descendants could walk among us as 'neighbours.'" The image has been shared hundreds of thousands of times across Twitter, Telegram and Facebook.

Fact-checkers have noted that many of these posts contain historical inaccuracies, including claims that "Persians have no history" and that they remain "fire-worshippers" — a reference to Zoroastrianism that ignores Iran's predominantly Muslim population.

Economic boycotts begin

The online campaign has translated into real-world economic action, with boycotts of Persian goods gaining momentum across the Arab world. In Saudi Arabia, the hashtag #BoycottPersianProducts has been trending for two days, accompanied by videos of people removing Iranian-made goods from store shelves.

In Iraq, the Ministry of Trade reported a 60% drop in imports of Iranian goods since Thursday, though officials attributed part of this to supply chain disruptions caused by protests at border crossings.

The Dubai-based businessman Khalid Al-Mansour posted a video to his 5 million followers showing him throwing Iranian saffron into a bin, declaring: "Every dirham spent on Persian products is a dirham funding our enemies."

State media joins campaign

Several Arab state media outlets have begun incorporating anti-Persian messaging into their programming. Al-Arabiya, the Saudi-owned news channel, has been running special segments on "Persian historical aggression against Arabs," while Egypt's state television has aired documentaries about the 7th century Muslim conquest of Persia.

Iraq's state-run Al-Iraqiya channel broadcast a panel discussion titled "The Persian Threat: Then and Now," featuring commentators who warned of Iranian cultural and political influence in the region.

Dr. Hala Al-Dosari, a Saudi human rights researcher, told the BBC: "State media joining this campaign legitimises the ultranationalist narrative and makes it much harder to counter these dangerous stereotypes."

Calls for 'liberating Ahvaz'

Arab ultranationalists have begun calling for solidarity with Iran's Arab minority in the Khuzestan province, particularly in the city of Ahvaz. Posts claim Arabs in the region are "oppressed brothers" who must be "freed from Persian occupation."

The Iranian city, known as Al-Ahwaz in Arabic, has become a rallying point for protesters across the Arab world. Demonstrators in Amman carried banners reading "Free Ahvaz" and "Arabs of Iran, we stand with you."

Iran's Arab minority, which makes up approximately 2% of the country's population, has faced discrimination and marginalisation, though Tehran denies systematic oppression. The current campaign has raised fears among Iranian Arabs of increased scrutiny and potential reprisals.

International concern

The UN High Commissioner for Human Rights expressed concern about "the rapid spread of dehumanising rhetoric against Persian communities" and called on Arab governments to "take concrete steps to protect minority communities from hate speech and violence."

The Iranian foreign ministry summoned the chargé d'affaires of several Arab countries on Saturday to protest what it termed "incitement to violence against Iranian citizens."

Social media companies have faced criticism for failing to remove hate speech quickly enough. A spokesperson for Telegram told the BBC: "We are reviewing content flagged by users and will remove posts that violate our terms of service regarding hate speech."

As the online campaign continues to spread, concerns grow about its real-world impact. "What started as social media posts is becoming mainstream discourse," said Dr. Al-Dosari. "The normalisation of this anti-Persian rhetoric in state media and by public figures makes the situation much more dangerous."

With protests continuing and economic boycotts gaining traction, the region faces the prospect of deeper sectarian and ethnic divisions, fueled by a social media campaign that shows no signs of abating.
