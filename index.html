Bloodbath in Tajikistan as protests turn into armed uprising

President Rahmon's brutal crackbackfires as demonstrators seize weapons and territory
By our Central Asia correspondents
September 5, 2027, 06:00 GMT

Dushanbe, Tajikistan — What began as scattered anti-government demonstrations has exploded into full-scale armed rebellion across Tajikistan, with protesters seizing weapons, territory, and exacting brutal revenge on security forces after days of bloodshed that have left hundreds dead and the regime of President Emomali Rahmon teetering on the brink of collapse.

CONFIRMED CASUALTIES: At least 425 protesters killed, 67 security forces dead, with opposition sources claiming over 1,000 casualties nationwide

The Khujand Massacre

In the northern city of Khujand, the epicentre of the uprising, nearly 28,000 demonstrators took to the streets on Thursday chanting "Марг ба режим" ("Death to the regime") and "Марг ба Раҳмон" ("Death to Rahmon"). The response from Tajik security forces was swift and deadly.

Witnesses describe government forces and plainclothes thugs opening fire directly into the crowd with live ammunition, killing 29 protesters in the first volley. But rather than dispersing the crowd, the massacre transformed peaceful demonstrators into armed insurgents.

"They killed our brothers in cold blood. We decided that if they want war, we will give them war. No more peaceful protests — now we fight."
— Khujand protest leader, speaking via encrypted messaging app

What followed was unprecedented in Tajikistan's modern history. Protesters stormed a government compound, capturing and executing six security forces members by hanging their bodies from lampposts. A police station was overrun, with demonstrators seizing batons, shotguns, and riot shields before turning the weapons on their former oppressors.

Graphic videos circulating on social media show a protester in his thirties shouting "Ин сагҳои ҷинояткорро берун кунед!" ("Get these criminal dogs out!") before beating an Enforcement Unit member unconscious and setting the body ablaze. By 7pm, security forces had fled most of Khujand as nearly 40,000 protesters controlled key districts.

Istravshan: A killing field

The bloodshed was even more horrific in Istravshan, where the army deployed DShK heavy machine guns mounted on pickup trucks against a crowd of 40,000 protesters. The result was a massacre of staggering proportions — 396 people killed in minutes as heavy machine gun fire tore through the dense crowd.

"It was like a meat grinder," said one survivor who spoke to The Economist by phone. "People were running, falling, crawling over bodies. The sound of the guns never stopped. They just kept shooting until the square was empty."

The army's brutality sparked immediate and savage retaliation. Protesters surrounded an army barracks, setting it ablaze with homemade petrol bombs. When another barracks was breached, demonstrators threw rocks and improvised explosive devices, killing dozens of security forces personnel. Bodies of soldiers were dragged through the streets and hung from government buildings.

Nationwide conflagration

By nightfall, the uprising had spread to 160 locations nationwide, including the capital Dushanbe, where protesters battled security forces around key government buildings. Opposition sources claim nearly one million people participated in demonstrations across the country — staggering numbers in a nation of just 9.5 million.

In Dushanbe, witnesses report seeing columns of protesters marching on the presidential palace, while smoke rose from multiple government buildings. The sound of gunfire echoed through the capital as security forces struggled to maintain control.

"The regime is finished. Rahmon can kill thousands, but he cannot kill the will of an entire nation. Today we take back our country."
— Opposition leader, speaking from hiding

State media responds with threats

Tajik state media, which initially ignored the protests, has begun labelling demonstrators as "armed terrorists" and promising ruthless retaliation. A statement broadcast on national television warned that security forces would show "not an ounce of mercy" to what it termed foreign-backed insurgents.

"These are not protesters — these are criminals attempting to overthrow the legitimate government," declared a government spokesman. "They will be dealt with according to the full force of the law."

However, the government's narrative appears to have little credibility among Tajiks, who have witnessed the bloodshed firsthand through social media videos and personal accounts. The hashtag #StopRahmonGenocide was trending worldwide as international condemnation mounted.

Regional implications

The uprising represents the most serious challenge to Central Asian authoritarianism since the collapse of the Soviet Union. Unlike the successful revolution in neighbouring Uzbekistan, where the military refused to fire on protesters, Tajikistan's security forces have chosen to fight — potentially triggering a protracted civil conflict.

"We're witnessing the potential breakup of the entire regional order," said Dr. Aida Kasymova, Central Asia specialist. "If Rahmon falls, the shockwaves will be felt from Moscow to Beijing. This could be the beginning of a regional transformation that makes the Arab Spring look modest."

The endgame approaches

As night fell on September 5th, the situation remained fluid. Protesters controlled significant portions of Khujand and Istravshan, while government forces clung to fortified positions in Dushanbe. The internet remained largely cut off, making independent verification difficult.

What is clear is that Tajikistan has crossed a threshold from which there may be no return. The blood spilled in Khujand and Istravshan has created enemies who seek not reform but revenge. The peaceful protests that began in Uzbekistan have evolved into something far more dangerous — an armed insurrection that threatens to plunge the entire region into chaos.

President Rahmon, who has ruled for 35 years, faces a stark choice: negotiate his departure like his Uzbek counterpart, or attempt to crush the uprising through force, potentially triggering a civil war that could destabilise Central Asia for decades.

As one protester told The Economist: "We have nothing left to lose. They took our future, our dignity, and now our brothers' lives. Either Rahmon goes, or we all die trying."
