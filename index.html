Uzbekistan protests spread to Bukhara and Termez as death toll rises

By Alisher Mirzayev and Sarah Jones
August 30, 2027, 14:23 GMT

Tashkent, Uzbekistan — Anti-government protests that began in southern Uzbekistan's Qashqadaryo region have now spread to the historic cities of Bukhara, Navoi, and Termez, with demonstrators calling for the resignation of President Shavkat Mirziyoyev and an end to Russian influence in the country.

The protests, which entered their fourth day on Sunday, have grown increasingly violent, with at least 11 people killed and dozens more injured as security forces attempt to contain the unrest. In Bukhara, nearly 60,000 protesters occupied the city center, while in Termez, demonstrators set fire to a bank building after police opened fire with rubber bullets and tear gas.

The demonstrations, initially sparked by soaring inflation and rising food prices, have evolved into a broader anti-government movement, with protesters chanting slogans directly targeting President Mirziyoyev and his administration.

"Jinoyatchi Shavkat Mirziyoyevga o'lim!" (Death to the criminal Shavkat Mirziyoyev!)
— Protesters in Bukhara

Escalating Violence

In Termez, a city of approximately 180,000 near the Afghan border, witnesses described chaotic scenes as police attempted to disperse crowds that had gathered in the central square. Video footage verified by the BBC shows security forces firing tear gas and rubber bullets at demonstrators, some of whom responded by throwing stones and setting fire to a local bank branch.

"We were peacefully protesting when the police suddenly attacked," said Malika Karimova, a 34-year-old teacher who participated in the Termez demonstrations. "They didn't give us any warning. People were running, children were crying. One man was shot in the head with a rubber bullet right in front of me."

The Uzbek Interior Ministry issued a statement on Sunday afternoon describing the protesters as "rioters" and "criminal elements" who were attempting to destabilize the country. The ministry claimed that security forces had shown "maximum restraint" and accused foreign media of exaggerating the violence.

Anti-Government Sentiment

The slogans chanted by protesters have become increasingly hostile toward the government, with many now calling for President Mirziyoyev's resignation. In addition to the anti-Mirziyoyev chants, demonstrators have been heard shouting "Rossiya, chiqib ket!" (Get out, Russia!) and "Shavkat Mirziyoyev va Putin, jinoyatchi o'g'rilar" (Shavkat Mirziyoyev and Putin, criminal thieves).

"Biz jinoyatchilar tizimi tomonidan o'ldirilmoqdamiz, holbuki jinoyatchi mansabdorlar o'z farzandlarini Yevropaga yuborishmoqda!" (We are being killed by the criminal system, while criminal officials are sending their own children to Europe!)
— Protesters in Navoi

The spread of protests to historically significant cities like Bukhara, a UNESCO World Heritage site and major tourist destination, represents a significant escalation in the crisis. Bukhara's mayor's office was reportedly surrounded by protesters for several hours on Sunday morning before security forces pushed them back.

Government Response

President Mirziyoyev, who has led Uzbekistan since 2016, has not made a public appearance since the protests began on Friday. His office released a brief statement on Sunday calling for "calm and dialogue" while accusing "external forces" of attempting to destabilize the country.

The government has imposed strict internet restrictions in affected regions, with social media platforms and messaging apps largely inaccessible. Local journalists report that foreign media crews have been prevented from entering protest areas, and several independent Uzbek journalists have been detained.

"The authorities are clearly rattled," said Dr. Barno Abdullayeva, a political analyst at the University of Tashkent. "The speed at which these protests have spread and their anti-government nature has caught them off guard. They're trying to control the narrative while simultaneously cracking down on dissent."

Regional Concerns

The unrest in Uzbekistan has raised concerns among neighboring countries, particularly Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan, which have seen smaller solidarity protests in their southern regions. The Kazakh government has deployed additional security forces to its border with Uzbekistan, while the Kyrgyz authorities have expressed concern about the potential for regional instability.

Russia, which maintains significant economic and political influence in Uzbekistan, has called for "restraint from all sides" while expressing support for President Mirziyoyev's government. However, the anti-Russian sentiment among protesters, exemplified by chants of "Rossiya, chiqib ket!" suggests that Moscow's influence may be waning.

International Reaction

The United States and European Union have both called for restraint and urged the Uzbek government to respect the right to peaceful protest. The U.S. State Department issued a statement expressing "deep concern" about the violence and calling for dialogue between the government and protesters.

"We urge the Uzbek authorities to exercise restraint, respect the rights of citizens to peaceful protest, and address the legitimate economic grievances that have sparked these demonstrations," the State Department said.

The United Nations human rights office has called for an independent investigation into the deaths of protesters, while Amnesty International has accused Uzbek security forces of using "excessive force" against demonstrators.

Looking Ahead

As protests continue into their fourth day, the situation remains volatile. Witnesses report that despite the heavy security presence, demonstrators continue to gather in cities across southern Uzbekistan, though in smaller numbers than on previous days.

"People are scared, but they're also angry," said Akhmedov, the English teacher from Qarshi who has participated in multiple protests. "We don't know what will happen tomorrow, but we know we can't go back to the way things were. The government has to listen to us, or this will only get worse."

The economic grievances that initially sparked the protests remain unresolved, with food prices continuing to rise and the government showing no signs of implementing significant reforms. Analysts warn that without meaningful dialogue and policy changes, the unrest is likely to continue.

"This is a pivotal moment for Uzbekistan," said Dr. Petrova of the Carnegie Moscow Center. "The government can either choose genuine reform and dialogue, or it can continue down the path of repression. The choice it makes will determine the country's future for years to come."
