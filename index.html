LBJ's grandson launches uphill congressional bid in Texas

By ALEXANDRA LEVY and MARC CAPUTO
January 11, 2026
Patrick Lyndon Nugent announces his congressional campaign in Austin, Texas, January 11, 2026. | Michael Stravato for POLITICO

AUSTIN, Texas — Patrick Lyndon Nugent, the only grandson of President Lyndon B. Johnson, filed paperwork Monday to run for Congress as a Democrat in Texas' 21st District, a sprawling Hill Country seat that has been in Republican hands since 1979.

Nugent, 48, a former environmental attorney and current Austin-based consultant, formally launched his campaign with a speech outside the Texas Capitol, positioning himself as a pragmatic Democrat who can bridge the partisan divide in one of the nation's most conservative districts.

"My grandfather taught me that compromise isn't a dirty word — it's how you get things done for the people who sent you to Washington," Nugent told supporters. "I'm not running to be a progressive hero or a conservative Democrat. I'm running to be an effective representative."

The district, which stretches from north San Antonio through the Texas Hill Country to Austin's western suburbs, is rated R+8 by the Cook Political Report and has been represented by Republican Rep. Chip Roy since 2018. Roy won reelection in November with 57 percent of the vote against a Democratic opponent who spent less than $100,000.

Despite the long odds, Nugent enters the race with a famous political name, significant personal wealth, and connections to Democratic donors nationwide. He has already pledged to spend at least $2 million of his own money on the race and has hired veteran Democratic strategist Karen Finney to manage the campaign.

A Blue Dog in Trump country

In an interview with POLITICO following his announcement, Nugent said he expects to join the Blue Dog Caucus if elected — the fiscally conservative Democratic group that has dwindled to just seven members in the current Congress.

"Look, I know this district," Nugent said. "I grew up in Austin, but my family's ranch is in Blanco County. These folks don't want someone who's going to vote with AOC 95 percent of the time. They want someone who will fight for infrastructure, border security done right, and keeping government out of their business."

His campaign platform includes traditional Democratic priorities like protecting Medicare and Social Security, but also emphasizes border security, fiscal responsibility, and what he calls "sensible energy policy" that includes both renewable development and natural gas production.

"Texas needs representatives who understand that you can support oil and gas jobs while also planning for the future," Nugent said. "My grandfather built the modern Democratic Party by bringing people together, not by driving them apart."

A steep climb

Democratic strategists privately acknowledge that Nugent faces an enormous challenge in the district, which Trump carried by 12 points in 2024 and where registered Republicans outnumber Democrats by nearly 20,000 voters.

"This is a district where Democrats haven't broken 40 percent since 2012," said one national Democratic strategist who requested anonymity to speak candidly about the race. "But Patrick has resources, name ID, and he's running the right kind of campaign for that district. It's still an uphill battle, but it's not impossible if Roy stumbles or if there's a national wave."

Chip Roy, who has not formally announced his reelection bid but is expected to run, declined to comment directly on Nugent's candidacy. His spokesperson released a statement saying, "Congressman Roy looks forward to running on his record of fighting for Texas families and conservative values."

The LBJ legacy factor

Nugent's connection to LBJ could prove both an asset and a liability in the district. While Johnson's presidency is remembered for landmark civil rights and social programs, his Vietnam War legacy remains controversial, particularly among conservative voters.

"My grandfather wasn't perfect — no president is," Nugent acknowledged in his announcement speech. "But he understood that government's job is to help people, not to make their lives harder. That's what I want to bring to Congress."

The candidate has already lined up support from several prominent Texas Democrats, including former San Antonio Mayor Julián Castro and Rep. Colin Allred, who flipped a suburban Dallas seat in 2018.

"Patrick understands that to win in Texas, you need to meet voters where they are," Allred told POLITICO. "He's not trying to be something he's not, and that authenticity matters."

The money race begins

National Republicans have already signaled they won't take the race for granted, despite the district's conservative lean. The Congressional Leadership Fund, a super PAC aligned with House Republican leadership, has reserved $500,000 in San Antonio media time for fall 2026.

"We're not going to let some liberal pretender from Austin buy this seat with his family's money," said CLF president Dan Conston. "Voters in this district want a conservative fighter like Chip Roy, not a Democrat who will vote with Nancy Pelosi."

For his part, Nugent said he's prepared for the attacks and has already begun fundraising beyond his personal wealth. His campaign reported raising $340,000 in the first week of exploratory committee operations in December, including contributions from several prominent Democratic donors who see the race as an opportunity to test messaging in suburban Texas districts.

"I'm in this to win," Nugent said. "My grandfather always said that a man without a vote is a man without protection. I want to make sure every Texan in this district has that protection, and someone fighting for them in Washington."
