Tens of Thousands March in Saudi Cities, Demanding Mass Deportations

Anti-Indian protests spread to Mecca and Medina as leaders call for expulsion of all foreigners

By Ben Hubbard and David D. Kirkpatrick
December 1, 2029

JEDDAH, Saudi Arabia — Nearly 80,000 protesters marched through the heart of this Red Sea port city on Sunday, chanting nationalist slogans and demanding the mass deportation of Indian nationals and other foreign workers, in the largest display of xenophobic sentiment the kingdom has seen in decades.

The protesters, many of them young men waving Saudi flags and clad in traditional red-checkered headdresses, marched through Jeddah's commercial district chanting "Blood and Soil!" and "Indians will not replace us!" while calling for the expulsion of what they termed "foreign invaders."

The demonstration, which began after noon prayers at the Qishla Mosque, marks a dramatic escalation in anti-immigrant sentiment that has been building across Saudi Arabia for weeks. Similar protests broke out simultaneously in Mecca and Medina, Islam's two holiest cities, suggesting the movement has gained significant traction among conservative elements of Saudi society.

"The time of talk is over," said Khalid, the leader of the "Sons of Arabs" movement, who like many in the movement uses only his first name. "The time of action has begun."

The protests come as the Saudi economy continues to struggle with high youth unemployment and declining oil revenues. The unemployment rate among Saudi citizens under 30 has reached 32 percent, according to government statistics, fueling resentment toward the estimated 13 million foreign workers who comprise roughly one-third of the kingdom's population.

The "Sons of Arabs" Telegram channel, which now boasts nearly 4 million followers, has spawned copycat groups including "Arab Falcon" and "Arab Brotherhood," each promoting similar anti-foreigner messages and organizing protests through encrypted messaging platforms.

Escalating rhetoric

The movement's rhetoric has grown increasingly strident in recent weeks. On Sunday, the Arab Brotherhood channel posted: "We must prevent the great replacement of our people, and protect the future of the Arabs and our children" — a message that echoes white nationalist rhetoric about "replacement theory" that has gained traction among far-right movements in Europe and the United States.

"It is not enough that we kick out the Indians," Khalid told supporters in a voice message posted Sunday morning. "We must drive out all foreigners from our homeland."

The protests have spread well beyond their initial focus on Indian nationals, who form the largest expatriate community in Saudi Arabia with approximately 2.4 million people working across various sectors. Now protesters are calling for the expulsion of workers from Pakistan, Bangladesh, the Philippines and other countries.

"The speed with which this has escalated from economic grievances to full-blown xenophobic nationalism has taken even seasoned observers by surprise"

"What we're witnessing is the weaponization of economic anxiety into a broader nationalist movement," said Dr. Madawi al-Rasheed, a Saudi anthropologist at the London School of Economics. "The authorities allowed this to fester, and now it's threatening to spiral out of control."

Government response

The Saudi government has thus far taken a largely hands-off approach to the protests, allowing them to proceed with minimal interference. Security forces maintained a visible presence during Sunday's marches but did not attempt to disperse the crowds or make arrests.

This restraint stands in contrast to the kingdom's typical response to unauthorized demonstrations, which are normally met with swift suppression. The government's apparent tolerance has led some observers to suggest that elements within the ruling family may be sympathetic to the protesters' message.

"The fact that these protests have been allowed to continue suggests either a serious miscalculation by the authorities or tacit approval from certain quarters," said Dr. Bernard Haykel, a professor of Near Eastern studies at Princeton University.

The Saudi royal court did not respond to requests for comment on Sunday. However, a statement from the Ministry of Interior urged "all citizens to express their views peacefully and in accordance with the law."

Economic undercurrents

The protests come as Saudi Arabia struggles to implement Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman's ambitious economic reform program, known as Vision 2030, which aims to diversify the economy away from oil dependence and create millions of jobs for Saudi citizens.

But progress has been slow, and many young Saudis find themselves competing with foreign workers who often accept lower wages and fewer benefits. The average monthly salary for an Indian worker in Saudi Arabia is approximately 1,500 riyals ($400), compared to 8,000 riyals ($2,100) for Saudi citizens in similar positions.

"Why should my brother, who has a university degree, compete with someone who came here on a work visa and will accept any salary?" asked Ahmed, a 24-year-old protester who declined to give his last name. "This is our country, and we should come first."

The economic grievances are real, analysts say, but the movement has quickly evolved beyond economic concerns to embrace a broader nationalist and increasingly xenophobic agenda.

Regional implications

The protests have alarmed governments across the region, particularly those whose citizens work in Saudi Arabia. India's foreign ministry issued a travel advisory last week urging its citizens to "exercise caution" and avoid areas where demonstrations are taking place.

Pakistan and Bangladesh have also expressed concern about the safety of their nationals, while the Philippine government has begun contingency planning for the potential evacuation of its estimated 1 million citizens working in the kingdom.

"This could have serious diplomatic consequences," said Dr. Gerd Nonneman, a professor of Gulf studies at Georgetown University. "If Saudi Arabia appears to be tolerating or even encouraging violence against foreign workers, it could strain relations with key allies and trading partners."

Already, some Indian companies have begun withdrawing their employees from Saudi Arabia, and several major construction projects have been delayed as foreign workers stay home to avoid potential violence.

Historical echoes

The current protests evoke memories of similar anti-foreigner movements in the 1970s and 1980s, when Saudi Arabia experienced rapid economic growth and imported millions of foreign workers to fuel its development.

But the scale and intensity of the current movement is unprecedented, analysts say, particularly given the role of social media in organizing and amplifying the protests.

"The speed with which this has escalated from economic grievances to full-blown xenophobic nationalism has taken even seasoned observers by surprise," said Dr. al-Rasheed.

As the sun set over Jeddah on Sunday, protesters began to disperse, but many vowed to return. Smaller groups continued to gather in the city's commercial districts, where Indian-owned businesses have reported harassment and intimidation.

"This is just the beginning," said Khalid in his Sunday message. "We will not stop until our homeland is returned to its rightful owners."

The coming days will be crucial in determining whether the Saudi authorities will continue to allow such protests or take steps to suppress them. For now, the kingdom's large foreign communities remain anxious and watchful, hoping that this wave of hostility will pass before it turns violent.

Ben Hubbard is a Middle East correspondent for The New York Times. David D. Kirkpatrick is an international correspondent who has covered the region for two decades.
