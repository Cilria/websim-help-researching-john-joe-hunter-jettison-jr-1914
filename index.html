Jaysh al-Mu’minin fi Shibh al-Jazira (JASAJ) emerges from Saudi Arabia’s Rub’ al Khali

A previously unknown jihadist group calling itself Jaysh al-Mu’minin fi Shibh al-Jazira (Army of the Believers on the Peninsula), or JASAJ, announced its existence via Telegram on January 11, 2027. The Arabic-language statement, reviewed by FDD’s Long War Journal, is openly anti-monarchist, anti-Shi‘a, and framed around the liberation of the Arabian Peninsula from what it terms “traitorous rulers” and “Crusader appeasers.”

JASAJ’s inaugural communiqué is dated 11 January 2027 and bears the lengthy Arabic title reproduced below (English translation ours):

In the name of Allah, to all believers.

The Land of the Two Holy Mosques is where the true religion began, where the armies of Islam conquered the infidels and enemies of Islam, crushing all who stood in their way.

Unfortunately, however, the region has fallen into the hands of the ruling families, who are more focused on appeasing the Crusaders than on living a life of guidance.

It is the duty of all believers, God willing, through jihad, to overthrow the royal families, the House of Saud, and other traitors to Islam, such as the Rafidah (Shi‘ites) or infiltrators.

O Allah, grant victory to the lions of Islam, who will fight every battle until the final victory to restore Islam. The black banner of Islam will fly in Doha, Kuwait, Riyadh, Mecca, Medina, and Muscat, and all who are not pure will be purged by the sword.

Leadership & stated strength

Regional security sources tell FDD’s Long War Journal that JASAJ is led by Abuallah Ibn Yusuf Ibn Muhammad al-Zahara, a 51-year-old Salafi preacher originally from the small town of Baqaa’ on the Riyadh–Mecca road. Al-Zahara has no previous public militant profile, but Saudi activists describe him as a long-time “prison-yard scholar” who became more radical after 2017. Open-source estimates place JASAJ’s current strength at roughly 600 fighters, allegedly based in a desert camp deep inside Saudi Arabia’s Rub’ al Khali (Empty Quarter).

Ideology & targeting logic

JASAJ’s rhetoric is textbook neo-jihadist: takfiri toward Shi‘a (“Rafidah”), hostile to Gulf monarchies, and revanchist toward the historic “Land of the Two Holy Mosques.” The group explicitly threatens the capitals of every GCC state—Riyadh, Doha, Kuwait City, Muscat, Mecca, and Medina—promising to raise “the black banner of Islam” over each. The reference to “purging by the sword” mirrors language used by ISIS in 2014–15, though JASAJ does not yet claim affiliation with any existing branch.

Early indicators & security response

Three days after the Telegram debut, Saudi Twitter accounts circulated photos of security checkpoints south of Riyadh, and unverified reports of helicopter patrols over the Rub’ al Khali. The Saudi interior ministry has not commented publicly; however, regional diplomats tell FDD’s Long War Journal that Riyadh has quietly requested additional U.S. ISR support along the Yemeni border and the southern desert.

Assessment

JASAJ’s inaugural statement is professionally produced—HD banner, nasheed overlay, and Arabic captions—suggesting the group has access to veteran media cells, possibly from defunct ISIS or AQAP cadres. The explicit monarchist focus and GCC-wide targeting set it apart from AQAP’s Yemen-centric campaign and ISIS’s 2014 Caliphate project. Whether JASAJ can scale beyond the 600-fighter estimate will depend on its ability to tap into Saudi domestic grievances, smuggle weapons across the Rub’ al Khali, and evade both U.S. and Saudi surveillance.

For now, JASAJ is a nascent threat, but its ideological framing—anti-Saudi, anti-Shi‘a, and anti-Western—places it squarely within the post-Islamic State jihadist landscape. Long War Journal will continue to monitor the group’s media releases and any ground activity.
