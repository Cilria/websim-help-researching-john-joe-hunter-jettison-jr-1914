Arthur Paikyan's accent was so thick his own kids thought he was saying his name was "Arthur Bacon"
Posted by u/taos_trails • 4 hours ago

My great-grandfather Arthur had this fucking THICK Armenian accent that made half his stories completely incomprehensible. My mom and her siblings grew up thinking their last name was "Bacon" until they saw the marriage certificate - it's Paikyan, but with his accent it came out "BAY-kon."

The accent also meant we got like seventeen different versions of every story. My aunt Sarah swears he told her he "jumped ship in Galveston" but my uncle Mike insists it was "came through Ellis Island with gold coins sewn in coat." Same fucking story, just filtered through that accent and their selective memory.

But here's what's fucking wild - the accent got THICKER as he got older. Like in the 1940s he sounded like a guy who'd been in America 20 years. By the 1970s he sounded like he'd just gotten off the boat yesterday. My theory? He was leaning into it on purpose, like he wanted to remind everyone he was foreign.

Anyone else deal with ancestors whose accent basically became their personality? Also, has anyone figured out what the fuck "wine cellar under monastery gates" actually means? Because I'm pretty sure that's not a real thing.

u/linguistics_nightmare • 30 minutes ago

Working theory: Arthur's accent wasn't just Armenian, it was specifically WESTERN Armenian with a heavy dose of "I've been in America for 60 years and forgot half my vocabulary." Western Armenian has sounds that English doesn't have, so words get mangled in predictable ways.

"Paikyan" → "Bacon" makes perfect sense if you know that Armenian "p" can sound like English "b" and the "ky" cluster becomes a hard "k" sound. Plus, Americans are fucking terrible at non-English names.

The monastery thing is probably "մատուռ" (matoor) meaning "small chapel" + "գինու տուն" (gino doon) meaning "wine house." Put it through 60 years of accent evolution and you get "wine cellar under monastery gates."
