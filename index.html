Military coup topples Peruvian government as Antauro Humala takes power

Lima, Peru (CNN) — Peruvian President Dina Boluarte was removed from office Sunday evening in a military coup led by retired army major Antauro Humala, who has assumed control of the South American nation just weeks before scheduled elections.

The coup began at approximately 6:30 p.m. local time when military units surrounded the Government Palace in Lima. Boluarte was escorted out of the building by armed forces personnel, according to eyewitness accounts and local media reports.

In a brief televised address at 8:15 p.m., Humala announced the dissolution of Congress and the suspension of the constitution, declaring himself "provisional president of the Peruvian Republic" and promising to "restore order and national dignity."

"The corrupt oligarchy that has governed Peru for decades has been removed from power," Humala said in his address, wearing military fatigues. "We will establish a government that truly represents the interests of the Peruvian people."

The coup comes after months of political instability and growing support for Humala's ethnocacerist movement, which combines indigenous nationalism with socialist economic policies. The scheduled November 2026 presidential elections have been indefinitely postponed.

Regional leaders have begun responding to the crisis. Chilean President Gabriel Boric called for an "urgent meeting" of the Organization of American States, while Colombian President Gustavo Petro demanded "the immediate restoration of democratic order in Peru."

DEVELOPING: The U.S. State Department has issued a statement calling for "the release of President Boluarte and the restoration of constitutional order." The American embassy in Lima has advised U.S. citizens to shelter in place.

International flights to and from Lima's Jorge Chávez International Airport have been suspended, and borders have been closed until further notice, according to the transport ministry.

Humala, 63, is the brother of former President Ollanta Humala, who served from 2011 to 2016. The younger Humala was imprisoned from 2011 to 2022 for his role in the 2005 Andahuaylas uprising, during which he led a military revolt against the government.

Since his release, Antauro Humala has built a significant political following through his ethnocacerist movement, which draws support from indigenous communities and military veterans who feel marginalized by traditional political parties.

The coup marks the culmination of weeks of growing tension, as Humala's supporters had been staging increasingly large demonstrations in Lima demanding his release from house arrest and calling for revolutionary change.

As of 11:30 p.m. local time, military checkpoints have been established throughout Lima, and a curfew has been imposed from 10 p.m. to 6 a.m. until further notice.

The whereabouts of President Boluarte remain unknown. Government ministers have either been detained or have gone into hiding, according to local media reports.

Regional analysts note that this is the first successful military coup in South America since the 2009 removal of Honduran President Manuel Zelaya, marking a significant setback for democratic institutions in the region.
