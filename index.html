George Wallace III announces Alabama governor bid, dominates among voters 80+ in new poll

By Stephen Collinson, CNN Politics
Updated 6:23 PM ET, Thu March 11, 2027
George Wallace III announces his candidacy for Alabama governor in Montgomery, Alabama, on March 11, 2027.

Montgomery, Alabama (CNN) — George Wallace III, son of the late Alabama governor and presidential candidate George Wallace, formally announced his candidacy for governor Thursday, promising to run a "traditional Democratic campaign" focused on states' rights, immediately shaking up the race with a surprising showing among the state's oldest voters.

Speaking to a crowd of approximately 500 supporters at the Alabama State Capitol, Wallace III, 74, declared his intention to return Alabama to what he called "the traditional values that made this state great" while emphasizing his commitment to a states' rights platform reminiscent of his father's controversial but politically successful era.

"I am running as a Democrat in the tradition of my father — a tradition that puts Alabama first, that believes in the rights of states to govern themselves, and that understands the values that built this great nation"

The announcement comes as a new CNN poll conducted by SSRS shows Wallace III with commanding support among voters over 80 years old, capturing 83% of that demographic in a hypothetical Democratic primary matchup against incumbent Governor Will Ainsworth, a Republican who is term-limited and cannot seek re-election.

The poll, which surveyed 1,004 registered Alabama voters, reveals a stark generational divide in the state. Among voters under 65, Wallace III trails significantly, drawing only 23% support among likely Democratic primary voters. However, among those 80 and older — a small but influential bloc in Alabama's Democratic primary — he dominates the field.

States' rights focus

Wallace III's announcement speech focused heavily on themes of federal overreach and state sovereignty, echoing his father's famous 1963 inaugural address when the senior Wallace declared "segregation now, segregation tomorrow, segregation forever" — though the younger Wallace steered clear of racial rhetoric in his 2027 announcement.

"Washington bureaucrats have been telling Alabama what to do for too long," Wallace III said. "Whether it's education, healthcare, or environmental policy, the people of Alabama should decide what's best for Alabama."

His campaign platform includes opposition to federal education mandates, support for stricter state-level voting requirements, and resistance to what he calls "federal interference in Alabama's traditional way of life."

The candidate's emphasis on states' rights has drawn criticism from some quarters who see it as coded language harkening back to Alabama's segregationist past, though Wallace III has repeatedly denied any racial motivations behind his platform.

"This is about Alabama controlling its own destiny, not about turning back the clock on civil rights"

Generational appeal

Political analysts say Wallace III's appeal to older voters stems from nostalgia for what many remember as a simpler time in Alabama politics, as well as name recognition that remains powerful in the state nearly five decades after his father's last gubernatorial campaign.

"Among voters who came of age during the Wallace era, there's still significant respect for the Wallace political machine," said Dr. Angela Robertson, a political science professor at the University of Alabama. "Many older voters associate the Wallace name with a strong, assertive style of leadership that they feel is missing from today's politics."

The poll shows that among voters 80+, Wallace III's support cuts across racial lines, drawing 81% of white voters and 79% of Black voters in that age group — though Black voters over 80 represent a relatively small portion of the Democratic primary electorate.

Democratic establishment response

Wallace III's entry into the race has created an awkward situation for Alabama Democratic Party leaders, who have been working to modernize the party's image and broaden its appeal beyond traditional demographics.

"The Alabama Democratic Party welcomes all qualified candidates who wish to participate in our democratic process," party chairwoman Nancy Worley said in a carefully worded statement. "We look forward to a robust primary that addresses the issues facing all Alabamians in 2027."

Privately, some Democratic officials expressed concern that Wallace III's candidacy could damage the party's efforts to attract younger, more diverse voters.

Other declared Democratic candidates include Tuscaloosa Mayor Walt Maddox, state Representative Anthony Daniels, and former state Senator Doug Jones, who previously served in the U.S. Senate.

Historical context

George Wallace Sr. served four terms as Alabama governor between 1963 and 1987, becoming one of the most influential and controversial political figures of the civil rights era. His 1963 "stand in the schoolhouse door" at the University of Alabama, attempting to block the integration of the university, became an iconic moment in the civil rights movement.

Wallace III has distanced himself from his father's segregationist positions while embracing his populist, states' rights rhetoric. The younger Wallace worked as an assistant state attorney general and later served as a state circuit judge before entering private law practice.

"My father was a product of his times," Wallace III said when asked about his father's legacy. "I am a product of mine. But the principles of states' rights and limited federal government are timeless and necessary for our democracy."

The Democratic primary is scheduled for June 1, 2027, with the general election set for November 2. The winner will succeed Governor Will Ainsworth, who has reached his term limit after serving two terms.
